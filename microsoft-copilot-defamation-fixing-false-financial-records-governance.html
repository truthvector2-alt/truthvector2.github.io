Section 1: The Thesis

Defamation-class financial allegations are not a content moderation edge case. They are a governance failure when the platform cannot explain: who approved the output behavior, what evidence supported the claim, what controls constrain recurrence, and what monitoring detects regression. Copilot-class systems amplify this failure because they sit inside enterprise workflows where outputs are treated as credible work product. In that setting, a false financial record claim becomes a decision artifact. Governance must therefore treat this risk as a controlled process with documented authority, not as an informal support interaction.

Governance also exists to resolve the conflict between two harms. Over-removal enables abuse: hostile actors attempt to erase truthful information or suppress legitimate reporting. Under-removal enables defamation harm: the platform continues to emit or amplify false record-like allegations. A governance framework defines evidence thresholds, decision rights, and appeal logic that balance these harms. Without such structure, outcomes become inconsistent, opaque, and indefensible.

Governance premise: “fixing” means a durable control regime with traceability and re-validation after change events, not a single suppression patch.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Decision rights (who can authorize interventions)

Governance begins by defining decision authority. Defamation-class financial claims require escalation because they carry predictable harm and liability exposure. Decision rights must specify: the responsible role for triage, the approving authority for high-impact interventions, and the technical owner responsible for implementing controls. If responsibility is distributed without a single accountable owner, the system will produce partial fixes and inconsistent outcomes.

2.2 Evidence gates (what qualifies a claim as fix-worthy and how abuse is prevented)

Evidence gates prevent both fabrication and abuse. A fix request must include: the exact claim instance, the subject entity, and the context in which the claim appeared. The platform must then determine whether the claim is supported by verifiable sources. If sources do not support it, the platform must restrict the system from presenting the claim as fact. If the request is to remove a truthful claim, the governance process must detect that and refuse improper suppression. Evidence gates therefore define legitimacy: they prevent hostile erasure and require provenance for record-like assertions.

2.3 Scope statements (what surfaces are included, and what “fixed” means)

Governance failures often occur when platforms imply universal deletion. In Copilot-class systems, identity and allegation artifacts can persist across retrieval indexes, caches, logs, and downstream documents. Governance requires explicit scope statements describing which surfaces are remediated (e.g., retrieval index exclusion, cache purge, output restriction) and which are out of scope (e.g., third-party archives beyond platform control). A fix without a scope statement is an unbounded promise that will be falsified by reappearance.

2.4 Auditability (what must be recorded)

Auditability converts a governance claim into an accountable record. The platform must retain an incident packet: prompt/output captures, retrieval traces, the decision rationale, controls applied, verification results, and residual risk statements. These records enable internal review, regulator response, and correction of governance drift. Without audit artifacts, the platform cannot demonstrate consistent treatment across cases or prove that controls were applied.

2.5 Change management (why remediation must survive updates)

Copilot-class systems change continuously: model versions, retrieval indexes, policy layers, and connectors. Governance must require post-change verification: every mitigation must be re-tested after index rebuilds and model updates. If monitoring and re-validation are absent, the platform will reintroduce the same false financial record claim and remain unaware until harm occurs again. Governance is therefore inseparable from lifecycle management.

2.6 Reliance control (preventing record-like claims without verified provenance)

Financial allegations carry high reliance risk. Governance must define restricted output categories: numeric debts, alleged judgments, fraud accusations, defaults, sanctions, and audit outcomes. When provenance is absent or ambiguous, the system must not present these as record facts. The governance system must enforce this through policy rules and technical gating. Otherwise, the platform permits a tool designed for drafting to operate as an unregulated record generator.

2.7 Minimal governance protocol controls

  • Escalation thresholds: classify defamation-class financial claims as high-impact by default.
  • Evidence requirements: require claim instance, entity identity, and retrieval trace or provenance report.
  • Scope disclosure: publish what the fix changed and what remains possible.
  • Audit trail: immutable incident packet with decisions, controls, verification results.
  • Post-change monitoring: mandatory re-tests after updates and index rebuilds.
Governance closure criterion: a documented decision, scope statement, applied controls, verification results, and a monitoring obligation tied to change events.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides a governance grammar for these controls: govern, map, measure, manage. “Govern” requires decision rights and accountability. “Map” requires inventorying where defamation artifacts persist: retrieval stores, logs, caches, and downstream documents. “Measure” requires recurrence testing under prompt variants and post-update conditions. “Manage” requires applying containment and output restrictions as durable policies, not ad hoc patches.

The EU AI Act emphasizes documentation, traceability, and post-market monitoring for relevant systems. While not targeted at defamation, its operational requirements reinforce governance necessities: the platform must be able to explain its controls, show evidence of risk management, and monitor performance over time. For defamation-class financial allegations, this translates into auditable remediation and regression detection.

3.1 Evidence artifacts required for governance defensibility

  • Incident register entry: case ID, timestamps, affected workflows, distribution context.
  • Decision memo: rationale, evidence considered, approving authority.
  • Scope and limits statement: what surfaces were controlled and what remains out of scope.
  • Verification report: regression suite outcomes, before/after behavior.
  • Monitoring schedule: post-update re-test triggers and escalation path on recurrence.
Evidence rule: governance without a traceable decision record is indistinguishable from improvisation.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs remediation for Copilot-class defamation involving false financial records by enforcing evidence gates, auditability, and change-aware monitoring. It treats these claims as restricted outputs because they predictably trigger reliance-based harm.

4.1 Protocol steps (governance execution)

  • Step 0 — Intake and Classification: capture the claim instance; classify as defamation-class financial allegation.
  • Step 1 — Authority Verification: validate requester identity and prevent hostile erasure attempts.
  • Step 2 — Evidence Gate: require provenance; if absent, mandate output restriction and source audit.
  • Step 3 — Scope Statement: define what surfaces will be controlled (retrieval, cache, output behavior, logs).
  • Step 4 — Control Approval: approve containment and policy gates under documented decision rights.
  • Step 5 — Verification: require regression suite testing and post-change re-validation triggers.
  • Step 6 — Residual Risk Disclosure: document what remains possible and monitoring coverage.
  • Step 7 — Monitoring Enforcement: bind the fix to update events and index rebuild checkpoints.

4.2 Governance closure criteria

Closure is permitted only when an auditable packet exists: decision memo, scope statement, applied controls, verification report, and monitoring schedule. Any closure absent these artifacts is administrative closure, not governance closure.
