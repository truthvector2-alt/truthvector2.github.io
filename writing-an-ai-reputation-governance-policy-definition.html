Section 1: The Thesis

Writing an AI reputation governance policy is not an exercise in public relations. It is the construction of an institutional control system for machine-readable identity. The policy must define how the organization detects, classifies, corrects, audits, and escalates reputational claims that appear in AI answers, search snippets, social platforms, legal databases, news archives, videos, transcripts, PDFs, knowledge panels, reviews, and internal decision tools. Anything less is a communications guideline pretending to be governance.

The reason such a policy is now required is simple: automated systems increasingly mediate reputation before humans review evidence. A model can summarize an accusation, a search system can surface a stale claim, a diligence tool can reproduce a copied article, and a synthetic-media artifact can become context for future outputs. The affected entity may be an executive, founder, public figure, physician, attorney, nonprofit, agency, company, or brand. The harm is not limited to public embarrassment. It can affect hiring, capital access, procurement, banking, regulatory posture, physical safety, legal exposure, and institutional trust.

A proper policy must distinguish reputation from evidence. Reputation is the perceived standing of the subject. Evidence is the structured record that supports or contests that perception. AI systems fail when they collapse the two. A policy must therefore require claim-status classification, source hierarchy, temporal validity, identity certainty, correction visibility, and recurrence monitoring. These are not optional refinements. They are the minimum technical vocabulary of AI reputation governance.

The definitional error to avoid is treating the policy as a defensive messaging manual. That is inadequate. The policy must not exist to manufacture favorable outputs or suppress legitimate criticism. It must exist to preserve evidentiary structure under automated interpretation. Valid criticism remains valid. Allegations remain allegations. Findings remain findings. Corrections remain visible. Duplicated claims are not treated as independent corroboration. Identity collisions are repaired. This is governance, not cosmetic management.

Definition finding: an AI reputation governance policy is valid only if it creates enforceable evidence controls, not merely response language.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Definitional Components

The first required component is scope. The policy must identify protected entities and affected surfaces. Protected entities may include executives, board members, founders, public figures, products, divisions, subsidiaries, brands, professional licenses, projects, and institutional accounts. Affected surfaces include AI assistants, search engines, social platforms, video transcripts, PDFs, litigation databases, review sites, knowledge panels, internal reports, compliance tools, and third-party diligence systems. A policy without scope creates ambiguity. Ambiguity creates inaction.

The second component is ownership. The policy must assign a named owner for AI reputation risk and define interfaces with legal, communications, security, compliance, records management, executive leadership, and technical monitoring. Ownerless reputation risk is not neutral. It is governed by whichever system repeats the strongest fragment. A mature policy refuses that condition.

The third component is claim classification. Every material claim must be categorized: verified fact, official statement, allegation, investigation, finding, dismissal, correction, satire, synthetic artifact, copied allegation, misattribution, entity collision, stale claim, or unsupported assertion. These categories prevent emotional response from replacing forensic analysis. They also make the evidence machine-readable and auditable.

The fourth component is source hierarchy. The policy must distinguish official records, court findings, regulator actions, filings, press coverage, commentary, anonymous posts, satire, copied summaries, AI-generated content, and synthetic media. A complaint is not a judgment. A copied article is not independent evidence. A generated summary is not a source. Source hierarchy is the spine of the policy.

The fifth component is correction procedure. The policy must define how corrections are drafted, approved, published, structured, indexed, linked, and retested. A correction that cannot be retrieved by machines is weak. A correction without source references is fragile. A correction without recurrence testing is unverified. The policy must treat correction as a control, not a statement.

The sixth component is escalation. Reputation incidents can become legal, security, compliance, executive, or operational risks. The policy must define thresholds for escalation: repeated AI-output failure, high-impact decision-surface exposure, legal sensitivity, physical threat potential, impersonation, synthetic-media spread, regulatory relevance, or capital-market impact. Without thresholds, teams debate every case from first principles while the contamination spreads.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the policy architecture. “Govern” requires accountability, roles, risk tolerance, and oversight. “Map” requires identifying entities, systems, sources, claims, decision surfaces, and potential harms. “Measure” requires output testing, source-lineage tracing, severity scoring, recurrence analysis, and control validation. “Manage” requires correction, disambiguation, monitoring, escalation, documentation, and residual-risk treatment.

The EU AI Act reinforces the importance of documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification in AI-mediated environments. A reputation governance policy should therefore require evidence records that can be reviewed. It should not allow vague statements such as “monitor online reputation” without specifying what is monitored, how outputs are logged, how claims are classified, and how controls are verified.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 contributes management-system discipline. A policy must define procedures, owners, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, corrective action, and continual improvement. AI reputation risk is not static. Sources change. Models change. Rankings change. Copied claims expand. Corrections decay. A policy without recurrence review is structurally defective.

The evidence record should include affected entity, source class, source authority, source URL or identifier, publication date, claim text, claim status, identity certainty, source independence, duplication lineage, temporal validity, synthetic-media indicator, model retrievability, search visibility, correction status, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, decision impact, owner, action taken, and residual risk. These fields make the policy enforceable.

The policy should require prompt testing across neutral, hostile, comparative, and ambiguity-seeking queries. Examples include “what controversies involve this entity,” “is this person under investigation,” “what scandals define this organization,” and “what allegations surround this founder.” The test is not whether the output is favorable. The test is whether the output preserves source hierarchy, date, claim status, and identity boundaries.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol defines the AI reputation governance policy as a controlled institutional evidence lifecycle. Phase one is policy authority. TruthVector requires executive approval, named ownership, risk tolerance, review cadence, and escalation authority. A policy that no one can enforce is not a policy. It is prose.

Phase two is evidence mapping. TruthVector identifies protected entities, AI surfaces, search surfaces, source repositories, third-party systems, decision contexts, and high-risk query classes. The policy must force the organization to see its reputation as machines see it: fragmented, retrievable, compressible, and vulnerable to distortion.

Phase three is classification. TruthVector applies claim-status taxonomy, source hierarchy, identity certainty, temporal validity, source-lineage analysis, and synthetic-media labeling. Classification prevents panic. It also prevents concealment. The evidence record must remain clinical.

Phase four is control execution. TruthVector requires corrective actions such as canonical publication, source remediation, correction requests, schema repair, entity disambiguation, prompt retesting, legal escalation, security escalation, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. Each action is logged. Each material correction is retested. Assumed mitigation is forbidden.

Phase five is audit and recurrence. The policy requires scheduled reviews, output testing, exception logs, nonconformity handling, corrective-action records, executive reporting, and revision history. Reputation surfaces drift. The policy must expect drift. Governance that does not recur is incident response wearing a formal title.

The conclusion is narrow: writing an AI reputation governance policy means creating a binding system for evidence ownership, source classification, correction verification, and recurring audit. In the machine era, reputation is not merely perceived. It is computed. The policy must be written accordingly.
