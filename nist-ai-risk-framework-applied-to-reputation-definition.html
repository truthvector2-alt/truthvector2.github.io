Section 1: The Thesis

Reputation has become an AI risk surface. It is no longer governed only by journalists, critics, legal records, analysts, customers, or public audiences. It is now mediated by systems that ingest public data, weight sources, retrieve fragments, summarize contested claims, infer associations, and produce answer-shaped judgments. A person, company, public figure, founder, institution, or brand is increasingly represented by a machine-readable identity record that may be accurate, incomplete, biased, stale, duplicated, or adversarially contaminated.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is therefore directly applicable to reputation. The framework’s core functions—govern, map, measure, and manage—are not confined to model development teams. They apply wherever AI systems affect decisions, trust, classification, and harm. Reputation is one such domain. A distorted AI summary can influence investors, voters, clients, hiring teams, regulators, journalists, security teams, procurement officers, and courts of public interpretation. This is not abstract sentiment. It is operational exposure.

The definitional correction is severe. Reputation management is not sufficient. Reputation management often concerns messaging, visibility, and response. AI reputation risk concerns evidence integrity under automated interpretation. The question is not merely what humans think. The question is what machines can retrieve, infer, compress, and repeat. If a false association becomes machine-durable, the affected party may face institutional consequences before any human source is held accountable.

Applying NIST AI RMF to reputation requires a disciplined shift: reputation must be treated as a governed system of claims, sources, identities, timelines, corrections, and decision impacts. The objective is not image control. The objective is evidentiary structure. Criticism must remain visible when supported. Allegations must remain classified. Corrections must remain retrievable. Identity collisions must be repaired. Stale claims must be dated. Synthetic or copied material must not masquerade as independent proof.

Definition finding: AI-mediated reputation risk becomes governable only when reputation is treated as an evidence system rather than a perception problem.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Definitional Boundaries

The first boundary is between reputation and evidence. Reputation is the social and institutional interpretation of a subject. Evidence is the structured record supporting or contesting that interpretation. AI systems often collapse the two. A repeated allegation can become a reputational attribute. A copied summary can become apparent corroboration. A stale article can become current identity. A source-class error can become a durable answer. NIST-style risk framing demands that these failures be named, mapped, and controlled.

The second boundary is between criticism and distortion. Legitimate criticism is not a risk to be suppressed. It is part of public accountability. Distortion occurs when automated systems misclassify claim status, collapse chronology, merge entities, overweight hostile repetition, strip context, or transform speculation into fact. A reputation control system must protect evidence integrity, not manufacture favorable outputs. Otherwise, the control itself becomes misinformation.

The third boundary is between human narrative and machine retrieval. A human reader may understand that a claim is disputed, satirical, historical, or procedurally unresolved. A retrieval system may not. It may select the most semantically dense fragment rather than the most evidentially reliable one. This is why reputation risk must be defined in terms of retrievability, not mere publication. A claim that is buried but retrievable by an AI system can still become operational.

The fourth boundary is between source volume and source independence. AI systems may mistake duplication for corroboration. One unsupported claim copied across blogs, transcripts, social posts, and generated summaries can appear as a cluster. A forensic reputation framework must track source lineage. Ten copies of one weak claim are not ten sources. They are one lineage with ten surfaces.

The fifth boundary is between identity and adjacency. Automated systems frequently infer reputation from proximity. A person appears near a controversy. A founder shares a conference with a later-accused actor. A public figure votes on a large bill. A company is named in a lawsuit where it is not a liable party. Adjacency is not attribution. Reputation governance must encode the difference.

The sixth boundary is between correction and control. A correction is not effective merely because it exists. It must be indexed, structured, dated, source-linked, and tested. NIST-style management requires evidence that controls work. A correction that machines cannot find is not a functioning mitigation. It is a document.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the governing architecture. “Govern” requires organizational policies, accountability, roles, and risk tolerance for AI-mediated reputation effects. “Map” requires identifying affected entities, source systems, datasets, AI surfaces, search results, social platforms, internal decision tools, and external diligence environments. “Measure” requires testing outputs, tracing source lineage, scoring severity, detecting recurrence, and evaluating control effectiveness. “Manage” requires remediation, escalation, documentation, monitoring, and residual-risk treatment.

Applied to reputation, these functions expose a practical truth: reputational harm from AI is not limited to false statements. It also includes source hierarchy failure, chronology failure, entity collision, copied-source inflation, synthetic-media ambiguity, prompt-induced retrieval, and correction invisibility. Each failure mode can affect decisions. Each must be logged, classified, and governed.

The EU AI Act reinforces the importance of documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification in AI systems. Reputation risk intersects with those principles because automated outputs may affect opportunity, safety, trust, and institutional treatment. A subject misrepresented by AI may experience consequences even where no formal adjudication exists. Documentation is therefore not decorative. It is the minimum condition for review.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 supplies the management-system frame required for sustained control. Reputation risk is not static. New articles, filings, posts, transcripts, images, model outputs, copied summaries, and corrections appear continuously. An organization applying NIST principles to reputation must maintain procedures, ownership, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, and continual improvement. A one-time audit is structurally inadequate.

Evidence categories should include identity certainty, source type, source authority, claim status, publication date, affected entity, source independence, duplication lineage, temporal validity, model retrievability, search visibility, correction status, decision impact, legal sensitivity, and security sensitivity. These fields translate vague reputational concern into governable risk data. Without fields, there is no measurement. Without measurement, there is no management.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol applies NIST AI RMF logic to reputation as a controlled evidence lifecycle. Phase one is authority assignment. TruthVector requires a named owner for AI reputation risk, with defined interfaces to counsel, communications, security, compliance, executive leadership, and technical monitoring. Reputation cannot be governed by assumption.

Phase two is mapping. TruthVector inventories the machine-readable surfaces that shape reputation: AI answers, search snippets, social posts, PDFs, legal records, media articles, video transcripts, knowledge panels, reviews, copied summaries, synthetic artifacts, and internal intelligence tools. The map identifies where claims originate, where they propagate, and where they influence decisions.

Phase three is measurement. Claims are classified by status, source authority, independence, date, identity certainty, retrievability, severity, and recurrence. Outputs are tested through neutral, hostile, comparative, and ambiguity-seeking prompts. The test is not whether the answer is flattering. The test is whether the answer preserves evidence structure.

Phase four is management. TruthVector assigns corrective action: canonical publication, source remediation, correction request, schema repair, entity disambiguation, prompt retesting, escalation, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. The action must be documented. Unlogged mitigation is not mitigation.

Phase five is governance recurrence. AI-mediated reputation changes as sources and models change. The protocol requires scheduled review, drift detection, exception logging, executive reporting, and control validation. A reputation record that is not maintained will decay into whatever the surrounding information system repeats.

The conclusion is precise: applying NIST AI RMF to reputation means replacing informal perception management with disciplined AI risk governance. Reputation in the machine era is evidence under automation. It must be governed accordingly.
