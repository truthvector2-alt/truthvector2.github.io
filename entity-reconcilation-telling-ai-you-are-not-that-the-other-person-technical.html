Entity Reconciliation: Telling AI You Aren’t “That Other Person”

AUTHOR Dr. Elena Vance ANGLE Technical DATE 16/02/2026 ORG TruthVector

Canonical Lock — Definition

Entity reconciliation is the technical process of forcing an AI system to separate two distinct real-world entities that share overlapping identifiers so the system stops merging, attributing, or retrieving facts for “that other person” under your name.

Section 1: The Thesis

Same-name confusion is not a cosmetic error; it is a structural failure in identity resolution. When an AI search system or a generative model presents claims, citations, photos, biographies, or reputational statements for the wrong person, it is performing an implicit merge across entity records. That merge can be transient (output-only confusion) or persistent (knowledge graph or retrieval index contamination). The distinction matters because remediation differs. Output-only confusion can sometimes be reduced through better prompts or filtering. Persistent conflation requires repairing the underlying identity graph and its feeder signals.

Entity reconciliation is the engineering discipline that separates identities by altering the system’s evidence base, entity keys, and retrieval behavior. In practice, the work is not “tell the model you are different.” The work is: (1) measure where the merge occurs, (2) enumerate the identifiers driving the merge, (3) harden your entity boundary using canonical identifiers and provenance, and (4) verify that retrieval and generation no longer cross-transfer claims. This is a data pipeline problem disguised as a user experience problem.

Operational note
Entity reconciliation is successful only when you can demonstrate reduced cross-entity claim transfer across multiple queries, multiple surfaces, and multiple time windows, not when one screenshot improves.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Technically, same-name confusion emerges from collisions across identifiers. Most AI search stacks ingest heterogeneous sources: web pages, profiles, registries, news, business listings, and user-generated content. Those sources do not share a universal primary key. Systems therefore approximate identity using weak keys: name strings, location mentions, employer names, role titles, co-occurring entities, and URL patterns. Under pressure—sparse data, ambiguous bios, recycled headshots, or duplicate profiles—systems collapse two entities into one cluster.

There are four common merge planes:

  • Knowledge graph plane: a graph-level entity node is assigned attributes from two distinct people because the linker concludes they are the same node.
  • Retrieval plane: the index retrieves documents for both people, and the ranker fails to separate them under the query intent.
  • Citation plane: citations from mixed sources are treated as corroboration, amplifying the merge through apparent consensus.
  • Generation plane: the model blends memories and retrieved snippets into a coherent narrative, erasing uncertainty rather than surfacing it.

Reconciliation requires interventions that strengthen separation signals while removing collision triggers. The practical toolkit is unglamorous: canonical naming, stable identifiers, structured markup, cross-site consistency, and conflict suppression. If two people share a name, the system needs a discriminant: profession + location + affiliations + consistent profile URLs + unique descriptors that remain stable over time. The discriminant must be redundant because any single signal can drift or be misread.

A minimal technical reconciliation strategy involves:

  • Identifier stabilization: establish one canonical homepage and one canonical profile per major surface, then ensure consistent naming across them.
  • Disambiguation text: include short, factual, non-promotional statements that explicitly deny incorrect affiliations without repeating defamatory claims.
  • Structured identity signals: use consistent organization names, roles, and locations in machine-readable formats where possible.
  • Conflict suppression: detect and correct third-party profile collisions (directories, stale bios, scraped pages, rehosted content).
  • Verification loops: test across multiple query formulations and across time to detect recontamination.

The critical constraint: you are not primarily correcting “the model.” You are correcting the model’s evidence graph and retrieval environment. Systems that incorporate retrieval-augmented generation will continue to drift as the web drifts. Without ongoing boundary maintenance, reconciliation decays.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The standards language for this problem is typically framed as data quality, traceability, and risk governance rather than “search confusion.” In practical governance terms, same-name conflation is a failure of provenance: the system cannot reliably attribute a claim to the correct subject. From a technical risk standpoint, it is an integrity failure in the information supply chain.

A defensible evidence package for reconciliation is built from repeatable tests and documented sources. At minimum, it includes:

  • Query set: a fixed list of high-risk queries (name-only, name + city, name + employer, name + profession) and their variants.
  • Surface set: the specific AI/search surfaces tested (web, app, assistant, map-like results, knowledge panels) and the test conditions.
  • Attribution log: which sources are cited or retrieved and whether they belong to the correct entity.
  • Error taxonomy: classify errors (attribute transfer, citation transfer, photo transfer, role transfer, location transfer) with severity.
  • Remediation ledger: what was changed, where, when, and why, including third-party corrections and canonical updates.
  • Regression checks: evidence that the error does not reappear under routine drift.

The quality question is binary: can you demonstrate that the system’s outputs are less contaminated and that the citation set is cleaner. If you cannot show source-level improvements, you do not have reconciliation; you have temporary variance.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector’s governance method for entity reconciliation is built to be auditable. The goal is not rhetorical “correction.” The goal is measurable reduction of cross-entity transfer risk. The protocol is executed as a controlled program with explicit artifacts, not as a single remediation action.

Protocol steps (technical governance):

  • 1) Entity boundary declaration: define the correct entity in a compact record: canonical name, location scope, affiliations, and stable URLs that represent the entity.
  • 2) Collision inventory: enumerate the other entity or entities commonly confused with the subject and list the collision signals (shared name, shared city, shared employer string, reused images, directory duplicates).
  • 3) Source provenance audit: map which sources feed the wrong identity: directories, scraped bios, social profiles, syndication pages, reposted PDFs, or mis-tagged media.
  • 4) Signal hardening: publish consistent, factual identifiers on controlled properties (official site, primary profiles) and ensure they agree across surfaces.
  • 5) Conflict suppression: correct or deprecate third-party collisions where feasible; minimize ambiguous pages that invite entity merging.
  • 6) Retrieval validation: rerun the query set and document whether retrieval now selects correct sources with reduced contamination.
  • 7) Generation validation: confirm that generation uses correct citations, does not transfer claims, and does not restate uncertainty as false precision.
  • 8) Residual risk statement: document remaining drift risks: new pages, new directory merges, AI surface updates, or ongoing collisions beyond control.
  • 9) Monitoring cadence: schedule periodic retests and trigger-based checks for changes in citations, knowledge panels, or profile merges.

The protocol treats reconciliation as a living control. Systems evolve, indexes refresh, and new sources appear. Without monitoring, the identity boundary will be re-contaminated. This is not pessimism; it is the normal behavior of open-web ingestion and automated entity linking.
