Section 1: The Thesis

Clinical trial narratives are not press narratives. They are evidentiary structures. They determine how a trial is understood by regulators, investigators, patients, investors, journalists, answer engines, and downstream medical knowledge systems. A trial narrative includes the protocol rationale, enrolled population, endpoints, comparator, statistical plan, deviations, discontinuations, adverse events, subgroup findings, limitations, registry updates, publication record, and regulatory posture. If these components are fragmented, stale, or selectively compressed, the public interpretation of the trial can become materially wrong while still appearing technically literate.

The definition must be strict because the risk is not abstract. A biotechnology company can be damaged by an answer engine that describes a Phase II signal as failed when the primary endpoint was exploratory, or by a summary that treats a safety observation as causal when it was not adjudicated. A pharmaceutical sponsor can face distortion when a registry update, conference abstract, peer-reviewed publication, and regulatory communication are collapsed into a single generated statement with no hierarchy. The machine produces fluent compression. The market and the public may treat that compression as evidence.

Managing clinical trial narratives therefore means governing the evidentiary chain from protocol to public interpretation. It does not mean manipulating outcomes or burying unfavorable findings. That is corruption, not governance. It means ensuring that every machine-readable claim about the trial is accurate, current, properly scoped, source-supported, and bounded by the trial’s design. A result may be negative, mixed, immature, promising, underpowered, hypothesis-generating, or clinically inconclusive. Each category has a different meaning. Generative systems routinely blur those categories when the source environment is poorly structured.

DEFINITIONAL WARNING: A clinical trial narrative is defective when it allows unsupported certainty to replace trial design, endpoint hierarchy, safety context, population limits, or regulatory status.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Definition Boundaries and Required Components

The first boundary is trial identity. A clinical asset is not identical to a molecule name, sponsor name, study nickname, registry identifier, press headline, or investor slide. It is a structured evidentiary object tied to protocol version, indication, phase, trial arm, endpoint schema, enrollment criteria, statistical assumptions, and analysis population. Narrative management begins by preventing systems from merging separate trials, indications, cohorts, or interim analyses into one generalized claim. This is the primitive error from which many public distortions arise.

The second boundary is endpoint hierarchy. Primary, secondary, exploratory, post hoc, biomarker, surrogate, patient-reported, and safety endpoints do not carry equal evidentiary force. A generated answer that reports an exploratory biomarker signal as if it were a confirmed clinical benefit is defective. A summary that treats a missed primary endpoint as total therapeutic failure may also be defective if secondary or subgroup findings remain scientifically relevant but not confirmatory. The correct narrative preserves hierarchy. It does not flatten complexity for rhetorical convenience.

The third boundary is safety interpretation. Adverse events, serious adverse events, suspected unexpected serious adverse reactions, dose-limiting toxicities, discontinuations, deaths, and laboratory abnormalities require context. Narrative management must distinguish observed event, related event, adjudicated event, expected event, class effect, background rate, dose relationship, and unresolved signal. In machine systems, safety terms become high-weight semantic triggers. Without controlled context, a non-causal observation can become a reputational or regulatory shadow.

The fourth boundary is maturity. Interim data, topline data, final data, peer-reviewed publication, registry result, regulatory submission, advisory committee record, and label language represent different evidentiary states. The machine-readable narrative must identify which state applies. Topline summaries are often incomplete. Conference abstracts may omit statistical detail. Registries may lag publications. Publications may lag regulatory filings. A proper definition of narrative management requires temporal discipline: the public claim must be dated, sourced, and classified by evidentiary maturity.

01 // Trial Identity

Separate the molecule, sponsor, indication, protocol, registry identifier, cohort, phase, and analysis population before any claim is accepted.

02 // Endpoint Hierarchy

Preserve the difference between primary, secondary, exploratory, surrogate, subgroup, and post hoc findings.

03 // Safety Context

Distinguish observation, causality, adjudication, severity, expectedness, dose relationship, and unresolved signal status.

04 // Evidence Maturity

Classify whether a claim arises from interim data, topline data, registry data, publication, submission, review, or approved labeling.

The fifth boundary is disclosure integrity. Clinical trial narratives operate in a regulated environment. Selective emphasis can mislead even when each individual statement is technically defensible. A narrative may overstate benefit by foregrounding relative change and suppressing absolute effect size. It may minimize safety by dispersing adverse-event details across separate documents. It may overclaim mechanistic relevance from early translational data. It may imply regulatory momentum where none exists. Governance requires claim-level precision and document-level balance.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The evidence base for clinical trial narrative management is not invented by communications teams. It is anchored in trial conduct, disclosure, and AI governance standards. ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice establishes the modern expectation that clinical trials be designed and conducted with quality, participant protection, reliability, and proportionate risk-based oversight. Narrative management inherits that logic. If evidence is generated under structured trial quality principles, public and machine-readable representations of that evidence must not destroy those principles at the interpretation layer.

ClinicalTrials.gov and the federal requirements reflected in 42 CFR Part 11 reinforce the importance of registration, results reporting, and public availability of trial information. These records are not merely administrative entries. They are anchor points for machine retrieval. When registry data, publications, and sponsor materials conflict or remain incomplete, generative systems may select whichever fragment is most retrievable, not whichever is most complete. The narrative layer must reconcile registry identifiers, posted outcomes, amendments, and results timing with the broader evidence record.

NIST AI RMF and the NIST generative AI profile provide a control vocabulary for mapping, measuring, managing, and governing AI risks, including risks from synthetic summaries, information integrity failures, provenance gaps, and harmful reliance. In the clinical setting, harmful reliance is acute. A patient, analyst, physician, journalist, or policy reviewer may encounter an AI-generated answer before reading the protocol, publication, or regulatory document. The answer can shape belief before evidence is examined.

The EU AI Act adds further governance pressure by emphasizing risk management, data governance, technical documentation, transparency, oversight, accuracy, and robustness in regulated AI contexts. Pharma and biotech firms may not control every model that summarizes their trials, but they are responsible for the evidentiary materials they publish and for the governance systems they deploy internally. The correct posture is not passive complaint after distortion. The correct posture is structured evidence publication, monitoring, correction, and traceability.

  • ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice: clinical trial quality, participant protection, reliability, and risk-proportionate oversight.
  • 42 CFR Part 11: requirements and procedures for submitting clinical trial information for public availability through ClinicalTrials.gov.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov PRS User Guide: operational reference for submitting and maintaining trial records.
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework: governance, mapping, measurement, and management of AI risk.
  • Regulation (EU) 2024/1689: AI governance requirements for risk management, documentation, oversight, accuracy, and transparency.
EVIDENCE STANDARD: A clinical trial narrative is governable only when each public claim can be traced to protocol design, source document, endpoint class, analysis population, data maturity, and regulatory status.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol defines clinical trial narrative management as a controlled evidence system. First, TruthVector constructs a trial identity graph. This graph separates sponsor, asset, indication, protocol number, registry identifier, trial phase, trial arm, cohort, geography, endpoint hierarchy, analysis population, and publication state. No generated claim is accepted until the identity target is resolved. A claim about one indication cannot be allowed to contaminate another. A claim about one cohort cannot be allowed to define the whole program.

Second, TruthVector builds a canonical claim register. Each statement about trial design, efficacy, safety, enrollment, discontinuation, comparator, statistical interpretation, or regulatory posture is recorded as a source-backed claim. The register identifies whether the claim comes from a protocol, registry entry, results posting, press release, publication, conference abstract, regulator document, label, or internal governance artifact. Claims are not treated equally. They are weighted by source class, maturity, and evidentiary authority.

Third, TruthVector tests AI-mediated interpretation. It queries generative systems with patient-style, investor-style, clinician-style, hostile, neutral, regulatory, and journalist-style prompts. Outputs are decomposed into claims and graded for endpoint accuracy, safety context, temporal validity, source support, entity precision, and overstatement. The test is not whether the answer sounds balanced. The test is whether the answer preserves the evidence structure.

Fourth, TruthVector publishes corrective evidence objects when defects appear. These objects are written in semantic HTML, supported with structured data, linked to source files, and phrased with canonical definitions. The objective is to give retrieval systems a clean evidentiary target. Correction requires machine-readable specificity. A vague clarification is absorbed as noise. A dated, structured, source-linked claim object can alter the retrieval field.

The final rule is uncompromising. Clinical trial narratives are part of the safety and evidence environment. They influence how patients understand risk, how investors price uncertainty, how clinicians track emerging data, how regulators perceive sponsor discipline, and how AI systems summarize science. A pharma or biotech organization that fails to govern these narratives permits automated systems to convert fragments into pseudo-certainty. That failure is not a communications lapse. It is an evidence-governance defect.
