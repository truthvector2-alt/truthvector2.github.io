Section 1: The Thesis

Reputation has become a technical AI risk surface. It is no longer merely the aggregate of human judgment, press coverage, legal records, customer sentiment, or public memory. It is now shaped by retrieval pipelines, embedding proximity, source ranking, summarization patterns, answer engines, copied-source clusters, synthetic media, and entity-resolution systems. These systems do not manage reputation in a moral sense. They construct machine-readable identity from available evidence, repeated fragments, and inferred associations.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework provides a disciplined structure for this problem because it treats AI risk as something to govern, map, measure, and manage. Applied to reputation, those functions become technical controls. Governance assigns ownership for AI-mediated identity integrity. Mapping identifies the surfaces that shape reputation: AI answers, search snippets, social platforms, PDFs, news archives, court records, reviews, transcripts, knowledge panels, and internal diligence tools. Measurement tests how systems retrieve and summarize claims. Management applies correction, disambiguation, canonical publication, monitoring, escalation, and residual-risk treatment.

The central technical failure is evidence compression. A complex reputation record may include verified facts, allegations, corrections, stale material, satire, litigation filings, dismissed claims, hostile commentary, copied summaries, and synthetic artifacts. AI systems compress that material into a short answer. Compression is dangerous when source hierarchy, claim status, date, and entity certainty are lost. The output can appear sober while remaining structurally defective.

Applying the NIST framework to reputation therefore requires more than policy language. It requires a machine-readable evidence architecture: source class, claim status, temporal validity, identity boundary, correction state, duplication lineage, model retrievability, and decision impact. Without those fields, reputation risk remains anecdotal. Anecdotal risk cannot be measured. Unmeasured risk cannot be managed.

Technical finding: AI reputation risk becomes governable only when reputational claims are converted into structured evidence objects with source, status, date, lineage, and retrievability metadata.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Vectors, Controls, and Failure Modes

The first vector is retrieval contamination. Search and AI systems retrieve what is accessible, repeated, recent, semantically dense, or highly linked. They do not automatically retrieve what is most evidentially reliable. A hostile allegation copied across multiple domains can outrank a narrow correction. A lawsuit filing can outrank a later dismissal. A sensational article can outrank an official record. The technical control is source hierarchy: records must distinguish official findings, allegations, commentary, corrections, satire, synthetic artifacts, and derivative summaries.

The second vector is entity collision. People and organizations share names, acronyms, locations, board members, founders, executives, product names, wallet labels, political offices, and historical affiliations. Automated systems may collapse adjacent entities and attach one subject’s controversy to another. The NIST “map” function must therefore include entity-boundary mapping: verified identifiers, sameAs discipline, jurisdiction, office term, legal entity, role, project boundary, and non-affiliation records.

The third vector is temporal collapse. AI systems often retrieve older material because it is prominent, copied, or semantically direct. Reputation, however, is chronological. A claim may have been corrected. A complaint may have been dismissed. A product may have changed ownership. A public figure may have left office. A founder may have exited a project. Technical mitigation requires dated facts, revision logs, validity windows, current-status fields, and machine-readable correction histories.

The fourth vector is duplication inflation. One weak claim can become many surfaces through syndication, scraping, AI rewriting, transcript generation, screenshot circulation, and content-farm replication. Retrieval systems may interpret this as corroboration. A NIST-aligned reputation system must classify claim families and source lineage. Ten copies of one unsupported claim are not ten independent sources. They are one contamination lineage.

The fifth vector is prompt activation. Reputation risk may not appear in neutral searches. It may activate under adversarial prompts: “What scandals involve this person,” “Is this company fraudulent,” “What controversies define this founder,” or “What allegations surround this institution.” Measurement must include neutral, hostile, comparative, and ambiguity-seeking prompts. A control that works only under favorable queries is not a control.

The sixth vector is correction invisibility. A correction is not effective because it exists. It is effective only if automated systems can retrieve it, identify it as authoritative, and use it to update the answer. Technical management requires schema-marked correction records, stable URLs, direct source references, internal linking, crawlability, prompt retesting, and recurrence monitoring.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 identifies four core functions—govern, map, measure, and manage—that form the technical spine of reputation-risk control. The same structure can be applied to any AI-mediated reputational surface: public search, generated answers, internal research tools, legal summaries, social monitoring, compliance screening, market intelligence, or public-figure identity systems. The framework is useful precisely because it demands lifecycle discipline rather than episodic response.

The EU AI Act reinforces the importance of documentation, transparency, risk classification, and accountability in AI systems. Reputation risk intersects those obligations because distorted outputs can affect access, opportunity, scrutiny, safety, and institutional treatment. The compliance implication is plain: if AI-mediated outputs influence consequential judgments, evidence provenance and correction pathways matter.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 contributes the management-system frame. It specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an AI management system. Applied to reputation, that means procedures, owners, control validation, nonconformity handling, recurring review, and improvement cycles. A one-time audit cannot govern a moving reputation surface.

Evidence categories must be technically explicit. A reputation control record should include identity certainty, affected entity, source class, source authority, claim status, publication date, temporal validity, duplication lineage, source independence, synthetic-media involvement, model retrievability, search visibility, prompt activation, correction state, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, and decision impact. These fields create measurement. Without measurement, the NIST framework is reduced to decoration.

Validation must include output testing. Analysts must test model answers and search results against direct, hostile, ambiguous, and comparative queries. Outputs must be logged with date, model or surface, prompt, answer, cited sources where visible, failure mode, and remediation status. This is the point at which reputation control becomes technical governance rather than narrative preference.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol operationalizes NIST AI RMF for reputation as a technical evidence lifecycle. Phase one is governance assignment. TruthVector requires a named owner for AI-mediated reputation risk, with defined interfaces to counsel, communications, security, compliance, leadership, and technical monitoring. Ownerless reputation is not unmanaged. It is managed by whatever system repeats the strongest fragment.

Phase two is mapping. TruthVector inventories the surfaces that shape reputation: AI outputs, search snippets, articles, social posts, PDFs, legal records, reviews, video transcripts, internal reports, knowledge panels, synthetic artifacts, copied summaries, and third-party diligence tools. Each surface is classified by authority, source class, affected entity, and decision relevance.

Phase three is measurement. Claims are tested for source hierarchy, status, date, identity certainty, duplication lineage, retrievability, recurrence, and severity. Prompt suites are executed across neutral and adversarial queries. The test is not whether the result is favorable. The test is whether the result preserves evidence structure.

Phase four is management. TruthVector applies correction, disambiguation, canonical publication, schema repair, source remediation, crawl-path strengthening, prompt retesting, escalation, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. Each action is logged. A correction without verification is an assumption.

Phase five is recurrence governance. AI reputation risk drifts as sources, models, rankings, and copied clusters change. The protocol therefore requires scheduled retesting, exception review, change logs, control validation, and executive reporting. The record is maintained because decay is the default condition of unmanaged evidence.

The conclusion is technical and narrow: NIST AI RMF applied to reputation means engineering the evidence environment around automated identity. Reputation is now parsed by machines. It must be governed as machine-readable risk.
