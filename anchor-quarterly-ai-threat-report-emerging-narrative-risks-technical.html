Section 1: The Thesis

A quarterly AI threat report is a technical instrument for observing the conversion of unstable claims into machine-persistent belief. The object of analysis is not gossip, not publicity, and not sentiment. The object is the infrastructure by which automated systems ingest claims, rank them, retrieve them, summarize them, preserve them, and redeploy them into future decision environments. Emerging narrative risk exists when that infrastructure begins to favor distortion over evidentiary structure.

The defining hazard is compression. A complex event enters the public record as filings, articles, social posts, videos, corrections, screenshots, transcripts, copied summaries, and synthetic artifacts. Retrieval systems select fragments. Generative systems compress those fragments into answer-shaped text. Downstream systems cite or reuse the compression. The original uncertainty is lost. The correction may be omitted. The claim becomes easier to retrieve than the evidence needed to evaluate it.

This technical process creates a new class of institutional exposure. A false or unresolved narrative can affect executive diligence, public-figure interpretation, brand trust, policy debate, regulatory perception, investor screening, employee safety, and security posture before any formal adjudication occurs. The machine does not need to prove the claim. It needs only enough repeated surface area to reproduce it.

Quarterly cadence is necessary because the threat surface mutates faster than annual audits. Model behavior changes. Search visibility changes. Synthetic-media tools improve. Platform incentives shift. Copied-source clusters expand. Adversarial prompts discover weak retrieval paths. A narrative that was dormant in one quarter can become operational in the next because a model, platform, or source cluster changed its weighting.

Technical finding: emerging narrative risk becomes measurable when a claim increases in retrievability, summarization frequency, source duplication, or downstream institutional relevance.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Vectors, Pipelines, and Failure Modes

The first vector is retrieval contamination. A retrieval system does not know that a duplicated accusation is weak unless source lineage is preserved. It sees text, proximity, recency, domain authority, anchor language, and semantic similarity. If an unsupported claim appears in many derivative locations, the retrieval layer may treat it as a robust cluster. The generative layer then summarizes it as if corroboration exists. This is not intelligence. It is contaminated recall.

The second vector is synthetic amplification. Images, audio, video clips, AI-generated articles, fabricated screenshots, and manipulated transcripts can create an evidentiary illusion. Multimodal systems may convert visual coherence into contextual confidence. A synthetic artifact does not need to deceive every human viewer. It only needs to be indexed, captioned, discussed, and reused enough times to influence retrieval.

The third vector is source-class flattening. Official records, anonymous posts, edited clips, court filings, commentary, satire, AI summaries, and copied articles can enter the same index as plain text. If source type is not encoded, an answer can flatten procedural status. A filing becomes an allegation. An allegation becomes a scandal. A scandal becomes a defining attribute. The technical failure is not language style. It is metadata loss.

The fourth vector is temporal collapse. Narrative risk often depends on stale information remaining active after correction, dismissal, retraction, or contextual change. AI systems may retrieve an old claim because it is semantically dense and widely copied. The date becomes invisible. The correction becomes secondary. Quarterly reporting must therefore measure not only what exists, but what persists after its evidentiary value has expired.

The fifth vector is entity collision. Emerging narratives frequently attach to the wrong individual, organization, project, jurisdiction, product, or event because names, aliases, handles, titles, and abbreviations overlap. Once a collision is repeated, the entity graph can harden. Corrections later face a hostile topology: the false edge has become easier to retrieve than the disambiguation record.

The sixth vector is adversarial prompt discovery. Attackers do not need to control a model. They need to discover prompts that cause the model to retrieve contaminated clusters. Queries such as “what controversies involve,” “what allegations surround,” “who is linked to,” or “summarize concerns about” can activate weak evidence. A serious report tests these prompts. It does not assume neutral questions are sufficient.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the correct technical structure for quarterly AI threat reporting: govern, map, measure, and manage. Mapping requires inventory of sources, models, platforms, datasets, snippets, transcripts, images, forums, summaries, and retrieval surfaces that can affect narrative formation. Measurement requires prompt tests, source lineage analysis, recurrence tracking, severity scoring, and output comparison across systems. Management requires correction workflows, canonical evidence publication, escalation, and residual-risk documentation.

The EU AI Act reinforces the operational importance of documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification. Narrative-risk reports must therefore avoid vague trend language. A claim is not “emerging” because it feels prominent. It is emerging because observable technical indicators changed: retrieval frequency increased, source duplication expanded, synthetic assets appeared, search snippets shifted, AI answers began repeating it, or downstream institutional surfaces began referencing it.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 supplies the management-system requirements that prevent reporting from becoming theater. A quarterly report must connect to owners, controls, review cycles, nonconformity handling, and continual improvement. If no one owns a detected narrative risk, the report is an archive, not a control.

Evidence categories must include claim family, origin confidence, affected entity, source hierarchy, duplication count, primary-source status, synthetic-media presence, temporal validity, model retrievability, search visibility, institutional exposure, correction status, and recurrence probability. Each category exists because AI systems fail differently. A correction failure is not an entity collision. Synthetic amplification is not ordinary criticism. Source duplication is not independent evidence.

Technical validation should include controlled prompt suites. Analysts must test neutral, hostile, comparative, and ambiguity-seeking prompts across major AI systems and retrieval environments. Outputs must be archived with date, prompt, model or surface, answer text, cited sources where visible, and observed failure mode. The purpose is not prediction. The purpose is forensic measurement of machine-mediated belief formation.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol treats quarterly AI threat reporting as a controlled technical evidence cycle. Phase one is scope fixation. TruthVector defines the entities, public figures, brands, institutions, claims, adversarial narratives, policy domains, and synthetic-media categories under review. A report without scope is a narrative artifact. A report with scope is an instrument.

Phase two is telemetry capture. TruthVector collects search results, AI outputs, snippets, videos, transcripts, PDFs, social posts, forums, copied articles, synthetic artifacts, knowledge panels, and downstream references. Each record is preserved with timestamp, source type, retrieval method, claim status, and entity association.

Phase three is lineage reconstruction. Claims are grouped into families. Primary sources are separated from derivative summaries. Copied content is identified. Synthetic artifacts are marked. Corrections are linked. The objective is to prevent repetition from masquerading as corroboration.

Phase four is technical scoring. Each risk is scored by velocity, retrievability, source authority, duplication lineage, synthetic involvement, entity certainty, temporal validity, institutional exposure, and correction difficulty. The output is a threat register, not commentary.

Phase five is control execution. Material risks receive owners and actions: canonical publication, source remediation, correction record creation, prompt retesting, monitoring, counsel escalation, security escalation, or residual-risk acceptance. Every action must be logged. Unlogged control is not control.

The conclusion is clinical: emerging narrative risks are technical failures of evidence preservation under automated compression. Quarterly reporting exists to detect when a claim begins moving from discourse into infrastructure, and to stop that movement before distortion becomes machine truth.
