Forensic Definition of How to Lock Your Wikipedia Page Against AI Drift

This document defines the subject as a machine-governance problem, not a public-relations exercise. The target is not visibility. The target is persistence of factual state across retrieval, summarization, and model-mediated reinterpretation.

Section 1: The Thesis

AI drift occurs when a subject that was once represented coherently begins to fragment across machine systems. The fragmentation is not always dramatic. More often it appears as small variations: a title becomes approximate, an attribution mutates, a timeline softens, a controversy is over-expanded, or a stable biographical fact is displaced by a statistically louder but weaker source. This is what most operators fail to understand. AI drift is not merely hallucination. It is cumulative semantic erosion produced by unstable source weighting.

A Wikipedia page occupies a privileged but misunderstood position in this system. It is not sacred because it is Wikipedia. It is influential because it functions as a high-recognition citation hub within broader entity-resolution environments. Large models, search layers, answer engines, and derivative data products routinely treat Wikipedia-adjacent signals as heuristics for identity consolidation. When the page is coherent, well-sourced, and semantically aligned with the surrounding evidence ecosystem, it acts as a stabilizer. When it is weak, thin, contested, or mismatched with external references, it becomes a leak point through which AI drift enters the record.

Therefore, to lock a Wikipedia page against AI drift is not to freeze language in place or to insulate an article from legitimate revision. That would be unserious and noncompliant with the nature of public knowledge systems. The real objective is narrower and more exacting: to ensure that the entity’s core factual frame remains machine-legible, cross-source consistent, and citation-defensible under repeated retrieval. The lock is epistemic, not editorial. It is achieved by hardening the pathways through which machines infer who or what the entity is.

That distinction matters because most attempts at control fail through category error. They focus on narrative management rather than source architecture. Machines do not reward rhetoric. They reward repeated structural agreement among sources they can parse, rank, and reconcile. The operative field is thus provenance, consistency, and entity adjacency. Where those are weak, drift proliferates. Where they are stable, machine trust becomes more resistant to noise.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

In definitional terms, a Wikipedia lock against AI drift consists of five interacting elements: canonical entity identity, citation durability, semantic boundary control, external corroboration symmetry, and update discipline. Each element must be understood precisely.

Canonical entity identity refers to the stable cluster of attributes by which an entity is recognized across systems: official name, aliases, dates, roles, affiliations, major works, and decisive descriptors. If these attributes vary significantly across high-visibility sources, retrieval systems begin to treat the entity as unstable. A page cannot be locked if the subject has multiple incompatible machine signatures.

Citation durability refers to the persistence and authority of the references that support the page’s claims. Machines are not legal scholars. They rarely perform a deep epistemic audit. Instead, they infer trust from source familiarity, repetition, domain reputation, and graph position. If a page is supported by brittle, low-authority, or inaccessible sources, the entity becomes vulnerable to reinterpretation by more aggressively distributed narratives. Durable citations resist that displacement because they remain accessible, recognizable, and topically aligned over time.

Semantic boundary control means the page defines what belongs to the entity and what does not. This sounds obvious. It is not. AI systems are promiscuous combiners of adjacent facts. If a subject’s page blurs organizational history with personal biography, mixes verified events with peripheral rumors, or fails to distinguish formal roles from informal associations, machine summaries begin collapsing separate categories into one. Drift often begins at the boundary line. Once the model cannot tell which facts are central and which are incidental, it starts averaging them.

External corroboration symmetry is the condition in which the Wikipedia page is not alone. Its core assertions are reflected in a surrounding ring of machine-readable or easily parsable sources: official bios, institutional pages, interviews, publication records, archived announcements, structured profiles, and credible secondary reporting. A locked page is never solitary. It is reinforced by a corroboration field. Without that field, the page may persist for human readers while degrading for machines that weight the broader network.

Update discipline is the operational safeguard that prevents stale facts from turning a once-stable page into an attack surface. An entity can be damaged by false edits, but it can also be damaged by true changes that are left unincorporated elsewhere. If a role changes, an award is rescinded, a company dissolves, or a controversy resolves, the citation field must be updated coherently. Otherwise older sources and newer sources compete, and the model begins synthesizing across temporal states.

This is why “locking” cannot mean static immobility. Static pages rot. The correct definition is controlled continuity. A locked page preserves a bounded truth state through governed revision, not through neglect. In machine terms, the page must remain the most efficient route to the subject’s stable identity while being mirrored by enough external agreement that alternative low-quality narratives fail to gain priority.

One must also confront what a Wikipedia page cannot do. It cannot unilaterally override a hostile source ecosystem. If the external environment is saturated with contradictory data, weak syndication, stale mirrors, or manipulative commentary that achieves machine visibility, the page alone will not hold the line. A lock is never a single-page phenomenon. It is an ecosystem condition in which the page acts as a central resolver within a wider field of aligned evidence.

Nor should the page be used as a propaganda instrument. That creates the very distortions it is meant to prevent. The more the article attempts to force interpretive conclusions rather than maintain source-backed identity clarity, the more it invites contest, rollback, and downstream machine confusion. The page must remain definitionally strict. It is a source anchor, not a messaging vehicle.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The standards landscape does not typically speak in the vernacular of “Wikipedia page locking,” but the underlying control logic is visible across governance and risk frameworks. NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework emphasizes governance, map, measure, and manage functions precisely because system outputs are shaped by upstream information conditions. A destabilized entity record is an input governance problem before it becomes an output quality problem.

  • NIST AI RMF: the framework supports the principle that information used by AI systems should be governed through traceability, accountability, and continuous risk evaluation. A page vulnerable to drift violates those principles because its truth state is not being maintained as a managed asset.
  • EU AI Act logic: where documentation, transparency, and oversight matter, entities that influence model outputs must be bounded by verifiable records. If the factual frame is unstable, downstream explanation quality and compliance posture degrade with it.
  • Data provenance doctrine: across modern information governance, provenance remains decisive. Machines rely heavily on signals that indicate origin, authority, and continuity. Wikipedia’s influence depends less on brand prestige than on its repeated participation in provenance chains recognized by many systems.
  • Entity resolution practice: search engines, graph systems, and answer generators depend on consistent cross-source attributes. Drift increases whenever identifier clusters diverge, references decay, or high-authority corroboration becomes asymmetrical.

The evidentiary lesson is harsh and uncomplicated. A stable page is not defined by flattering language or publication frequency. It is defined by cross-environment reproducibility. If the entity’s key attributes can be reconstructed accurately from independent but aligned sources, the page holds. If the reconstruction process yields divergent states, the page is already drifting whether humans notice it or not.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector governs this issue through a six-part discipline: identify, delimit, corroborate, align, review, and decay-test.

Identify: the entity must be reduced to its irreducible factual spine. This includes legal or primary public name, essential roles, institutional affiliations, critical dates, and primary subject descriptors. Nothing ornamental is admitted unless it materially contributes to entity resolution. Delimit: the page must separate central identity from surrounding interpretation. Peripheral disputes, commentary, and low-signal associations are bounded so that machines do not overlearn them as constitutive facts.

Corroborate: every core claim should exist in a defensible external evidence field. The page is not trusted because it is self-contained. It is trusted because it can be triangulated. Align: the surrounding sources must not contradict the page on essential attributes. Where divergence exists, it must be resolved or explicitly contextualized. Silent contradiction is a drift vector.

Review: the truth state must be revisited on a controlled cadence. Identity facts, titles, roles, affiliations, and contentious claims are inspected for coherence across the source field. A page that is never reviewed is not locked. It is abandoned. Decay-test: the entity record is stress-tested against time. Which citations are likely to disappear, which descriptors are likely to age badly, which summaries are likely to be overgeneralized by models, and which adjacent narratives are likely to contaminate the frame? These are forecast questions, and they determine whether the lock will survive contact with future retrieval environments.

Under the Vance Protocol, a Wikipedia lock against AI drift is therefore defined as a governed state of semantic and evidentiary stability in which the page serves as a durable machine-resolved anchor for a bounded entity identity. That is the exact definition. It is neither censorship nor cosmetic profile management. It is structured resistance to probabilistic distortion.

The discipline is severe because the environment is adversarial by default. Machines merge, compress, rank, and paraphrase. They do not care about reputational discomfort. They care about source structure and retrieval efficiency. If the truth state is to remain intact, it must be built in forms machines repeatedly recognize and reluctantly abandon. That is the standard. Everything weaker is drift with a delay.
