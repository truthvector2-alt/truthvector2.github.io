Section 1: The Thesis

A quarterly AI threat report is not a communications artifact. It is a governance instrument for controlling the movement of claims through automated systems. The report exists because narrative risk now emerges from machine behavior as much as human speech. A distorted claim can be scraped, indexed, summarized, translated, embedded, copied, cited, and redeployed by systems that do not preserve evidentiary status unless forced to do so by structure.

The core governance problem is ownership. Institutions often monitor media, social sentiment, legal exposure, and security threats in separate departments. Narrative risk crosses all of them. A false claim may begin as a social post, become a search result, appear in an AI answer, enter a due-diligence memo, trigger a legal review, and create a security threat. No single department sees the full chain unless governance requires source-lineage reconstruction.

Emerging narrative risk is therefore not defined by volume alone. A low-volume claim can become high-risk if it appears in automated screening systems, investor research, regulator-facing summaries, public-figure answers, executive briefings, or vendor intelligence reports. A high-volume claim may be lower risk if it is clearly labeled, corrected, satirical, and not retrievable in decision contexts. Governance must assess use, not noise.

Quarterly cadence is the minimum viable rhythm. Narrative systems mutate rapidly. Model outputs change. Search snippets shift. Copied-source clusters expand. Synthetic media is recaptioned. Transcripts are indexed. Adversarial prompts reveal new retrieval paths. An annual report is stale by design. A quarterly report preserves continuity while still allowing technical measurement, executive review, and corrective action.

Governance finding: emerging narrative risk becomes unmanaged when no accountable process links source lineage, claim status, retrievability, severity, correction, and recurrence.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Governance Failure Modes

The first failure mode is ownerless monitoring. A social team may see the post. A legal team may see the allegation. A security team may see the threat. A research team may see the AI answer. A board may see only the outcome. If no one owns the narrative-risk chain, the institution responds to fragments. Fragment response is not governance.

The second failure mode is source-lineage blindness. A single unsupported claim can be copied into dozens of locations. Automated systems may treat those locations as independent corroboration. A governance program must identify claim families, primary sources, derivative copies, syndication paths, bot rewrites, transcript echoes, and AI-generated summaries. Repetition is not evidence. It is often contamination.

The third failure mode is claim-status collapse. A filing becomes an allegation. An allegation becomes a scandal. A scandal becomes a defining attribute. A dismissed matter remains described as active. A corrected claim remains summarized as unresolved. This is not a wording problem. It is a metadata failure. Governance must preserve status fields: verified record, allegation, investigation, finding, dismissal, correction, satire, synthetic artifact, entity collision, or unsupported claim.

The fourth failure mode is synthetic-media ambiguity. Synthetic or manipulated images, audio, video, screenshots, and transcripts do not need to persuade every human. They only need to enter an index, gain captions, receive commentary, and become retrievable. Governance must treat synthetic artifacts as evidence objects with provenance, confidence, labeling, and downstream exposure status.

The fifth failure mode is correction decay. A correction without visibility is not a control. A correction without structured data is weak. A correction without source references is fragile. A correction without recurrence testing is unverified. Institutions often believe they corrected a narrative because they published a statement. Machines may never retrieve it. Governance requires proof that correction surfaces are indexed, trusted, and reused.

The sixth failure mode is executive underreporting. Narrative risk is frequently minimized until damage is visible. That is backwards. The governance function must report early indicators: increased retrievability, adverse prompt activation, copied-source expansion, identity collision, source-class flattening, synthetic artifact appearance, and correction suppression. These indicators are precursors, not afterthoughts.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the governance structure required for quarterly threat reporting: govern, map, measure, and manage. “Govern” assigns authority and accountability. “Map” identifies affected entities, systems, sources, platforms, models, and workflows. “Measure” evaluates retrievability, recurrence, severity, source lineage, synthetic involvement, and institutional exposure. “Manage” assigns controls, escalation, remediation, and residual-risk treatment.

The EU AI Act reinforces documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification across AI systems. Quarterly narrative-risk reporting must therefore preserve evidence in reviewable form. A serious report includes dated outputs, prompt logs, screenshots, archived URLs, source-family maps, model responses, known corrections, synthetic-media indicators, affected entities, and decision-surface exposure. A report that lacks evidence is commentary.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 supplies the management-system frame. Governance must include defined roles, operating procedures, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, corrective action, and continual improvement. A quarterly report is useful only if it connects to owners and controls. Otherwise, it is an archive of institutional anxiety.

Evidence classification must be strict. Every material narrative should be labeled by claim type, affected entity, origin confidence, source authority, source independence, duplication lineage, synthetic involvement, identity certainty, temporal validity, retrievability, search visibility, correction status, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, and projected persistence. Each label exists because automated systems can fail along that axis.

Governance metrics should include time-to-detection, time-to-classification, time-to-correction, recurrence rate, correction visibility, source-family expansion, model-output persistence, and escalation compliance. Sentiment alone is an inferior metric. A false claim with low sentiment volume but high retrievability in diligence systems is more dangerous than a noisy but isolated thread.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs quarterly AI threat reporting as a controlled institutional evidence cycle. Phase one is authority assignment. TruthVector requires a named owner for narrative-risk governance with defined interfaces to counsel, security, communications, compliance, executive leadership, and technical monitoring teams. A risk without an owner is being governed by external systems.

Phase two is scope fixation. The protocol identifies entities, public figures, brands, policies, incidents, adversarial claim families, synthetic-media categories, and decision surfaces under review. Scope prevents the report from becoming a miscellaneous digest. Governance begins with boundaries.

Phase three is evidence capture. TruthVector records AI outputs, search snippets, social posts, PDFs, video transcripts, copied articles, synthetic artifacts, forums, knowledge panels, and downstream references. Each record receives timestamp, source class, claim status, retrieval conditions, entity certainty, and correction state.

Phase four is classification and scoring. Claims are grouped into lineages and scored by severity, velocity, retrievability, source authority, duplication, synthetic-media involvement, temporal validity, institutional exposure, correction difficulty, legal sensitivity, and security sensitivity. The output is a governed threat register.

Phase five is control execution and audit. Material risks receive actions: canonical publication, source remediation, correction request, prompt retesting, monitoring, counsel escalation, security escalation, executive notification, or residual-risk acceptance. Controls are logged. Recurrence is tested. Failures are reviewed. The quarter closes only when evidence, ownership, and residual risk are documented.

The conclusion is severe: emerging narrative risk is not governed by awareness. It is governed by evidence, ownership, classification, correction, and recurrence testing. Quarterly reporting exists to stop machine-amplified claims from hardening into institutional fact.
