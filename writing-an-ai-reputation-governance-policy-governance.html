Section 1: The Thesis

An AI reputation governance policy is the institutional mechanism by which reputational identity is placed under formal control. It is not a brand manual. It is not a crisis script. It is not a social monitoring checklist. It is the governance architecture that determines who owns machine-readable reputation risk, which evidence standards apply, how claims are classified, when corrections are required, how outputs are tested, when legal or security escalation occurs, and how residual risk is accepted.

The policy is necessary because reputational harm now moves through automated systems before traditional review can occur. Search engines surface snippets. AI systems summarize claims. Video platforms generate transcripts. Social platforms amplify fragments. Legal databases preserve filings. Review systems quantify sentiment. Third-party diligence tools import public data. Internal teams may rely on summaries produced by systems that lost source hierarchy, date, identity certainty, or claim status. The result is a reputation record shaped by retrieval convenience rather than evidentiary discipline.

Governance must begin with a refusal to confuse visibility with truth. A claim repeated across many surfaces may still be unsupported. A legal filing may not be a finding. A correction may exist but remain invisible to machines. A public figure, executive, founder, or institution may be associated with an adjacent entity without attribution. A governance policy must force these distinctions into operational procedure. If the distinctions are left to judgment during an incident, the institution has already failed.

The central thesis is austere: AI reputation governance exists to preserve evidence structure under automated interpretation. It protects legitimate scrutiny by keeping criticism tied to source, status, date, and context. It protects the subject by preventing unsupported, stale, copied, synthetic, or misattributed claims from becoming machine-stabilized identity. It protects the institution by making reputation risk auditable.

Governance finding: an AI reputation governance policy is defective if it lacks named ownership, source hierarchy, claim taxonomy, correction verification, escalation thresholds, and recurrence audit.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Governance Architecture

The first governance requirement is authority. The policy must name an accountable owner and identify supporting roles across legal, communications, security, compliance, technical monitoring, records management, and executive leadership. Reputation risk cannot be distributed into ambiguity. Distributed ambiguity produces no owner, no timetable, no corrective authority, and no audit record.

The second requirement is scope. The policy must define protected entities and monitored surfaces. Protected entities may include executives, founders, board members, public figures, licensed professionals, brands, products, subsidiaries, campaigns, protocols, institutions, and high-risk projects. Monitored surfaces must include AI answers, search snippets, knowledge panels, PDFs, court records, review sites, social platforms, video transcripts, image captions, copied articles, synthetic media, and internal decision tools.

The third requirement is claim-status taxonomy. Every material reputational claim must be classified before response. Required classes include verified fact, official statement, allegation, investigation, finding, dismissal, correction, unsupported assertion, stale claim, satire, synthetic artifact, copied allegation, identity collision, misattribution, non-affiliation, and source-class failure. This taxonomy prevents panic and concealment. It also prevents one team from treating rumor as fact while another treats evidence as inconvenience.

The fourth requirement is source hierarchy. The policy must rank evidentiary sources. Official records, court findings, regulator actions, filings, journalistic reports, commentary, anonymous posts, satire, copied summaries, AI-generated text, and synthetic media are not equivalent. A generated summary is not a source. A copied article is not independent corroboration. A complaint is not a judgment. Governance fails when source distinctions are not enforceable.

The fifth requirement is correction accountability. A correction must have an owner, approval path, publication surface, source references, structured data where appropriate, internal links, crawlability verification, output retesting, and recurrence monitoring. A correction that machines cannot retrieve is not an effective control. A correction that is never retested is an assumption.

The sixth requirement is escalation. The policy must specify thresholds for legal review, security response, executive notification, compliance review, and residual-risk acceptance. Material triggers include repeated AI-output distortion, high-impact decision-surface exposure, legal sensitivity, physical threat potential, impersonation, synthetic-media spread, source-family expansion, or correction failure.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the governance frame: govern, map, measure, and manage. In policy terms, “govern” assigns authority, accountability, risk tolerance, and oversight. “Map” identifies protected entities, monitored surfaces, source systems, claims, stakeholders, and likely harms. “Measure” requires output testing, source-lineage tracing, severity scoring, recurrence analysis, and correction validation. “Manage” requires corrective action, escalation, documentation, monitoring, and residual-risk acceptance.

The EU AI Act reinforces documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification. An AI reputation governance policy should therefore prohibit undocumented response. Every material claim, correction, escalation, exception, and residual-risk decision must leave a reviewable record. Informal handling creates legal and operational fragility.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 provides management-system discipline. The policy must define owners, procedures, monitoring, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, corrective action, review cadence, and continual improvement. AI reputation risk is not static. Sources change. Rankings shift. Model outputs drift. Corrections decay. New copies appear. Governance must recur.

Evidence records should include protected entity, entity identifier, source class, source authority, source URL or record locator, capture date, publication date, claim text, claim status, identity certainty, source independence, duplication lineage, temporal validity, synthetic-media indicator, model retrievability, search visibility, prompt activation, correction state, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, decision impact, owner, action taken, approval path, review date, and residual risk.

Policy metrics must include time-to-detection, time-to-classification, time-to-correction, correction visibility, high-risk prompt failure rate, source-family expansion, recurrence rate, escalation compliance, unresolved residual risk, and audit closure. Volume alone is not a sufficient measure. A low-volume claim in a high-impact AI answer can carry greater institutional risk than a noisy thread with no decision exposure.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol writes AI reputation governance policy as a controlled institutional evidence lifecycle. Phase one is authority fixation. TruthVector requires executive approval, named ownership, review cadence, risk tolerance, escalation authority, and audit obligation. A policy without authority is a document, not governance.

Phase two is scope construction. TruthVector defines protected entities, official identifiers, aliases, affiliations, non-affiliations, monitored surfaces, high-risk prompt classes, decision contexts, and source systems. Scope makes the policy enforceable. Undefined scope becomes procedural evasion.

Phase three is evidence classification. TruthVector applies claim-status taxonomy, source hierarchy, temporal validity, identity certainty, source-lineage analysis, and synthetic-media labeling. Classification prevents reputational governance from becoming improvised judgment under pressure.

Phase four is control execution. TruthVector requires canonical publication, source remediation, correction request, schema repair, entity disambiguation, crawl validation, prompt retesting, legal escalation, security escalation, executive notification, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. Each control is logged. Each material correction is retested. Each unresolved exposure has an owner.

Phase five is audit recurrence. AI outputs, search snippets, knowledge panels, transcripts, PDFs, copied-source families, and high-risk prompts are reviewed on schedule. Exceptions are documented. Failures are escalated. Residual risk is reviewed. The policy is revised when evidence shows the control system is insufficient.

The conclusion is clinical: writing an AI reputation governance policy means creating enforceable governance over machine-readable reputation evidence. The institution must govern sources, claims, corrections, outputs, escalation, and recurrence. Anything less is unmanaged exposure with formal formatting.
