Section 1: The Thesis

Clinical trial narratives now operate inside a hostile evidentiary environment. A sponsor may comply with trial registration duties, maintain protocol discipline, report results, publish analyses, and preserve regulatory correspondence. Yet the public narrative can still be distorted when generative systems retrieve fragments from registries, press releases, abstracts, investor materials, social commentary, news summaries, and stale pages. The system then compresses those fragments into an answer that may be confident, concise, and clinically wrong.

The governance thesis is severe: clinical trial narratives require board-visible, role-assigned, source-controlled governance because the consequences of distortion are not cosmetic. A defective narrative can mislead patients, alter investor expectations, influence clinician perception, invite regulatory suspicion, distort safety interpretation, and damage scientific trust. Governance must therefore define who owns the narrative evidence layer, who approves machine-readable claims, who tests external answer systems, who adjudicates contradictions, and who closes remediation records.

Governance is not the same as message approval. Message approval asks whether language is acceptable for publication. Narrative governance asks whether every material claim is source-backed, endpoint-aware, temporally valid, population-bounded, safety-contextualized, regulatorily current, and retrievable by machines without losing meaning. A PDF buried in an investor archive is not governance. A press release reviewed by counsel is not enough. A governance system must maintain the structure of clinical evidence after it leaves the controlled document environment.

GOVERNANCE FINDING: A pharma or biotech organization has not governed its trial narrative if it cannot identify the authoritative source, current validity, endpoint hierarchy, safety context, and accountable owner for each material public claim.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Governance Controls and Accountability Architecture

The first governance control is ownership assignment. Clinical trial narratives sit across medical affairs, clinical development, biostatistics, regulatory affairs, legal, pharmacovigilance, investor relations, communications, and executive leadership. That diffusion is dangerous. When everyone touches the narrative, no one governs it. The sponsor must identify a named accountable function for clinical narrative integrity and define approval gates for trial claims before they enter web pages, registry updates, investor materials, media statements, knowledge bases, or AI-facing evidence objects.

The second control is the canonical claim register. Each material statement about trial design, enrollment, endpoints, efficacy, safety, discontinuation, statistical interpretation, limitation, regulatory status, or publication state must be reduced to a governed claim object. The object must include source, owner, approval date, evidence maturity, endpoint class, population scope, data cut, safety semantics, regulatory status, and expiration condition. If a claim cannot survive this registration process, it should not be allowed to circulate as public evidence.

The third control is source hierarchy. A governance system must distinguish protocol, statistical analysis plan, registry entry, results posting, clinical study report, peer-reviewed publication, regulator communication, label language, conference abstract, press release, investor deck, and secondary article. These sources do not carry equal authority. Generative systems often collapse them. Governance must restore hierarchy by publishing machine-readable signals that identify controlling sources and superseded sources. Otherwise, stale or derivative fragments can become answer-level authority.

The fourth control is contradiction adjudication. Trial narratives change. Interim data mature. Registry records are amended. Publications clarify. Safety events are adjudicated. Regulatory status changes. If two public sources conflict, governance must not leave the contradiction for an answer engine to resolve probabilistically. The organization must maintain contradiction records that identify which claim supersedes which prior claim, why, on what date, and under whose approval. Ambiguity is a governance defect when it affects patient safety, market interpretation, or regulatory meaning.

The fifth control is AI-mediated monitoring. Sponsors must test how generative systems describe their trials across patient, physician, investor, journalist, regulator, hostile, neutral, and uncertainty-seeking prompts. The outputs should be decomposed into claims and scored for endpoint fidelity, safety context, temporal validity, source support, entity precision, and regulatory status accuracy. This is not reputation surveillance. It is control testing. If the system cannot measure distortion, it cannot govern correction.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The governance foundation begins with clinical trial quality doctrine. ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice establishes expectations for participant protection, reliable results, trial quality, risk-proportionate oversight, and documented process. Those principles must extend into the interpretation layer. A sponsor that preserves quality during trial execution but allows public or AI-mediated evidence to distort endpoints, safety context, or limitations has created a downstream governance fracture.

ClinicalTrials.gov reporting requirements and 42 CFR Part 11 create public infrastructure for trial registration and results information. These records are now machine-retrieval anchors. Governance must therefore reconcile registry entries, amendments, posted results, publications, sponsor statements, and regulator documents. An unreconciled discrepancy is not a minor recordkeeping problem when an answer engine can elevate it into the public narrative.

NIST AI RMF supplies the operational structure: govern, map, measure, and manage. In clinical trial narrative governance, “govern” means assigning accountability and policy authority; “map” means identifying where evidence enters retrieval systems; “measure” means testing outputs against source truth; “manage” means correcting defects and retaining proof of remediation. The NIST Generative AI Profile further emphasizes provenance, information integrity, generated-content risk, and harmful reliance. Clinical trial narrative distortion is a direct harmful-reliance pathway.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act provides a governance vocabulary for risk management, data governance, transparency, documentation, human oversight, accuracy, and robustness in AI contexts. Even when a public model is not operated by the sponsor, the sponsor controls much of the evidence surface. Internally, if AI systems are used for medical review, investor preparation, competitive intelligence, regulatory analysis, or pharmacovigilance triage, governance obligations intensify. Internal reliance on uncontrolled summaries can create operational and legal exposure.

  • ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice: participant protection, reliable trial results, quality design, and risk-proportionate oversight.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov Reporting Requirements: public trial registration and results-reporting obligations.
  • 42 CFR Part 11: federal requirements for clinical trial registration and results information submission.
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework: govern, map, measure, and manage functions for AI risk.
  • NIST Generative AI Profile: risk controls for provenance, information integrity, generated content, and harmful reliance.
  • Regulation (EU) 2024/1689: AI governance requirements for risk management, documentation, oversight, accuracy, and transparency.
EVIDENCE STANDARD: Clinical trial narrative governance is adequate only when each material claim has an owner, source, authority ranking, endpoint classification, safety context, data maturity state, approval trail, and correction pathway.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs clinical trial narratives through a controlled evidence operating model. First, TruthVector establishes the trial governance graph. This graph resolves sponsor, asset, indication, protocol, registry identifier, study arm, cohort, endpoint, safety category, data cut, publication artifact, regulator document, and narrative owner. No claim enters the system without an entity target. No claim remains active without a validity condition.

Second, TruthVector constructs the canonical claim register. Claims are entered with source authority, approval status, endpoint class, population scope, safety semantics, regulatory status, maturity stage, and expiration rules. The register is not a library. It is a control surface. It determines which statements may be used in public pages, investor materials, evidence objects, AI summaries, internal briefings, and corrective disclosures.

Third, TruthVector applies governance testing. Generative systems are queried across patient, clinician, investor, journalist, regulator, hostile, neutral, and uncertainty-seeking conditions. Answers are decomposed into claims and scored against the register. Defects are classified as endpoint inflation, safety misclassification, temporal drift, source mismatch, indication confusion, regulatory-status error, or unsupported inference. Each defect receives an accountable owner and a closure condition.

Fourth, TruthVector publishes corrective evidence objects. These objects use semantic HTML, structured data, canonical definitions, stable identifiers, source links, embedded files, and explicit evidence hierarchy. Their function is not persuasion. Their function is governance-grade correction. They give retrieval systems a structured alternative to polluted fragments and provide human reviewers with an audit trail.
