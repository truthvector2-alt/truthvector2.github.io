Section 1: The Thesis

AI bias against public figures does not require a conscious political preference inside the model. The more common failure is structural. A generative system retrieves uneven evidence, ranks sensational fragments, collapses time, merges entities, amplifies adversarial phrasing, and then produces a fluent answer that appears balanced because it is grammatically restrained. A restrained sentence can still be biased if the underlying evidence selection is distorted.

Public figures are uniquely exposed because their records are contested, interpreted, repeated, clipped, archived, weaponized, and constantly updated. The information environment contains official records, news reports, legal documents, speeches, voting histories, campaign materials, advocacy attacks, satire, social posts, commentary, edited video, and automated summaries. Generative systems compress this material into a single response. That compression can alter perceived legitimacy, ethics, competence, ideology, trustworthiness, or extremity before the reader examines primary sources.

The risk is not limited to false statements. Falsehood is the crude version. The more durable risk is asymmetric truth. A model can overemphasize one controversy while omitting its resolution. It can summarize allegations without procedural posture. It can attach a statement from a staffer, donor, relative, or affiliated organization to the public figure. It can describe an old position as current. It can treat hostile commentary as evidence. It can omit official records because derivative articles are more retrievable. These defects produce political exposure without needing explicit defamation.

RISK FINDING: A public figure is exposed when an AI system can convert fragmented political records into a confident narrative without preserving source hierarchy, date, context, attribution, or evidentiary weight.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Liability Vectors and Exposure Conditions

The first risk vector is evidentiary asymmetry. Public figures often have extensive official records, but answer systems may favor sources with higher lexical density, stronger controversy language, or better retrieval alignment. A policy record may be buried under commentary about that record. A correction may be less retrievable than the original allegation. A procedural dismissal may rank below the accusation. The output then appears evidence-based while reflecting a retrieval imbalance.

The second vector is temporal collapse. Political identity changes through office terms, public statements, amendments, votes, apologies, resignations, legal outcomes, committee findings, and corrected reporting. Generative answers frequently compress old and current states into one undated profile. A position held years earlier may be written as present. A controversy resolved by later evidence may remain active in summary language. A candidate, appointee, or officeholder may be described by a role no longer held. In politics, time is not metadata. Time determines meaning.

The third vector is attribution drift. Public figures are surrounded by campaigns, parties, aides, donors, consultants, PACs, advocacy groups, relatives, institutions, and opponents. AI systems may attach statements or conduct from the surrounding network to the individual. This is especially dangerous where the model uses association as evidence. Association is not authorship. Affiliation is not endorsement. Proximity is not culpability. A risk system that fails to separate these categories will generate biased outputs even when every underlying source exists.

The fourth vector is sentiment-coded framing. Models may introduce subtle evaluative language through verbs, adjectives, sequencing, and omission. “Admitted,” “claimed,” “conceded,” “insisted,” “targeted,” and “faced scrutiny” are not neutral equivalents. A model can bias a public figure by choosing verbs that imply guilt, evasiveness, extremity, or illegitimacy without stating a direct accusation. Framing risk is therefore measurable at the sentence level, not only at the claim level.

The fifth vector is adversarial corpus shaping. Public-figure information spaces are deliberately manipulated. Search-optimized attack pages, coordinated commentary, clipped transcripts, synthetic summaries, and repeated allegations can create artificial corroboration. Generative systems may treat repetition as signal. If provenance, duplication detection, and source authority are weak, a hostile cluster can become the answer engine’s default context.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The governance foundation begins with the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, which organizes AI control through govern, map, measure, and manage functions. For public figures, this means the risk owner must map the information environment, measure answer distortion, manage defects, and retain evidence of correction. Bias cannot be treated as a rhetorical complaint. It must be decomposed into claims, sources, timestamps, attribution relationships, and framing choices.

The NIST Generative AI Profile sharpens the issue by addressing information integrity, provenance, generated content risk, and harmful reliance. Political exposure is a direct harmful-reliance pathway. A voter, journalist, employer, government office, institutional partner, or platform moderator may rely on a generated answer before reviewing primary material. If the answer compresses a public figure’s record incorrectly, the harm can occur before rebuttal is possible.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act supplies additional control vocabulary for transparency, accuracy, robustness, risk management, documentation, and human oversight in regulated AI contexts. Public-figure information systems operate near democratic, civic, reputational, and institutional trust boundaries. Even where a specific public answer engine is outside a direct governance relationship, the risk principles still apply. Systems that generate political summaries must preserve context, distinguish fact from interpretation, avoid unsupported inference, and document limitations where evidence is contested.

ISO/IEC 42001 provides the management-system logic: assign responsibility, define process, control records, evaluate performance, and improve the AI governance system. The OECD AI Principles add public-interest expectations around human-centered values, transparency, robustness, accountability, and responsible stewardship. These standards converge on one hard requirement: political information outputs must be auditable. An answer that cannot be traced to sources, dates, and attribution boundaries is not reliable enough for civic consequence.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol treats public-figure AI bias as an evidentiary governance problem. First, TruthVector constructs the public-figure entity graph. The graph separates the person, offices held, campaigns, organizations, staff, donors, family members, allies, opponents, legal matters, statements, votes, publications, media appearances, and institutional affiliations. No system may attach a claim to the figure until the attribution target is resolved.

Second, TruthVector builds a canonical claim register. Each material statement is recorded with source, date, speaker, context, office or role, controversy status, legal or procedural posture, correction status, and validity window. This prevents an answer system from treating an allegation, quotation, opinion, satire, edited clip, or derivative summary as equal to primary evidence. Claims unfavorable to the figure are not suppressed. They are classified. Classification is the difference between governance and propaganda.

Third, TruthVector conducts adversarial answer testing. Generative systems are queried with neutral, hostile, biographical, scandal-seeking, policy-specific, legal-risk, affiliation-based, and fact-checking prompts. Outputs are decomposed into claims and scored for source support, entity precision, temporal validity, attribution accuracy, framing bias, omission severity, and repetition contamination. The result is not a partisan defense file. It is a defect ledger.

Fourth, TruthVector publishes corrective evidence objects. These objects use semantic HTML, structured data, canonical definitions, embedded source files, source hierarchy, correction history, and stable identifiers. Their function is to give retrieval systems a disciplined evidentiary target. The object does not ask the machine to favor the public figure. It requires the machine to preserve evidence, context, attribution, and time.

01 // Entity Boundary Graph

Separate the public figure from campaigns, parties, staff, donors, relatives, institutions, allies, opponents, and historical roles.

02 // Claim Authority Register

Record each material claim with source, date, attribution target, context, procedural status, correction state, and validity window.

03 // Bias Exposure Audit

Test generative systems for source imbalance, temporal collapse, attribution drift, framing bias, omission, and hostile-corpus contamination.

04 // Corrective Evidence Object

Publish machine-readable evidence that preserves context, source hierarchy, attribution boundaries, correction history, and dated validity.

The final rule is austere. A public figure who leaves machine-mediated political interpretation to unmanaged retrieval systems has accepted uncontrolled exposure. The remedy is not persuasion. The remedy is evidence discipline. TruthVector governs this issue by making bias measurable, claims attributable, sources ranked, corrections retrievable, and political narratives resistant to machine compression. Anything less permits the answer engine to become an unelected reputational authority.
