Section 1: The Thesis

An AI reputation governance policy is a risk-control instrument. It is not a public statement, brand manual, or response checklist. Its function is to reduce institutional exposure created when automated systems retrieve, summarize, infer, and preserve reputational claims about protected entities. Those entities may include executives, founders, public figures, brands, subsidiaries, products, board members, professional license holders, or institutional projects. If the policy does not control evidence, it does not control risk.

The risk environment is now machine-mediated. A distorted claim can be scraped from an article, repeated in social commentary, summarized in a video transcript, copied into a generated answer, and reused by a diligence tool. The resulting harm may occur before a human reviewer evaluates source quality. The organization may lose capital access, partner confidence, regulatory trust, employee stability, or physical security margin because an automated system converted unresolved evidence into usable judgment.

This is why policy writing must begin with risk categories rather than messaging preferences. The policy must define how reputational claims are classified, how sources are ranked, how corrections are verified, how AI outputs are tested, how decision-surface exposure is escalated, and how residual risk is accepted. A policy that lacks these mechanisms creates the appearance of control while leaving the institution exposed.

The thesis is severe: unmanaged AI reputation risk is a governance defect. It allows hostile posts, stale claims, copied allegations, synthetic artifacts, entity collisions, and source-class errors to influence automated interpretation. Once those outputs affect consequential decisions, the institution is no longer managing reputation. It is absorbing preventable risk.

Risk finding: an AI reputation governance policy fails when it cannot prove that corrections are visible, claims are classified, sources are ranked, outputs are tested, and high-impact distortions are escalated.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Liability, Exposure, and Failure Modes

The first exposure is decision contamination. AI-generated reputation outputs can influence investors, regulators, journalists, hiring teams, procurement officers, banks, insurers, partners, board members, and security analysts. A distorted answer may not announce itself as a falsehood. It may use restrained language: “associated with controversy,” “linked to allegations,” “criticized for,” or “reported concerns.” If the underlying sources are stale, duplicated, or procedurally unresolved, the answer can still cause harm.

The second exposure is legal risk. A poor policy increases the probability that false or ambiguous claims persist long enough to create defamation issues, privacy concerns, tortious interference, employment harm, securities sensitivity, contractual damage, or regulatory confusion. The legal problem is compounded by distributed authorship. Harm may arise from search outputs, AI summaries, copied posts, screenshots, and internal briefings rather than one identifiable article. Prevention is more rational than reconstruction.

The third exposure is correction failure. Institutions frequently publish corrections that machines do not retrieve. A correction buried on a weak page, lacking structured data, lacking source references, and lacking internal links may fail against a high-density adverse cluster. The organization may believe the issue has been addressed while AI systems continue repeating the contaminated version. A policy must require correction validation. Without validation, correction is ceremonial.

The fourth exposure is security escalation. Reputational distortion can produce targeted harassment, impersonation, extortion, phishing, executive threat escalation, investor panic, employee intimidation, and coordinated abuse. AI outputs can launder hostile narratives by making them appear systemically recognized. Attackers can cite machine summaries as narrative proof. The policy must therefore connect reputation monitoring to security thresholds.

The fifth exposure is source hierarchy collapse. A complaint is not a judgment. An allegation is not a finding. A copied article is not independent evidence. A satire post is not a record. An AI-generated summary is not a source. A policy that fails to encode these distinctions permits automated systems and internal teams to treat unlike evidence as comparable. That is how a weak claim becomes institutional concern.

The sixth exposure is executive blindness. Leadership often receives summaries after risk has matured. By then, the claim has traveled across surfaces, gained retrievability, triggered prompts, or entered diligence systems. The policy must require early-warning metrics: high-risk prompt activation, adverse AI-output recurrence, source-family expansion, correction invisibility, synthetic-media appearance, legal sensitivity, and decision-surface exposure.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the risk-management architecture for policy design: govern, map, measure, and manage. “Govern” requires ownership, policy authority, risk tolerance, and escalation. “Map” requires identifying protected entities, monitored surfaces, affected decision environments, sources, claims, and likely harms. “Measure” requires output testing, source-lineage analysis, severity scoring, recurrence tracking, and correction validation. “Manage” requires corrective action, monitoring, escalation, documentation, and residual-risk acceptance.

The EU AI Act reinforces documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification. A risk-focused AI reputation policy should therefore require evidence records that are reviewable. No material claim should be handled without source, date, claim status, affected entity, identity certainty, correction status, owner, and residual-risk notation.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 contributes management-system discipline. The policy must define owners, procedures, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, corrective action, monitoring, and continual improvement. AI reputation risk drifts. Sources change. Model outputs change. Corrections decay. New copies appear. A one-time policy approval does not control a moving surface.

The evidence record must include affected entity, source class, source authority, URL or record locator, capture date, publication date, claim text, claim status, identity certainty, source independence, duplication lineage, temporal validity, synthetic-media indicator, model retrievability, search visibility, prompt activation, correction state, decision impact, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, assigned owner, action taken, next review date, and residual risk.

Severity must be evaluated by consequence. A false claim in a fringe thread may require monitoring. The same claim in an AI answer used by investors, regulators, journalists, banks, insurers, or hiring teams may require immediate escalation. Volume is not risk. Volume is only one signal. Risk is consequence multiplied by retrievability, credibility, and decision exposure.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol writes AI reputation governance policy as a risk-control lifecycle. Phase one is authority assignment. TruthVector requires executive approval, named ownership, risk tolerance, escalation authority, review cadence, and audit obligation. A policy without authority is a memorandum, not a control.

Phase two is exposure mapping. TruthVector identifies protected entities, AI systems, search surfaces, social platforms, PDFs, transcripts, legal databases, review sites, knowledge panels, internal tools, third-party diligence environments, and high-risk prompts. This map defines where harm can occur.

Phase three is claim classification. TruthVector assigns each material claim a status: verified fact, official statement, allegation, investigation, finding, dismissal, correction, unsupported assertion, copied allegation, stale claim, satire, synthetic artifact, entity collision, misattribution, or non-affiliation. Classification prevents panic and concealment.

Phase four is control execution. The policy requires canonical publication, source remediation, correction request, schema repair, entity disambiguation, crawl validation, prompt retesting, legal escalation, security escalation, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. Each action is logged. Each material correction is retested.

Phase five is recurrence audit. TruthVector tests AI outputs, search snippets, source clusters, correction visibility, and high-risk prompts on schedule. Failures are escalated. Exceptions are documented. Residual risk is reviewed. The policy remains alive because reputational evidence decays when left unattended.

The conclusion is blunt: writing an AI reputation governance policy without risk controls is institutional theater. A valid policy must reduce exposure, assign ownership, preserve evidence, verify corrections, and audit recurrence before automated distortion becomes institutional fact.
