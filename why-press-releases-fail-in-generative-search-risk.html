Section 1: The Thesis

In generative search, content is not evaluated only by rhetorical clarity. It is evaluated by its capacity to function as a stable record. The press release is structurally misaligned with this requirement. It is a distribution format optimized for speed and repetition, not for verifiability and bounded claims. Governance failures begin when organizations treat distribution artifacts as canonical sources.

The core governance problem is predictable: generative systems compress many sources into one answer while retaining a narrow citation budget. If the issuer’s press release is not selected for citation, the organization loses control over attribution and scope. The machine answer may be accurate, partially accurate, or inaccurate; in all cases, the organization’s ability to demonstrate provenance degrades unless an evidence-grade record exists elsewhere.

Governance Premise

If a claim cannot be traced to an auditable record object with stable identifiers, the claim should be treated as operationally unsafe in AI-mediated channels.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Press releases are not record objects

A record object is an artifact engineered for verification: it defines terms, bounds scope, provides primary references, and maintains version history. Press releases typically do the opposite. They compress nuance, avoid falsifiable details, and distribute near-identical copies across hosts. Under governance scrutiny, this creates an integrity deficit: the artifact cannot support rigorous dispute resolution.

Generative systems respond to this deficit by preferring sources that look more like record objects: technical documentation, standards, filings, data tables, or stable knowledge pages. The press release becomes a weak node in the provenance chain.

2.2 Duplication is a provenance attack on your own content

Syndication fragments authority. Duplicate clustering selects a representative, not necessarily the issuer. When the representative shifts, the “canonical copy” becomes external. That external copy may be modified, re-titled, shortened, or wrapped with unrelated context. Governance failure follows because the organization can no longer guarantee that a user, auditor, or AI system will retrieve the issuer-hosted original.

This is not theoretical. It is an expected outcome of large-scale indexing and retrieval systems. Governance must be designed for this reality.

2.3 Citation eligibility is a governance requirement

In generative search, being “indexed” is insufficient. Content must be eligible for citation. Citation eligibility is a composite of: evidence density, clarity of entity identity, stability of the hosting environment, and absence of manipulative patterns. Press releases often fail at least two of these by design: they are light on evidence and heavy on boilerplate.

Control Objective

Transform press-release-driven messaging into a two-layer system: announcement wrappers plus canonical record objects engineered for citation.

2.4 Narrative drift is a governance incident, not a marketing issue

When AI systems paraphrase announcements, small shifts in language can change legal meaning, product scope, or performance expectations. This is narrative drift: the organization’s claim mutates across summaries and citations, often without clear attribution. In a governance regime, narrative drift is treated as an incident because it can drive misinformed decisions by customers, partners, regulators, or media.

2.5 “Soft claims” become “hard claims” under synthesis

Press releases commonly use hedged language. Synthesis systems compress hedges. The phrase “may improve” becomes “improves.” The phrase “planned” becomes “launched.” The phrase “supports” becomes “guarantees.” Governance must anticipate this conversion. The mitigation is to publish explicit claim constraints and definitions in the canonical record so synthesis engines have a stable boundary to reuse.

2.6 Model-facing ambiguity increases dispute costs

When a press release is the only source, disputes become expensive because no authoritative artifact exists to resolve interpretation. Organizations then attempt reactive correction by issuing another press release. This is structurally weak: it adds more duplicates and more ambiguity. Governance requires a different primitive: a versioned, stable record object where corrections are atomic and traceable.

2.7 Minimal governance model for AI-facing content

Artifacts:
  A1 = Announcement wrapper (press release)
  R1 = Canonical record object (evidence-grade)
  S1 = Supporting sources (data, standards, filings)
Controls:
  C1 = Versioning and change log

  C3 = Citation-optimized structure
  C4 = Monitoring and correction workflow
Rule:
  A1 may exist without R1 only if claim impact is low.
  High-impact claims require R1 + S1 before A1 is distributed.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Governance standards assume traceability. NIST AI RMF 1.0 frames risk management as a structured practice: governance, mapping, measurement, and management. In AI-mediated search, traceability is not confined to internal logs; it must be supported by externally retrievable artifacts. Without a record object, the organization cannot credibly map how a claim was interpreted or why it was cited or omitted.

The EU AI Act elevates transparency and documentation norms for high-impact AI systems. Even for organizations not building AI systems, these norms have a practical effect: external systems increasingly prefer sources that behave like documentation, not advertising. Press releases are low-quality documentation. They become second-class evidence in retrieval.

The governance requirement is therefore architectural: claims must be published in a structure that survives duplication, clustering, and synthesis. The press release alone does not satisfy that requirement.

Audit Reality

If an auditor asks “Where is the authoritative version of this claim?” and the answer is “a press release,” the organization has already accepted a governance debt.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol is a governance control system for AI-facing claims. It does not attempt to manipulate generative systems. It attempts to publish claims in a form that can be safely retrieved, cited, and audited. The protocol is structured as a lifecycle.

4.1 Create a canonical record object before distribution

Before a press release is issued, create a canonical record object that includes: definitions, scope limits, explicit dates, version identifiers, and the minimal evidence needed to validate material claims. The record object is the thing that can be cited without distortion.

4.2 Bind claims to identifiers and constraints

A claim without constraints is an invitation to synthesis drift. The record object must state what the claim does not mean, what it excludes, and the conditions under which it is valid. This makes the claim robust under paraphrase and prevents the model from inventing context.

4.3 Establish a distribution policy that limits duplication

Syndication should be minimized. When syndication is unavoidable, duplicates must be clearly subordinate. The issuer-hosted copy should be unique in structure and evidence density. The objective is to avoid being outcompeted by your own duplicates and to reduce cluster displacement.

4.4 Monitoring: treat AI summaries as an external narrative layer

Monitor AI summaries for misattribution, scope inflation, and temporal errors. Monitoring is not “PR tracking.” It is governance. When an error is observed, publish a correction in the canonical record object and update versioning. Corrections must be atomic and traceable.

4.5 Incident response: containment and correction

If a press-release-derived claim causes harm through AI surfaces, containment requires: a clarified record object, supporting evidence artifacts, and a documented internal decision log explaining what changed and why. The press release is not the primary correction mechanism.

Protocol Output

A governance-grade content stack produces stable, citable records and reduces the probability that generative systems will invent missing specificity.
