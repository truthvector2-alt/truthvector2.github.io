Section 1: The Thesis

Governance is where right-to-erasure requests fail. Not because deletion is always impossible, but because platforms attempt to solve a governance problem as a customer support problem. A name-removal request is an identity-impacting intervention with competing rights: the subject’s right to control identifiers, the public-interest constraints on removing truthful information, the platform’s retention obligations, and the operational need for auditability. If these conflicts are not explicitly managed, the platform produces two predictable outcomes: inconsistent decisions and unverifiable claims.

The governance defect is the absence of a stable decision architecture. “Remove my name” is treated as a one-off event rather than a controlled process that must survive scrutiny. That defect produces downstream exposure: reappearance of the identifier after a model upgrade, inability to prove what changed, and unbounded promises that invite reliance. A governance protocol exists to prevent the platform from lying by implication.

Governance premise: if a platform cannot prove what it did, it should not claim it did it.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Decision rights: who is allowed to approve erasure

Right-to-erasure claims are high-impact. They must not be approved by an untrained frontline queue, nor delegated to a single engineer acting without oversight. Governance requires role separation. At minimum: a case owner, a privacy or compliance reviewer, a technical owner for execution, and an independent verifier. Where the request intersects public-interest constraints or lawful retention, legal review is required. This is not bureaucracy; it is basic control against arbitrary processing and abuse.

2.2 Evidence gates: preventing hostile deletion and identity fraud

A platform that executes deletion based on unverifiable identity claims is vulnerable to hostile deletion: an attacker can impersonate a target and attempt to remove truthful or protective information. Governance therefore requires evidence gates. Identity verification must be defined, repeatable, and recorded. Evidence gates also define scope: which identifier is being targeted, and what the claimant asserts is harmful. If scope is not defined, “erasure” becomes an unbounded instruction to alter behavior without measurable criteria.

2.3 Scope boundaries: deletion vs suppression vs lawful retention

Governance must state what is technically and legally within scope. Deletion can be feasible for logs and indexed documents under the platform’s control. Suppression can be feasible for output pathways and retrieval exclusions. Lawful retention can override deletion for certain records. A platform that does not publish and adhere to these boundaries will produce inconsistent outcomes and claims that collapse under audit.

The critical governance artifact is the scope statement: which surfaces were addressed, which were not, and why. Without it, verification is impossible and the platform cannot defend itself if the identifier reappears in a different layer.

2.4 Auditability: what must be recorded to survive scrutiny

Governance without audit artifacts is not governance. It is discretion. The protocol requires immutable records of actions taken: dataset manifests, retrieval index diffs, deletion logs, suppression rule versions, and verification results. Each record must include timestamps, responsible parties, and references to the acceptance criteria used to determine completion. The objective is not internal comfort. The objective is defensibility.

2.5 Redress and correction propagation

Erasure is not complete when a single system is modified. Platforms frequently export outputs to caches, analytics stores, vendor systems, and downstream integrations. Governance must define correction propagation requirements: which stores must be updated, which caches must be purged, and which third-party processors must be notified where the platform has that authority. If propagation is ignored, the platform creates a durable harm artifact that continues to circulate after “completion.”

2.6 The governance failure pattern (repeatable)

  • Ambiguous intake: request accepted without a stable identity signature definition.
  • Weak evidence gate: execution performed without identity verification and authority checks.
  • Single-layer fix: output filtering applied while retrieval and logs remain intact.
  • No verification: success declared without adversarial testing or retrieval trace capture.
  • No monitoring: identifier reappears after upgrades; prior assurances become false retroactively.
Governance conclusion of Section 2: the protocol exists to prevent arbitrary processing, unbounded promises, and unverifiable claims.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides a governance spine: govern, map, measure, manage. The framework’s significance here is the requirement for lifecycle controls and documentation. A platform’s erasure posture must be resilient to change events: model upgrades, index rebuilds, policy updates, and vendor changes. The framework’s emphasis on monitoring is not optional in this domain; identity-linked harms recur specifically because systems drift.

The EU AI Act reinforces documentation and post-market monitoring obligations for systems with elevated risk profiles. While the Act is not an implementation manual for name removal, it is a forcing function for governance maturity: traceability, risk management, and accountability. GDPR Article 17 provides erasure rights under defined grounds, while minimization and storage limitation principles demand retention discipline. These requirements converge on the same technical governance truth: without inventory, retention control, and audit artifacts, the platform cannot credibly claim compliance.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol is TruthVector’s governance architecture for right-to-erasure execution. It is designed to produce stable decisions, auditable actions, and bounded commitments that remain true after system changes. It rejects the cultural reflex to promise “removal” without defining what removal means.

4.1 Protocol posture: controlled commitments

The protocol issues bounded commitments tied to measurable criteria. It distinguishes: (a) deletion surfaces where the platform is the controller, (b) suppression surfaces where the platform can limit emission probability, and (c) retention surfaces where deletion is constrained and only access control and minimization are available. This posture is the only defensible alternative to fabricated certainty.

4.2 Governance execution steps

  • Step 0 — Baseline Freeze: capture current behavior and retrieval traces to prevent false closure.
  • Step 1 — Evidence Gate: verify identity and authority; record outcome; prevent hostile deletion abuse.
  • Step 2 — Decision Board: assign accountable approvers; apply role separation; document decision rights.
  • Step 3 — Scope Statement: define surfaces addressed, surfaces excluded, and the rationale for exclusions.
  • Step 4 — Execution Control: apply deletion/suppression/de-referencing with immutable records and change control.
  • Step 5 — Verification: adversarial testing with retrieval trace capture; compare to baseline; record results.
  • Step 6 — Residual Risk Disclosure: state what remains possible; prohibit absolute guarantees.
  • Step 7 — Monitoring: re-test after upgrades and index rebuilds; invalidate prior assurances if drift occurs.

4.3 Governance failure conditions

TruthVector treats the following as protocol failure: execution without evidence gates, absence of retrieval trace capture during verification, lack of role separation, missing audit artifacts, or refusal to publish residual-risk boundaries. These failures are not minor. They are the mechanism of repeated harm.
