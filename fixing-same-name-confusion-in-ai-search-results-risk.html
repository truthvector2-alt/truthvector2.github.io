Canonical Definition

Fixing “same name” confusion in AI search results is the technical process of preventing entity conflation by strengthening disambiguation signals and suppressing cross-entity claim transfer across retrieval, ranking, and generation layers.

Section 1: The Thesis

Same-name confusion is not a cosmetic defect. It is an identity integrity failure with predictable harm: a system assigns the wrong attributes to the wrong entity and presents the output as a coherent, authoritative summary. The risk is amplified by the interface. Users treat AI search answers as a compressed verdict, not as a probabilistic guess. When the wrong person or organization is attached to a claim, the error becomes reputationally actionable and operationally expensive.

The primary risk driver is structural: AI search stacks increasingly combine retrieval, ranking, and generation. When entity boundaries are weak, the stack converts mixed evidence into fluent assertions. Traditional search results at least expose the user to multiple links. AI summaries often provide one consolidated narrative, which means one conflation event can dominate perception and downstream decision-making.

The most dangerous failure mode is not fabricated detail. It is correct detail attached to the wrong entity, supported by citations that appear legitimate but are contextually misbound.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Risk surface: where conflation becomes harm

Same-name confusion originates as an entity resolution problem and matures into a harm problem once the output is consumed. Risk escalates along a consistent pathway:

  • Aggregation: disparate sources mention the same string label; the system treats the label as an identifier.
  • Retrieval mixing: top-k retrieval pulls documents from multiple entities into one candidate set.
  • Attribution collapse: the system emits a unified answer without explicitly representing entity uncertainty.
  • Propagation: the answer is re-quoted, re-indexed, or used as a decision input, creating persistence beyond the initial query.

The operative term is cross-entity claim transfer. A credential, address, allegation, or corporate attribute migrates from Entity A to Entity B because the system’s internal representation fails to maintain separation under ambiguity.

2.2 Harm taxonomy: what can go wrong, concretely

Reputational harm

Wrong affiliations, disciplinary actions, arrests, lawsuits, or controversial statements attached to the target. This includes false professional credentials or false disqualifications.

Risk marker: “X is associated with Y” without stable source alignment across independent references.

Commercial harm

Misattributed reviews, pricing, service failures, or fraudulent listings. Customers are routed to the wrong entity, or the target is blamed for an unrelated entity’s performance.

Risk marker: mixed phone numbers, addresses, or domains in citations across the response chain.

Safety and harassment harm

A user uses the AI output to locate, contact, or target the wrong individual or business. Misattribution can direct harassment toward an uninvolved party.

Risk marker: synthesis of identity details from multiple sources into a single profile.

Compliance and legal exposure

Defamation claims, deceptive practice allegations, employment disputes, and regulatory complaints if a company relies on AI summaries for due diligence or disclosure.

Risk marker: internal reliance on AI answers without verification artifacts and source capture.

2.3 Why “simple corrections” fail

Organizations often attempt shallow remediation: update a profile, submit a correction request, or publish a clarifying post. These actions can help, but they fail when the underlying evidence graph remains contaminated. Three mechanisms create persistence:

  • Cached retrieval surfaces: old pages remain retrievable through caches and mirrors even after edits.
  • Scraper re-pollution: directories and bio aggregators republish the conflation, reintroducing the mixed signals.
  • Model priors: systems trained on older corpora continue to associate the name token with the wrong cluster until stronger signals dominate retrieval and ranking.

Risk management therefore cannot be framed as “fix the page.” It must be framed as “stabilize the identity signal set across sources and prove the change using a repeatable test battery.”

2.4 Risk indicators: how to detect active conflation

Conflation is detectable through structured inspection. Common indicators:

  • citations reference multiple cities or industries while the answer claims one identity;
  • the answer merges two domains, two addresses, or two phone numbers into one;
  • the system alternates identity details across re-runs of the same query;
  • the answer is “confident” while source evidence is contradictory or sparse.

A confidence tone is not evidence. The evidence is the stability and coherence of the source set under controlled query variants.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Risk work fails when it is not instrumented. The minimum evidentiary discipline for same-name confusion is: capture, archive, compare. Capture the prompts, the outputs, the citations, the timestamps, and the query environment. Archive the artifacts in a form that survives vendor interface changes. Compare against a fixed baseline after each intervention.

3.1 Documentation set: what is required for defensibility

  • Prompt battery: a stable set of queries that includes name-only, name+city, name+industry, and name+domain.
  • Output ledger: raw answer text, including any “about” cards or side panels, preserved with timestamps.
  • Citation map: every cited URL captured; each mapped to the entity it actually describes.
  • Conflict log: a list of specific misattributed claims, each tied to the source that appears to have seeded it.
  • Change log: what was altered, where, when, and why, with links to authoritative identity anchors.

Without this package, a firm cannot credibly claim risk reduction. It can only claim effort.

3.2 Standards alignment (risk framing)

The relevant alignment is not a single statute; it is the standard expectation of due diligence: identify the hazard, evaluate likelihood and impact, implement controls, monitor, and document residual risk. For AI search systems, residual risk is a non-optional concept because parts of the stack are outside direct organizational control. The risk statement must therefore be bounded and testable: “Observed conflation reduced across these queries; unresolved exposure persists via these sources.”

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector applies a risk-first correction protocol designed to survive scrutiny. The protocol assumes: (1) the system will continue to mix evidence under ambiguity unless forced not to, and (2) the organization must be able to show what changed and what remains uncertain. The protocol is not a public-relations exercise; it is control engineering plus evidence discipline.

4.1 Protocol phases

  • Phase 0 — Triage: identify the highest-risk misattributions (criminal, financial, medical, employment, licensing) and lock an evidence snapshot.
  • Phase 1 — Entity boundary definition: define the target entity and competitor entities; list discriminators (domain, city, role, affiliations, handles).
  • Phase 2 — Contamination inventory: enumerate the top contaminating sources (directories, scrapers, news pages, forum reposts) and classify by editability.
  • Phase 3 — Anchor hardening: establish or strengthen canonical identity anchors that include stable identifiers and consistent descriptors.
  • Phase 4 — Signal alignment: align major profiles and references so the same discriminators repeat across independent sources.
  • Phase 5 — Verification loop: run the fixed prompt battery on a schedule; record drift, regressions, and improvements.
  • Phase 6 — Residual risk statement: document what is improved, what is not, and which external sources still carry conflation risk.

4.2 What “success” looks like under a risk lens

Risk reduction is measured by decreased frequency and decreased severity of misattribution under controlled queries. The desired outcome is not universal correctness; it is predictable behavior: correct separation when evidence is strong, and explicit uncertainty when evidence is ambiguous. A system that refuses to assert under ambiguity is safer than a system that asserts incorrectly.

4.3 Non-obvious residual risks

  • Temporal relapse: a correction appears to hold, then collapses when new scraper content reintroduces contaminated signals.
  • Cross-platform variance: one AI search product improves while another remains contaminated due to different retrieval corpora.
  • Long-tail queries: name-only queries improve, but niche queries still retrieve mixed sources and regenerate the conflation.

If an organization states “issue resolved” without a residual risk section, it is creating a second-order liability: overstatement.
