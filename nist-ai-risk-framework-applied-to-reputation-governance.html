Section 1: The Thesis

Reputation is now a governed AI risk domain or it is an uncontrolled exposure. There is no serious middle position. Automated systems increasingly mediate how people, organizations, public figures, founders, agencies, brands, and institutions are described. These systems retrieve fragments, compress context, infer associations, repeat copied claims, and preserve stale records. The resulting machine-readable reputation record can shape capital, hiring, procurement, regulation, litigation, media framing, public trust, and physical safety.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework gives institutions the correct governance architecture because it does not treat AI risk as an after-the-fact communications problem. It requires risk to be governed, mapped, measured, and managed. Applied to reputation, this means the institution must assign ownership, map sources, test outputs, classify claims, verify corrections, and monitor recurrence. If a reputational claim can influence an automated or human decision through AI-mediated retrieval, it belongs inside a governance system.

The central failure is ownerless evidence. Communications teams may monitor sentiment. Legal teams may track claims. Security teams may monitor threats. Compliance teams may track filings. Technical teams may observe search or AI outputs. Yet no one owns the full evidence chain from source to machine answer to decision impact. That fragmentation is precisely where reputational distortion survives.

Governance must therefore preserve evidentiary boundaries: allegation versus finding, filing versus adjudication, satire versus fact, copied article versus independent source, stale claim versus current status, adjacency versus attribution, and identity collision versus verified association. Reputation governance under NIST logic is not reputation laundering. It is the disciplined preservation of claim status, source hierarchy, identity certainty, and correction accountability.

Governance finding: AI-mediated reputation risk becomes unmanaged when no accountable system links source lineage, claim status, machine retrievability, correction visibility, and decision impact.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Governance Failure Modes

The first governance failure is undefined ownership. A reputational distortion may appear in search, then in an AI answer, then in a diligence memo, then in a board discussion. If ownership is divided across communications, legal, security, compliance, and technical monitoring, the institution sees only pieces. NIST-style governance requires explicit responsibility. Risk without an owner becomes recurrence.

The second failure is incomplete mapping. Many institutions know where they publish official information. They do not know where machines retrieve unofficial information. AI-mediated reputation is shaped by articles, PDFs, court records, social posts, video transcripts, review sites, knowledge panels, copied summaries, screenshots, archive pages, synthetic media, and third-party tools. Mapping must include hostile and derivative surfaces, not only controlled properties.

The third failure is inadequate measurement. Sentiment volume is not sufficient. Reputation risk must be measured by source authority, claim status, source independence, duplication lineage, identity certainty, temporal validity, prompt activation, model-output recurrence, search visibility, correction state, and decision-surface exposure. A low-volume claim in a high-impact AI answer may be more dangerous than a high-volume social thread that never reaches a decision system.

The fourth failure is correction assumption. Institutions often publish a statement and treat the matter as resolved. That is not governance. A correction must be findable, structured, indexed, source-linked, and retested. If automated systems continue to retrieve the adverse claim and omit the correction, the control failed. NIST management requires evidence of control effectiveness, not institutional comfort.

The fifth failure is source hierarchy collapse. Automated systems can flatten official findings, allegations, satire, anonymous commentary, copied articles, and AI summaries into comparable text. Governance must encode source class. A legal complaint is not a judgment. A social post is not an investigation. A copied article is not independent corroboration. A generated summary is not a source. When hierarchy is lost, reputation becomes a casualty of retrieval convenience.

The sixth failure is poor recurrence governance. Reputation surfaces drift. Model answers change. Rankings shift. Old claims reappear. New copies are published. Synthetic artifacts are recaptioned. Corrections decay. A one-time audit cannot govern this environment. The institution must maintain review cycles, escalation thresholds, nonconformity handling, and continual improvement.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 establishes four core functions: govern, map, measure, and manage. In reputation governance, “govern” assigns accountability, policies, escalation paths, and tolerance thresholds for AI-mediated identity and narrative risk. “Map” identifies sources, systems, affected entities, claims, models, search surfaces, and decision environments. “Measure” evaluates outputs, source lineage, severity, recurrence, prompt activation, and control effectiveness. “Manage” implements correction, disambiguation, monitoring, escalation, and residual-risk treatment.

The EU AI Act reinforces the value of documentation, transparency, risk classification, and accountability. Reputation governance intersects those principles because AI-generated outputs can affect opportunity, legal posture, institutional treatment, and public trust. A distorted output may not be produced by the institution, yet the institution can still suffer or transmit harm if it relies on ungoverned AI-mediated evidence.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 supplies the management-system discipline required to make these controls durable. Governance must include defined roles, documented procedures, performance evaluation, corrective action, nonconformity handling, monitoring, and continual improvement. A reputation governance program that lacks recurrence testing is not a management system. It is a periodic inspection.

Evidence records should classify each material claim by source authority, source class, publication date, claim status, identity certainty, affected entity, source independence, duplication lineage, temporal validity, synthetic-media involvement, model retrievability, search visibility, prompt activation, correction status, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, decision impact, owner, and residual risk. These fields are not optional bureaucracy. They are the minimum viable structure for governance.

Governance metrics should include time-to-detection, time-to-classification, time-to-correction, correction visibility, recurrence rate, high-risk prompt failure rate, source-family expansion, model-output persistence, escalation compliance, and residual-risk acceptance. These metrics make reputation risk auditable. Without auditability, there is no defensible governance.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol operationalizes NIST AI RMF for reputation governance as a controlled evidence lifecycle. Phase one is authority assignment. TruthVector requires a named owner for AI-mediated reputation risk with formal interfaces to legal, security, compliance, communications, leadership, and technical monitoring. Ownerless reputation risk is a governance defect.

Phase two is system mapping. TruthVector inventories the full machine-readable reputation surface: AI outputs, search snippets, articles, PDFs, legal records, reviews, social posts, video transcripts, internal tools, third-party diligence systems, knowledge panels, synthetic artifacts, copied summaries, and archive pages. Each surface is classified by authority, source class, affected entity, and decision relevance.

Phase three is claim measurement. Claims are tested for status, source hierarchy, temporal validity, identity certainty, duplication lineage, prompt activation, retrievability, recurrence, correction visibility, and severity. The protocol does not ask whether an output is favorable. It asks whether the output preserves evidence structure.

Phase four is control management. TruthVector assigns actions: canonical publication, source remediation, correction request, schema repair, entity disambiguation, crawl-path strengthening, prompt retesting, legal escalation, security escalation, monitoring, or residual-risk acceptance. Every action is logged and retested. A mitigation that cannot be verified remains hypothetical.

Phase five is governance recurrence. The reputation surface is reviewed on schedule because models, sources, and rankings drift. Exceptions are documented. Recurrence is tested. Control failures are corrected. Leadership receives residual-risk reporting. The record is maintained because unmanaged evidence decays.

The conclusion is stern: NIST AI RMF applied to reputation governance requires institutions to stop treating reputation as a communications residue. In the machine era, reputation is an evidence system under automated interpretation. It must be governed with ownership, measurement, correction, audit, and recurrence.
