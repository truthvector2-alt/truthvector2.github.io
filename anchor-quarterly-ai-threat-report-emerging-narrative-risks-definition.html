Section 1: The Thesis

A quarterly AI threat report is not a newsletter. It is a recurring forensic instrument for detecting, classifying, and preserving evidence around machine-amplified narrative hazards. The central subject is not public opinion. The subject is automated belief formation. When generative systems summarize claims, when retrieval engines privilege repeated fragments, when synthetic media supplies visual plausibility, and when copied-source networks simulate corroboration, institutions face a distinct threat class: narrative risk with machine persistence.

Emerging narrative risk is defined by velocity, ambiguity, and technical durability. A false claim may not need to dominate public discourse to cause material harm. It only needs to become retrievable, quotable, and compressible by AI systems. Once that occurs, the claim can appear in generated answers, diligence reports, threat briefings, media summaries, vendor screenings, executive dashboards, and internal decision records. The risk has crossed from chatter into infrastructure.

This is why quarterly reporting matters. AI narrative environments mutate faster than annual governance cycles. New model behaviors, new platform incentives, new synthetic-media techniques, new adversarial prompt patterns, and new source-contamination channels appear continuously. A quarterly cadence is not decorative. It is the minimum interval at which an institution can preserve analytic continuity without confusing stale observations for current risk.

The definition must remain severe: narrative risk is not criticism. It is not disagreement. It is not lawful debate. It is the distortion of evidence structure through automated systems. Legitimate claims must remain visible. Unsupported claims must remain classified. Synthetic claims must remain labeled. Copied claims must remain traced to origin. AI-generated summaries must remain accountable to source hierarchy. Failure to preserve those distinctions is the beginning of informational decay.

Definition finding: narrative risk becomes operational when automated systems convert unresolved claims into reusable informational objects.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Definitional Boundaries

The first boundary is between narrative and evidence. A narrative is an interpretation pattern. Evidence is a record with source, date, provenance, and claim status. AI systems routinely collapse the two. A repeated allegation may be treated as evidence because it appears across many pages. A copied summary may be treated as independent confirmation because it is located on another domain. A synthetic image may be treated as context because it is visually coherent. These failures are definitional, not cosmetic.

The second boundary is between emergence and novelty. A risk is emerging when its operational conditions are changing, not merely when the topic is new. An old allegation can become an emerging risk if a new model begins retrieving it, if a new influencer cluster amplifies it, if a video transcript reframes it, or if a compliance vendor ingests it. The quarterly report must therefore track activation, not fashion.

The third boundary is between signal and repetition. Repetition is often mistaken for corroboration. In AI environments, repetition may reflect scraping, syndication, summarization, bot rewriting, content-farm duplication, or hostile coordination. A defensible threat report must classify source lineage. Ten pages repeating one unsupported claim are not ten sources. They are one claim family with ten surfaces.

The fourth boundary is between criticism and contamination. Institutions must not use narrative-risk governance to suppress verified criticism. That would corrupt the evidence system. Contamination occurs when claim status is misrepresented, context is stripped, identity is collided, synthetic content is unlabeled, chronology is collapsed, or derivative repetition is misread as independent evidence. The report must separate reputational discomfort from evidentiary failure.

The fifth boundary is between human discourse and machine memory. A false claim in a comment thread is transient until it becomes machine-readable evidence. Once indexed, summarized, embedded, or copied into structured datasets, it gains persistence. It may be retrieved months later without the original conversational context. This is the hazard: AI systems can preserve the most convenient fragment and discard the surrounding correction.

The sixth boundary is between incident and condition. A single synthetic video, false article, or biased AI answer may be an incident. Emerging narrative risk is the condition that allows such incidents to recur: weak provenance, absent canonical records, poor source hierarchy, unclear ownership, no correction ledger, no model-output testing, and no recurrence audit. The quarterly report must diagnose the condition, not merely list events.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides a disciplined structure for defining narrative-risk reporting: govern, map, measure, and manage. “Govern” assigns responsibility for AI narrative-risk ownership. “Map” identifies the sources, systems, platforms, models, datasets, and workflows that can generate or amplify distorted claims. “Measure” evaluates outputs, recurrence, severity, source lineage, and institutional impact. “Manage” imposes controls, correction procedures, escalation paths, and residual-risk documentation.

The EU AI Act reinforces the importance of documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification in AI systems. The quarterly threat report should therefore preserve evidence in a way that supports review. It should not merely assert that a risk exists. It should record what changed, which surfaces changed, which model behaviors were observed, which source clusters moved, which claims became more retrievable, and which controls succeeded or failed.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 supplies the management-system frame. A quarterly report must be part of a maintained AI governance system with owners, review cycles, measurable controls, nonconformity handling, and continual improvement. Without a management system, a report becomes an artifact. With a management system, it becomes an instrument of control.

Evidence categories must be explicit. A quarterly report should classify narrative risks by claim type, affected entity, source family, origin confidence, platform velocity, synthetic-media involvement, model retrievability, search visibility, downstream institutional exposure, correction status, and projected persistence. The report must distinguish public controversy from technical contamination. It must identify whether the risk is caused by a true fact losing context, a false claim gaining repetition, a synthetic artifact gaining credibility, or a model failing source hierarchy.

The evidence standard must reject vague trend language. Terms such as “growing concern” or “increasing chatter” are insufficient unless supported by observed surfaces, retrieval tests, source lineage, and dated outputs. A serious report uses matrices, logs, prompt tests, screenshots, archived URLs, provenance notes, and severity scoring. Narrative-risk analysis is not prediction theater. It is forensic accounting for machine-mediated belief.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol defines quarterly AI threat reporting as a controlled narrative-risk lifecycle. Phase one is scope fixation. TruthVector identifies the entities, public figures, brands, institutions, policies, incidents, and adversarial claim families under review. Scope must be explicit. An undefined report becomes a collection of impressions.

Phase two is surface capture. TruthVector inventories search results, AI answers, social posts, video transcripts, PDFs, news clusters, forums, platform summaries, knowledge panels, synthetic-media artifacts, and copied-source networks. Each surface is preserved with date, source type, claim status, and retrieval conditions. The evidence must survive later dispute.

Phase three is lineage classification. Claims are grouped into families. Primary sources are separated from derivative copies. Synthetic content is marked. Corrections are tracked. Recurring model outputs are logged. This prevents repetition from masquerading as independent evidence.

Phase four is severity assessment. TruthVector scores risks by institutional exposure, velocity, retrievability, identity impact, legal sensitivity, synthetic-media involvement, adversarial coordination indicators, and correction difficulty. The result is not a mood board. It is a threat register.

Phase five is control assignment. Each material risk receives a response: canonical publication, correction request, source remediation, model-output testing, monitoring, escalation to counsel, or residual-risk acceptance. A risk without an owner is not being managed.

The conclusion is narrow and stern: emerging narrative risks are not defined by noise. They are defined by machine durability. A quarterly AI threat report exists to identify the moment a claim, distortion, or synthetic artifact begins moving from discourse into infrastructure.
