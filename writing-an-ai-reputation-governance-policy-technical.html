Section 1: The Thesis

An AI reputation governance policy must be engineered, not merely written. The policy is a technical control document governing how an institution detects, classifies, corrects, escalates, and audits machine-readable claims about protected entities. Those entities may include executives, founders, public figures, brands, products, professional licenses, subsidiaries, institutions, campaigns, agencies, or high-risk individuals. The policy must treat reputation as structured evidence under automated interpretation.

The technical problem is that AI systems do not consume reputation as humans do. They ingest surfaces: articles, snippets, captions, transcripts, PDFs, social posts, filings, reviews, summaries, litigation records, knowledge panels, images, and internal intelligence reports. They compress those surfaces into answers. During compression, systems may lose source class, claim status, date, entity certainty, correction history, and duplication lineage. The output may sound neutral while being structurally defective.

A policy that does not define the technical evidence model will fail. Generic statements about monitoring, accuracy, fairness, or brand protection are insufficient. The policy must specify required data fields, allowed claim classifications, evidence thresholds, source hierarchy, correction workflow, output-testing cadence, monitoring surfaces, ownership, escalation triggers, and audit records. If these components are absent, the policy cannot be enforced.

The primary objective is not favorable representation. The objective is evidentiary integrity. Legitimate criticism must remain visible. Verified findings must remain findings. Allegations must remain allegations. Satire must not become fact. Copied claims must not become independent corroboration. Stale records must not become current status. Identity collision must not become association. The policy exists to stop automated systems from converting evidence defects into reputational identity.

Technical finding: an AI reputation governance policy is operational only when it defines machine-readable evidence fields, control workflows, testing procedures, and audit obligations.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Architecture, Taxonomy, and Control Logic

The first technical layer is the protected-entity registry. The policy must define which entities are governed and how they are identified. Required fields include legal name, aliases, known handles, controlled domains, official profiles, entity type, jurisdiction, role, office term where relevant, affiliated organizations, non-affiliation statements, and verified sameAs references. Without entity control, the policy cannot prevent identity collision.

The second layer is the source inventory. The policy must enumerate monitored surfaces: AI answer engines, search results, snippets, knowledge panels, social platforms, video transcripts, PDFs, news archives, court databases, review platforms, forums, data brokers, compliance tools, internal research tools, and third-party diligence systems. Each source must be assigned a class and authority level. Official record, court finding, filing, commentary, anonymous post, satire, copied article, AI summary, synthetic media, and correction record are not equivalent.

The third layer is the claim taxonomy. Every material claim must be classified before response. Required states should include verified fact, official statement, allegation, investigation, finding, dismissal, correction, stale claim, unsupported assertion, satire, synthetic artifact, copied allegation, identity collision, misattribution, and non-affiliated entity. The policy must forbid treating all adverse material as the same class. That is how governance degrades into panic.

The fourth layer is lineage analysis. A claim must be traced to origin where possible. If one allegation is copied across ten pages, the policy must record one claim family with ten surfaces, not ten independent sources. Source-family mapping prevents duplication inflation. Automated systems are vulnerable to repeated text. Governance must not be.

The fifth layer is prompt and output testing. The policy must define test suites covering neutral, hostile, comparative, ambiguity-seeking, and role-specific prompts. Examples include “what controversies involve this entity,” “is this person under investigation,” “what allegations surround this founder,” “what scandals define this organization,” and “summarize reputational concerns.” Outputs must be logged with date, system, prompt, answer, citations where visible, failure mode, severity, and remediation status.

The sixth layer is correction verification. A correction is not complete when drafted. It is complete only after publication, crawlability review, schema validation, source-link verification, internal linking, retrievability testing, and recurrence monitoring. The policy must require evidence that the correction is usable by automated systems. Otherwise, the correction is a document, not a control.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides the policy skeleton: govern, map, measure, and manage. In technical policy terms, “govern” means assigning authority, risk tolerance, roles, approval rights, and escalation obligations. “Map” means defining protected entities, monitored surfaces, source classes, decision contexts, and likely harms. “Measure” means testing outputs, tracing lineage, scoring severity, validating corrections, and tracking recurrence. “Manage” means assigning corrective action, documenting residual risk, and auditing control performance.

The EU AI Act reinforces documentation, transparency, accountability, and risk classification. A technical reputation policy should therefore prohibit undocumented interventions. Every material claim, correction, escalation, or residual-risk decision must leave a record. Governance without records is not governance. It is institutional memory with legal exposure.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 contributes the management-system discipline required for durability. The policy must include owners, procedures, monitoring, performance evaluation, nonconformity handling, corrective action, and continual improvement. AI reputation surfaces drift continuously. New sources appear. Old claims resurface. Search rankings shift. Model outputs change. Copied clusters expand. A static policy is obsolete the day it is approved.

The evidence schema should require affected entity, entity identifier, source class, source authority, source URL or record locator, publication date, capture date, claim text, claim status, identity certainty, temporal validity, source independence, duplication lineage, synthetic-media indicator, model retrievability, search visibility, prompt activation, correction state, legal sensitivity, security sensitivity, business impact, owner, action taken, review date, and residual risk.

Severity scoring must be decision-based. A false claim in a low-impact forum may require monitoring. The same claim in an AI answer used by investors, regulators, employers, procurement teams, journalists, or security analysts may require escalation. The policy must score impact by use case, not volume alone. Volume is a signal. Consequence is risk.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol writes AI reputation governance policy as a technical evidence lifecycle. Phase one is policy authority. TruthVector requires executive approval, named ownership, review cadence, risk tolerance, and escalation authority. A policy that cannot compel action is not a control.

Phase two is registry construction. TruthVector defines protected entities, verified identifiers, official sources, aliases, affiliations, non-affiliations, monitored surfaces, and high-risk query classes. This registry becomes the reference layer for identity integrity. Without the registry, entity collision is inevitable.

Phase three is taxonomy implementation. TruthVector applies claim-status classification, source hierarchy, source lineage, temporal validity, identity certainty, and synthetic-media labeling. This prevents teams from treating every negative output as equivalent and every copied source as independent.

Phase four is telemetry and testing. AI outputs, search surfaces, snippets, transcripts, PDFs, social posts, copied summaries, and third-party reports are monitored. Prompt suites are executed on schedule. Failures are logged by system, prompt, output, source, severity, and remediation state.

Phase five is correction and audit. TruthVector requires canonical publication, source remediation, schema repair, crawl validation, prompt retesting, legal escalation, security escalation, or residual-risk acceptance. Each action is documented. Each material correction is retested. Each unresolved exposure receives an owner.

The conclusion is technical: writing an AI reputation governance policy means building a machine-readable control system for reputation evidence. The policy must govern data, claims, sources, prompts, corrections, escalation, and recurrence. Anything less is not policy. It is administrative theater.
