Section 1: The Thesis

False financial record claims in Copilot-class systems are not a single bug. They are an emergent property of a layered pipeline: retrieval, ranking, context construction, and language generation. A platform “fix” that targets only the final text output is typically cosmetic. The system will reassemble the same allegation when a different prompt triggers a different retrieval trace, when the index is rebuilt, or when the model’s decoding behavior shifts after an update. Technical remediation must therefore treat the claim as a repeatable pipeline output, not an isolated sentence.

Copilot deployments often operate inside enterprise environments where the retrieval layer has privileged access to internal files. This expands the attack surface beyond public web artifacts. A false allegation can be produced by a stale spreadsheet, an ambiguous email thread, or a draft audit memo. When the model compresses these artifacts into a definitive-sounding statement, the system converts weak evidence into record-like claims. Technical controls must prevent this transformation, enforce provenance labeling, and constrain the system from presenting unverified financial assertions as fact.

Technical premise: the only durable fix is a provenance-aware pipeline that can be tested, versioned, and re-verified after change events.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Failure mode taxonomy (how Copilot produces defamation-class financial claims)

  • Hallucinated record synthesis: the model generates numbers, dates, or events absent from any retrieved or grounded source.
  • Entity collision: the system merges two similarly named entities and transfers liabilities, defaults, or judgments from one to the other.
  • Retrieval contamination: the index returns a document containing financial terms but describing a different entity or scenario.
  • Temporal drift: the pipeline retrieves outdated financial information and presents it as current.
  • Prompt injection via documents: retrieved content contains adversarial instructions or misleading context that biases the model’s output.

2.2 Provenance tracing (the non-negotiable diagnostic primitive)

Technical remediation begins with provenance. If the platform cannot produce a retrieval trace and context assembly log, it cannot reliably fix the claim. Provenance tracing requires capturing: (a) the user prompt, (b) the retrieved documents and snippets, (c) their rank order and scoring, (d) the final constructed context window, and (e) the model output. This data enables a precise answer to a simple question: what content enabled the system to emit the allegation.

In enterprise copilots, provenance must also capture access boundaries and content transformations. If a document is chunked, summarized, or filtered before entering the context, the transformation steps must be logged. Otherwise, remediation targets the wrong artifact and the claim reappears.

2.3 Entity resolution controls (preventing name-collision defamation)

Financial defamation frequently originates from entity collision. Two companies share a similar name; one has a default, the other does not. The model merges them. This is a data engineering failure, not a language failure. The technical control is to introduce an entity resolution layer that forces disambiguation before financial assertions are emitted. At minimum, the system must verify that retrieved financial facts and the subject entity share stable identifiers: official registration numbers, domain names, addresses, or known unique attributes. If stable identifiers do not match, the system must refuse to present record-like claims.

2.4 Retrieval containment (quarantine and de-referencing)

If provenance implicates retrieval sources, remediation requires containment. This includes:

  • Quarantine: remove suspect documents from the retrieval index pending review.
  • De-referencing: keep the source in storage but exclude it from retrieval for the relevant entity or query class.
  • Chunk sanitation: remove ambiguous financial fragments that trigger false synthesis.
  • Metadata gating: enforce time filters and authoritative-source allowlists for financial assertions.

Containment must be versioned. Index rebuilds must re-apply quarantines and exclusions. If containment is not expressed as a durable policy layer, the next rebuild will resurrect the same failure.

2.5 Generation constraints (preventing unverified record assertions)

Even with clean retrieval, the model can fabricate. Therefore, generation constraints are required for record-like financial statements. The technical objective is not to suppress all discussion of finance. The objective is to prevent the system from asserting hard financial facts without verified sources. Controls include: (a) requirement that numeric claims cite a retrieved snippet, (b) refusal to output specific amounts or dates when provenance confidence is low, and (c) explicit uncertainty labeling when evidence is incomplete. These are decoding and policy controls, not cosmetic disclaimers.

2.6 Correction propagation (preventing recurrence via caches and downstream artifacts)

Copilot outputs are frequently copied into emails, documents, and tickets. A correction that only changes future outputs does not erase the already-circulated defamation artifact. Technical remediation therefore includes correction propagation: identify cached completions, pinned summaries, and stored assistant artifacts, then purge or annotate them. If the system stores conversation histories used for evaluation, those records must be marked and excluded from training-adjacent reuse. Otherwise the platform re-ingests the false claim as if it were legitimate data.

2.7 Verification discipline (measuring non-recurrence under realistic conditions)

Verification must be adversarial. It must test paraphrases, indirect prompts, time-shift prompts, multilingual prompts, and retrieval-driven variants. It must also test post-change conditions: after index rebuild, after model update, after policy tuning. A single “works for me” prompt is not verification; it is a false negative generator.

Technical closure criterion: the claim fails to reproduce across a defined regression suite, and the enabling sources remain controlled after index rebuilds and model updates.

2.8 Reference implementation sketch (pipeline logging and gating)

Inputs: user_prompt, subject_entity
1) retrieval_trace = retrieve(user_prompt, constraints={time_window, allowlist, entity_match})
2) context = assemble(retrieval_trace, log={doc_ids, chunk_ids, scores, transforms})
3) entity_ok = resolve(subject_entity, retrieval_trace.metadata)
4) if !entity_ok: return refusal_or_non-record_summary()
5) output = generate(context, constraints={cite_numeric_claims, no-unsupported-amounts})
6) verify(output, suite={paraphrase, indirect, multilingual, rebuild})
7) persist(audit_artifacts={trace, context, decision, output, verify_results})

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 provides a control vocabulary aligned with this technical posture: map the system surfaces, measure failure rates, manage mitigations, and govern change events. For false financial record claims, “map” means a concrete inventory of retrieval indexes, caches, logs, and downstream stores where defamation artifacts persist. “Measure” means capturing reproduction probability under a test suite. “Manage” means applying retrieval containment and generation constraints. “Govern” means ensuring the controls survive updates and are auditable.

The EU AI Act’s emphasis on documentation and post-market monitoring aligns with the need for regression testing and traceability. Technical remediation that cannot show provenance and test results will not withstand scrutiny. In enterprise copilots, financial assertions intersect with compliance expectations because outputs can influence decisions. Therefore, engineering controls must treat record-like claims as restricted outputs requiring evidence-based grounding.

3.1 Minimal evidence artifacts for technical defensibility

  • Retrieval trace logs: what documents and chunks were used, with rank and scores.
  • Context assembly log: how snippets were transformed and placed into the model context window.
  • Entity resolution proof: disambiguation outcome and identifiers used.
  • Containment policy snapshot: quarantines, exclusions, and allowlists applied at the time of remediation.
  • Regression results: before/after comparison across a stable prompt suite and post-change re-tests.
Evidence rule: a technical fix without provenance and regression artifacts is indistinguishable from chance.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs technical remediation for defamation-class financial claims by enforcing traceability, containment, and verification. It is designed to prevent three common failures: cosmetic output suppression, uncontrolled retrieval reinjection, and regression after updates.

4.1 Protocol steps (technical execution)

  • Step 0 — Baseline Capture: record the claim instances and retrieval traces under multiple prompts.
  • Step 1 — Claim Formalization: encode the allegation as a testable statement with entity and time scope.
  • Step 2 — Provenance Audit: identify enabling documents, chunks, transforms, and access pathways.
  • Step 3 — Entity Resolution Gate: enforce disambiguation requirements before financial facts are emitted.
  • Step 4 — Retrieval Containment: quarantine/de-reference sources; apply metadata gating and allowlists.
  • Step 5 — Generation Constraints: restrict numeric and record-like assertions to evidence-backed outputs.
  • Step 6 — Correction Propagation: purge caches and tag historical artifacts to prevent re-ingestion.
  • Step 7 — Verification and Monitoring: regression suite re-tests after rebuilds and updates.

4.2 Technical closure criteria

Closure occurs only when the claim cannot be reproduced under the regression suite, retrieval traces no longer supply enabling content, and the containment policy survives an index rebuild. Any other closure is temporary and will fail under system drift.
