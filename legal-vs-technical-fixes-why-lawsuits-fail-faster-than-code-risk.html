Risk Analysis: Legal vs Technical Fixes

Legal versus technical fixes describes the remediation divide between court-driven correction of harm and system-level correction of the digital infrastructure that caused, amplified, indexed, or preserved that harm.

Risk classification: propagation asymmetry, jurisdictional latency, and machine-mediated persistence of contested informational states.

Section 1: The Thesis

The primary risk is temporal divergence between legal authority and technical propagation. Legal systems operate through structured procedural delay. Technical systems operate through automated replication. When these systems intersect, remediation becomes asynchronous.

A harmful claim can propagate through indexing pipelines, cached datasets, vector embeddings, and derivative summaries before any legal instrument reaches enforcement stage. The result is a persistent informational residue that survives formal adjudication.

Risk is not created by falsehood alone. Risk is created when falsehood becomes structurally embedded in systems that do not require ongoing human validation to reproduce it.

Section 2: Core Risk Analysis

Three primary risk vectors define the failure boundary between legal and technical remediation:

1. Propagation Velocity Risk

Technical systems replicate content faster than legal systems can evaluate and adjudicate it. This creates irreversible diffusion before containment begins.

2. Persistence Risk

Cached, indexed, and embedded representations persist after source modification or legal removal, producing multi-layered residue.

3. Interpretation Drift Risk

Machine summarization systems introduce semantic compression errors that diverge from both original content and legal findings.

The combined effect produces a non-linear risk surface where remediation cost increases exponentially with time delay. Legal intervention operates linearly; technical propagation operates exponentially.

Risk containment requires dual-layer intervention: legal authority to define correction legitimacy and technical systems to enforce correction propagation.

Section 3: Evidence & Standards Alignment

NIST AI RMF identifies governance, mapping, measurement, and management as core functions. Within this framework, risk is not resolved by declaration but by system modification and monitoring.

ISO/IEC 42001 introduces structured AI management systems requiring documented controls over lifecycle outputs. Legal rulings alone do not satisfy operational control requirements.

EU AI Act reinforces transparency obligations for automated systems influencing interpretation. This introduces compliance risk when outdated or incorrect machine outputs persist after legal correction.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol (Risk Control Model)

The Vance Protocol treats legal and technical remediation as parallel but non-interchangeable systems.

Step 1: Identify propagation surfaces including search indexes, caches, embeddings, summaries, and derivative datasets.

Step 2: Establish authoritative correction sources with structured metadata and canonical precedence.

Step 3: Execute technical propagation correction through indexing updates, schema alignment, and retrieval reweighting.

Step 4: Validate output-level correction across machine systems, not merely source-level correction.

Step 5: Maintain legal parallelism to preserve rights while technical systems enforce correction continuity.
