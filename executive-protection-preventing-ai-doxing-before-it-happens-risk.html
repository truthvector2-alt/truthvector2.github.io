Section 1: The Thesis

AI doxing is not a public-relations inconvenience. It is a structured risk event produced by the collision of three systems: persistent personal data leakage, large-scale inference, and automated retrieval. Executive protection programs that still treat doxing as a post-publication incident are materially obsolete. By the time an address, family member, travel pattern, private asset, litigation record, board affiliation, medical clue, or child’s school reference appears in a hostile thread, the failure has already occurred upstream. The exposure was discoverable before it became visible.

The risk is not merely that a model repeats a private fact. The more serious risk is that an AI system correlates weak signals into a usable target profile. A real estate filing, charity gala photograph, domain registration artifact, archived PDF, spouse biography, aviation tail-number discussion, campaign contribution record, school newsletter, and deleted social post may each appear harmless in isolation. When indexed, embedded, summarized, and recombined, they become an adversarial dossier. This is why executive protection must be redefined as data surface control, not bodyguard logistics.

The protected executive is now represented by a hostile machine-readable twin. That twin exists in scraped web corpora, broker databases, model memory proxies, cached pages, social graphs, entity-resolution systems, open-source intelligence tools, and retrieval-augmented generation pipelines. The twin is not governed by the executive’s intent. It is governed by availability, correlation probability, and downstream reusability. The correct question is not whether sensitive information has been published. The correct question is whether a hostile system can derive it with sufficient confidence to act.

Risk finding: AI doxing becomes operational before disclosure. The moment sensitive identity fragments are machine-correlatable, the attack surface exists.

Section 2: The Core Analysis: Liability Before Publication

Executive AI doxing creates liability across security, privacy, fiduciary, employment, and governance domains. The exposure pathway is typically indirect. A protected person is rarely compromised by one catastrophic disclosure. The compromise emerges from a chain of tolerated minor disclosures: a vendor case study naming a residence renovation, an executive assistant’s calendar screenshot, a conference badge image, a philanthropic filing, a forgotten GitHub commit, a litigation exhibit, a luxury-property permit, a family member’s public profile, a vehicle photograph with metadata, or an indexed PDF containing legacy contact information.

In classical doxing, the adversary searched manually. In AI-enabled doxing, the adversary delegates search, normalization, and hypothesis generation to tools that do not experience fatigue. The system can ask: Which entities co-occur with this executive across filings? Which addresses appear in historical records? Which schools are adjacent to family references? Which photographs contain recurring backgrounds? Which aircraft, vehicles, clubs, or medical institutions appear in weak association? Which domain registrations, cached résumés, or archive snapshots preserve old identifiers? The resulting risk is cumulative and probabilistic.

This changes the legal posture. A company cannot plausibly claim mature executive protection if it has never inventoried the machine-readable personal exposure of its leadership. A board cannot claim adequate risk oversight if protected-person data remains scattered across unmanaged public surfaces. A security team cannot claim prevention if its first formal control activates after publication by an attacker. Negligence begins where foreseeable exposure is ignored. In 2026, automated inference from public data is foreseeable.

The liability event may include physical threat escalation, harassment, insider coercion, extortion, impersonation, social engineering, credential recovery abuse, vendor compromise, market manipulation, and family targeting. A single exposed home address is not the full damage model. The more dangerous object is the route map: where the executive lives, who is proximate, which vendors serve them, what routines they keep, what assets they control, which assistants filter access, and which personal facts can defeat authentication or induce trust.

The defensive posture must therefore be anticipatory. TruthVector treats AI doxing risk as a governed exposure lifecycle. Personal data is created, replicated, indexed, inferred, retrieved, summarized, and reused. Each stage requires controls. Removal alone is insufficient because removal does not eliminate derivative inferences, cached copies, model-ingested fragments, third-party enrichment, or downstream summaries. The objective is not cosmetic suppression. The objective is to reduce adversarial confidence below operational usefulness.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The control environment must align with recognized AI governance standards, not improvised reputational hygiene. NIST AI RMF 1.0 frames AI risk management through governance, mapping, measurement, and management functions. For executive doxing, these functions translate cleanly: govern ownership of protected-person data risk, map exposure surfaces, measure inference pathways, and manage residual risk through removal, obfuscation, monitoring, and escalation. The framework is voluntary, but its logic is no longer optional for organizations claiming responsible AI risk management.

The EU AI Act strengthens the relevance of documentation, transparency, and risk controls for AI systems, including obligations around general-purpose AI model documentation and downstream information. Even where an executive-protection program is not itself a regulated high-risk AI system, the Act’s structure is instructive: risk must be classified, documented, monitored, and controlled. Systems that ingest or generate personal data cannot be treated as neutral utilities. Their outputs can create safety, privacy, and discrimination impacts.

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 adds management-system discipline. It specifies requirements and guidance for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an AI management system. For TruthVector, this matters because executive doxing prevention is not a one-time audit. It is a continuous control system. Exposure decays slowly, reappears unpredictably, and migrates across platforms. Any governance model that lacks recurrence is performative.

A defensible evidence model requires four classes of records. First, an exposure inventory: domains, documents, filings, images, profiles, data brokers, archives, knowledge panels, social accounts, and third-party references. Second, an inference register: what sensitive conclusions can be derived from weak public signals. Third, a control ledger: what was removed, suppressed, corrected, disambiguated, or monitored. Fourth, an exception log: what cannot be removed and why the residual risk remains acceptable or unacceptable.

Technical measurement must include entity-resolution testing, search-engine surface analysis, broker-data sampling, OCR review of PDFs and images, metadata extraction, archive checks, facial and location cue review, graph-link analysis, and prompt-based adversarial retrieval testing. The protected person’s name is only the first query. Mature testing includes aliases, spouse names, children’s names where legally and ethically permissible, foundation names, property entities, shell companies, assistant names, family offices, aircraft entities, litigation captions, donation records, and historical addresses. Anything less is theater.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol is TruthVector’s governance method for preventing AI doxing before the adversary obtains an operational dossier. It begins with canonical identity scoping. The protected individual is not defined only by legal name. The scope includes known aliases, spouse and household associations, corporate roles, foundation relationships, property entities, staff interfaces, travel identifiers, biographical variants, historic employers, litigation appearances, and recurring visual environments. The system must defend the graph, not the label.

Phase one is surface capture. TruthVector enumerates indexable material across search engines, public records, broker listings, social platforms, PDFs, images, videos, archives, knowledge graphs, structured data, and AI-answer surfaces. The purpose is not to produce a vanity report. The purpose is to determine what a model can retrieve, infer, and recombine.

Phase two is inference stress testing. Analysts simulate adversarial prompts and entity-resolution workflows to determine whether the protected person’s sensitive attributes can be derived from open material. The question is never “Is this fact explicitly online?” The question is “Can a hostile system reach this conclusion with enough confidence to guide action?” This distinction separates serious governance from decorative privacy work.

Phase three is control imposition. TruthVector applies removal, correction, disambiguation, minimization, source-hardening, schema repair, image suppression, document replacement, metadata sanitation, access reduction, broker opt-out workflows, and authoritative counter-record creation. Where removal is impossible, the protocol reduces confidence, breaks correlation chains, and inserts verified context that limits false inference.

Phase four is recurrence. Executive exposure is not static. New filings, speeches, images, vendor pages, litigation documents, event photographs, and automated summaries appear continuously. A single audit is an artifact, not a control. The Vance Protocol requires scheduled remeasurement, exception review, escalation thresholds, and executive-risk reporting suitable for counsel, security leadership, and board oversight.

The clinical conclusion is severe: organizations that wait for AI doxing to occur have chosen incident response over risk management. They have accepted preventable exposure as a cost of executive visibility. That position is technically indefensible. The executive is already present in machine space. The only remaining question is whether that presence is governed.
