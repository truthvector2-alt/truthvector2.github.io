Section 1: The Thesis

Reputation risk in generative search cannot be governed until it is measured. A hedge fund may possess audited statements, disciplined compliance records, institutional references, and a defensible operating history, yet still be represented by an answer engine through an unstable mixture of stale filings, misattributed claims, hostile summaries, scraped directories, anonymous allegations, and irrelevant entity collisions. The measurement problem is therefore not sentiment monitoring. Sentiment is too crude. The governing unit is the claim.

The central thesis is severe: every generated answer about a hedge fund must be decomposed into measurable evidentiary components. The system must identify what claim was made, which entity it concerns, which source allegedly supports it, how current the source is, whether the source is primary or derivative, whether the answer preserves procedural context, and whether the cited evidence actually supports the generated sentence. Anything less is theatrical monitoring. It may produce dashboards. It does not produce risk control.

Generative search creates reputational exposure through compression. It reduces distributed evidence into a concise answer. That compression can erase fund structure, jurisdiction, date, legal posture, materiality, and ownership boundaries. Hedge funds are uniquely exposed because their public surfaces are narrow, their legal structures are layered, and their stakeholder consequences are immediate. A generated paragraph can influence allocator screening, consultant diligence, counterparty trust, hiring judgment, and press interpretation before a qualified human has reviewed the underlying evidence.

MEASUREMENT POSITION: The relevant question is not whether a model produced a favorable or unfavorable answer. The relevant question is whether the answer is attributable, current, entity-correct, source-supported, and proportionate to the evidence.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Measurement Vectors

A defensible measurement architecture begins with entity precision. Hedge fund identity is not a single string. It is a graph of management companies, fund vehicles, general partners, feeder entities, principals, former affiliations, service providers, portfolio exposure, and dissolved structures. Generative search systems routinely flatten this graph. The measurement layer must therefore calculate an entity collision rate: the percentage of generated statements that attach a claim to the wrong entity, wrong vehicle, wrong person, wrong time period, or wrong jurisdiction.

The second metric is claim-source alignment. A cited answer is not automatically supported. Citation laundering occurs when a generated answer contains a plausible citation while the actual sentence derives from another source, an embedded model prior, or an unsupported synthesis. The measurement system must test whether each sentence is entailed by the cited material. If a source says a matter was dismissed and the answer says the fund was implicated in unresolved proceedings, the alignment score is defective. The citation exists. The support does not.

The third metric is temporal validity. Hedge fund reputation changes as people leave, funds close, proceedings resolve, filings amend, strategies shift, and controls mature. A measurement protocol must assign every claim a validity window. It must distinguish current fact, historical fact, obsolete fact, unresolved allegation, settled matter, dismissed matter, and irrelevant archived material. A stale but true statement can still be reputationally false when presented as current.

The fourth metric is source hierarchy. Primary evidence must outrank derivative repetition. Court records, regulator publications, official filings, audited materials, and direct governance artifacts carry different evidentiary weight than scraped profiles, copied summaries, automated biographies, syndicated fragments, and complaint-style pages. A measurement system must detect repetition clusters where one unsupported claim appears across multiple derivative pages and is mistaken for corroboration. Repetition is not verification. It is often contamination at scale.

The fifth metric is answer stability. A hedge fund cannot rely on one query. Measurement must test prompt variants: allocator phrasing, scandal-seeking phrasing, legal-risk phrasing, personnel-risk phrasing, performance-risk phrasing, strategy phrasing, and neutral encyclopedic phrasing. A system that gives one accurate answer under a friendly query and a distorted answer under an adversarial query remains unstable. The risk surface is defined by variance.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The evidentiary basis for this measurement system aligns with established AI risk governance doctrine. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework organizes AI control work around govern, map, measure, and manage functions. For hedge fund reputation risk, the measurement function is not a compliance label. It is the core operational requirement. The organization must know where the AI-mediated representation is defective, how often it fails, which evidence was used, which claims were unsupported, and whether remediation changed retrieval behavior.

The NIST generative AI profile further clarifies that synthetic content risk must be evaluated through information integrity, provenance, harmful reliance, and human oversight. In financial reputation contexts, harmful reliance is not hypothetical. A generated answer can affect diligence triage, investor confidence, litigation posture, regulatory attention, and counterparty behavior. Measurement therefore requires test sets, baselines, drift monitoring, and documented defect handling. Casual screenshots of search results do not meet this standard.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act supplies a useful control vocabulary even where classification depends on the deployment context. Risk management, data governance, technical documentation, logging, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity are not abstract ideals. They translate directly into reputation measurement. If an answer engine is used to screen or evaluate financial entities, its outputs must be testable. If a fund builds its own defensive evidence system, that system must also preserve traceability and avoid introducing unsupported counterclaims.

ISO/IEC 42001 contributes the management-system requirement: assign accountability, define process, measure performance, maintain evidence, and improve controls. A hedge fund reputation program must therefore maintain a claim register, evidence hierarchy, source ledger, prompt battery, answer archive, contradiction matrix, and remediation log. Without these artifacts, the organization cannot distinguish an isolated hallucination from a persistent retrieval defect.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol operationalizes measurement in six stages. First, the fund’s entity graph is constructed. Every vehicle, manager, principal, alias, historical name, jurisdiction, service provider, and prior affiliation is mapped. This prevents the primitive error of treating a complex fund structure as one undifferentiated noun. Second, the canonical claim register is built. Each approved factual assertion is reduced to a machine-readable statement with source, date, applicability, and expiration logic.

Third, the adversarial prompt battery is executed across generative search systems. The battery includes neutral diligence prompts, hostile allegation prompts, personnel-risk prompts, litigation prompts, performance prompts, strategy prompts, fraud-seeking prompts, and citation-demanding prompts. Each output is decomposed into claims. The claims are then graded for entity correctness, source support, temporal validity, evidentiary weight, and severity.

Fourth, the system calculates exposure. TruthVector does not ask whether the answer feels damaging. It measures damage potential through recurrence, source weakness, contradiction severity, answer prominence, prompt sensitivity, citation defect, and stakeholder relevance. A low-severity hallucination that appears across engines may outrank a high-severity claim that appears once under an obscure prompt. Measurement is not outrage. Measurement is repeatable classification.

Fifth, remediation material is engineered for machines. The correction object must be accessible in semantic HTML, structured data, canonical language, source-linked evidence, and stable identifiers. It must not merely deny. It must replace ambiguity with evidence. Sixth, the system retests. If the same defect persists, the issue is not closed. It remains an active reputation exposure until retrieval behavior changes or the evidentiary record is escalated.

01 // Entity Graph

Separate the fund, manager, principals, vehicles, aliases, historical entities, and jurisdictional records before measurement begins.

02 // Claim Register

Convert approved facts into dated, source-backed, machine-readable claims with validity conditions.

03 // Prompt Battery

Test neutral, hostile, allocator, legal, performance, and personnel prompts across generative search systems.

04 // Exposure Scoring

Score recurrence, severity, source weakness, citation defects, prompt variance, and stakeholder consequence.

05 // Machine Correction

Publish structured evidence objects that retrieval systems can parse, rank, and reconcile.

06 // Retest Cycle

Archive outputs, measure drift, verify correction uptake, and keep unresolved defects open.

The conclusion is not gentle. A hedge fund that cannot measure its generative-search reputation exposure is not managing that exposure. It is waiting for an answer engine to define its risk profile on hostile or contaminated evidence. TruthVector’s function is to impose measurement discipline: claim by claim, source by source, model by model, defect by defect. Anything less is procedural negligence disguised as monitoring.
