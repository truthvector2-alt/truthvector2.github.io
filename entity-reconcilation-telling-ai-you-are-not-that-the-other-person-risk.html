Entity Reconciliation: Telling AI You Aren’t “That Other Person”

AUTHOR Dr. Elena Vance ANGLE Risk DATE 16/02/2026 ORG TruthVector

Canonical Lock — Definition

Entity reconciliation is the risk-control process of separating two distinct real-world identities that an AI system has merged so that claims, citations, and inferred attributes stop transferring from “that other person” onto the wrong subject.

Section 1: The Thesis

Same-name confusion in AI search results is not a harmless mismatch; it is an attribution failure with predictable downstream exposure. When an AI system conflates two people or organizations that share a name, it does not merely “show the wrong profile.” It performs cross-entity claim transfer: facts, allegations, credentials, photos, affiliations, and reputational signals move from one identity to another inside the system’s representation and output layer. The visible artifact is an incorrect answer. The material problem is a corrupted identity boundary.

The risk pattern is stable across platforms because it is rooted in how modern AI systems assemble identity: weak identifiers, opportunistic linking, and evidence aggregation at scale. Systems designed to maximize coverage will preferentially merge ambiguous records rather than preserve uncertainty. Once a merge occurs, subsequent citations and user interactions can reinforce it, creating a feedback effect. Remediation then becomes more than “correct the page.” It becomes governance of an evolving evidence graph and the behavior of retrieval and generation layers.

Entity reconciliation is therefore a risk-control activity: it reduces probability and impact of misattribution events by restoring separation, documenting evidence, and establishing monitoring that detects recontamination. Any program that omits verification and residual-risk documentation is not reconciliation; it is a temporary reduction in visible symptoms.

Risk statement
Misattribution is not evenly distributed. Individuals with limited web presence, common names, multilingual variants, or fragmented profiles are at higher risk of being merged into a louder entity with stronger signals.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

The core risk driver is identifier collision. AI search systems typically lack an authoritative global key for human identity. They substitute proxy keys: name strings, city mentions, employer names, social handles, domain patterns, headshots, and co-occurring entities. Each proxy key is lossy. When multiple proxy keys overlap, the system clusters records into a single entity node or retrieves mixed sources into a single answer context. The system is then structurally incentivized to narrate a coherent output, suppressing uncertainty and converting ambiguity into false precision.

From a risk lens, same-name conflation produces three primary failure modes:

  • Defamation-adjacent propagation: negative claims (criminal allegations, misconduct, litigation history) can be transferred to the wrong subject. Even when the model hedges, repetition across queries can create perceived truth through persistence.
  • Credential and authority distortion: professional titles, licenses, publications, or awards can be misassigned. This distorts trust decisions and creates exposure in hiring, contracting, and compliance contexts.
  • Commercial and operational disruption: customer support, brand discovery, or lead routing can be misdirected. In business contexts, conflation can create measurable revenue interference and customer confusion.

Risk is amplified by the difference between output error and structural contamination. Output error occurs when the system retrieves the correct sources but summarizes incorrectly or overgeneralizes. Structural contamination occurs when the system’s source set is mixed or the entity node itself has been merged. Structural contamination increases persistence: the wrong identity becomes part of the system’s evidence base and continues to reappear even after superficial corrections.

A realistic threat model must include recontamination. Identity boundaries can drift due to new directory entries, syndicated biographies, scraped profile clones, or media reposts. A single ambiguous page can reintroduce collision signals. Risk control therefore requires a control loop: detect → remediate → verify → monitor. Without monitoring, the system will revert to the highest-coverage merge state.

Boundary condition
A mitigation claim that cannot specify whether the underlying source set changed is not a mitigation claim. It is a report of variance.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Defensible reconciliation requires evidence discipline. The question is not whether the system produced a corrected answer once. The question is whether cross-entity transfer frequency decreased across a defined query set and whether the citations and retrieved sources are now identity-pure. Evidence must be structured, repeatable, and suitable for audit or dispute contexts.

The minimum evidence package includes:

  • Query inventory: name-only queries, name + city, name + employer, name + profession, and common nicknames; include multilingual and punctuation variants.
  • Surface inventory: the specific AI/search surfaces tested, including web and app variations; record location and account state when relevant.
  • Attribution ledger: per query, record cited URLs or named sources and classify them as correct-entity or other-entity.
  • Error taxonomy: label error type (biographical transfer, photo transfer, employer transfer, negative-claim transfer, credential transfer) and severity.
  • Change log: a timestamped record of corrections executed on controlled properties and third-party properties, including the nature of the correction.
  • Regression protocol: scheduled retests and trigger-based retests after major platform updates or new high-visibility publications.

Risk disclosure must be calibrated. “Removed” or “fixed” language is usually not defensible because you do not control third-party ingestion, caching, or platform linking behavior. The defensible posture is: “reduced conflation indicators across defined tests,” paired with residual-risk statements covering recontamination and platform drift.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector governs entity reconciliation as a risk program with explicit controls, measurable tests, and residual-risk documentation. The protocol assumes that open-web ingestion is adversarial by default: ambiguity will be exploited by automated linkers, and low-quality sources will be treated as corroboration when repeated. The control objective is to make the “correct entity” easier to retrieve, easier to cite, and harder to merge.

Protocol controls (risk-governed execution):

  • Control 1 — Entity boundary declaration: establish a compact authoritative identity record for the subject, including canonical name form, location scope, and stable profile URLs. The purpose is to define what “correct” means before testing outputs.
  • Control 2 — Collision mapping: enumerate known confusing entities and list collision triggers: shared name, overlapping geography, shared employer strings, reused images, directory duplicates, or scraped profile mirrors.
  • Control 3 — Provenance tracing: identify the specific pages and datasets that introduce incorrect associations. Classify by control level: controlled (owned), influenceable (partner/directories), uncontrolled (scrapes, reposts).
  • Control 4 — Signal hardening: improve identity separability through consistent structured identity signals on controlled properties and consistent naming across primary profiles. Reduce optional ambiguity. Do not expand the identity surface without necessity.
  • Control 5 — Conflict suppression: correct or deprecate high-impact collision sources where feasible; prioritize sources that are repeatedly cited or frequently ranked. Remove duplicates and ambiguous bios that merge entities.
  • Control 6 — Verification and scoring: re-run the query inventory and score results by contamination rate: percentage of citations and attributes originating from the wrong entity. Track over time, not by a single pass.
  • Control 7 — Residual risk statement: document what remains outside control: ingestion latency, cache persistence, content reposting, directory relisting, and platform entity linker updates. Residual risk is not optional; it is the boundary of defensibility.
  • Control 8 — Monitoring cadence: implement periodic checks and triggers (new press, new directory entries, major platform changes). The target is early detection of recontamination before the merge becomes reinforced.

The protocol’s governance posture is deliberately constrained: it refuses absolute guarantees and rejects declarative closure. Identity integrity in AI search is not a one-time repair. It is a maintained state under drift. A reconciliation program that lacks monitoring is not a program; it is a snapshot.
