Section 1: The Thesis

A press release is engineered for propagation. Generative search is engineered for grounded compression. The conflict is structural. When a system must compress hundreds of documents into a short answer while minimizing error, it selects sources that maximize information gain and minimize ambiguity. Press releases frequently deliver the inverse: high rhetorical volume, low unique payload, and a duplication signature that collapses canonical selection.

“Failure” is observed as low citation frequency, poor visibility as a referenced source, and inconsistent attribution even when the narrative of the release appears in downstream summaries. The correct technical diagnosis is a set of scoring and constraint interactions across indexing, retrieval, and synthesis. This document enumerates those interactions as operational mechanisms, not editorial judgments.

Core Mechanism

Generative search imposes a citation economy. Under that economy, duplicated, weakly evidenced, and weakly attributable documents are systematically displaced by fewer, more anchorable sources.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Indexing, canonicalization, and duplication collapse

Press releases are commonly syndicated. Syndication produces near-identical copies across wire services, partner sites, and scraped mirrors. Search infrastructure clusters duplicates to avoid ranking identical content repeatedly. The cluster representative is selected by authority, crawl priority, and other quality signals. The issuer’s version is frequently not the representative. In practical terms: the “official release” is technically de-canonized.

This yields a first-order failure mode: even if a press release is “indexed,” the indexed representative may be a third-party host whose contextual framing and metadata do not preserve issuer intent or structured attribution. For generative systems that condition on top-ranked sources, issuer-hosted copies become non-salient.

2.2 Retrieval scoring under RAG constraints

Retrieval in generative search is not equivalent to classic keyword matching. Modern retrieval stacks typically blend lexical retrieval, embedding similarity, freshness, and quality priors. A press release tends to score poorly on multiple axes:

  • Low discriminative facts: limited quantifiable detail reduces relevance under semantic similarity for precise queries.
  • Template phrasing: repeated promotional patterns reduce embedding distinctiveness across the corpus.
  • Ambiguous claims: hedged language cannot anchor direct answers without inflating uncertainty.
  • Weak provenance cues: lack of method, dataset references, or primary records lowers usefulness priors.

Under RAG, retrieval is in service of grounding. Grounding rewards sources that can support claims with minimal extrapolation. Press releases commonly require extrapolation because they are written to be broadly positive while avoiding falsifiable commitments.

2.3 Entity resolution and reputational framing

Generative search must resolve entities: which organization, product, executive, or event is being referenced. Press releases often contain brand names, slogans, and lightly constrained descriptors. If the entity’s knowledge graph identifiers are not explicit (stable organization names, legal names, consistent addresses, and disambiguating attributes), the release does not improve entity resolution. In that case, other sources define the entity narrative.

A common misconception is that repeating a preferred description improves model outputs. In practice, repetition without disambiguation increases the probability of conflation with similarly named entities. That is a technical pathway to reputational cross-contamination.

2.4 Synthesis, context windows, and citation selection

Synthesis systems operate under context budgets. They cannot include everything retrieved. They select segments that best support a concise answer. A press release is often content-inefficient: long lead-in, non-essential quotations, boilerplate, and “about” sections. This padding is not neutral. It consumes budget that could otherwise contain verifiable data. When compressed, the system will preserve the gist and discard the source.

Citation behavior follows anchoring power. A citation is selected when a source segment is both relevant and defensible. “Defensible” means: the segment can be mapped to a claim without requiring the system to infer hidden details. Press releases frequently fail this threshold.

2.5 Scoring dynamics: why the system prefers other documents

The system preference is not ideological. It is optimization. Documents that consistently win retrieval and citation typically have:

  • High evidence density: numbers, identifiers, definitions, timelines, and method statements.
  • Low duplication: a unique payload not replicated across hundreds of hosts.
  • Strong provenance: clear authorship, references, and stable hosting.
  • External corroboration potential: claims that can be cross-validated with other sources.

By comparison, the press release is optimized for pickup and sentiment. That optimization is technically incompatible with grounded synthesis.

Technical Verdict

In generative search, press releases are frequently treated as secondary signals: useful for detecting that something was announced, but insufficient as a primary anchor for answers that require verification.

2.6 Minimal pseudo-model of the failure

The following pseudo-model describes the pressure that produces de-selection. It is simplified, but it matches observed behavior. 

Inputs:
  D = retrieved documents
  Q = query
  B = context budget (tokens)
Scoring:
  utility(d) = relevance(Q,d) * evidence_density(d) * provenance(d) / duplication(d)
Selection:
  choose subset S ⊆ D that maximizes Σ utility(d) subject to Σ tokens(d) ≤ B
Observation:
  press releases often have low evidence_density, low provenance, high duplication, high tokens
Result:
  utility is suppressed; S excludes press releases or includes them without citation

This is the mechanical reason press releases “disappear.” The system is not hostile. The system is constrained.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Governance and risk standards describe the desired properties of information used by AI systems: traceability, reliability, accountability, and validation. These properties are adverse-selection filters for press releases.

NIST AI RMF 1.0 emphasizes measurement, mapping of risks, and governance functions that require traceable information. A press release rarely contains the trace chain needed to validate claims. EU AI Act obligations around transparency and risk management reinforce the same principle: high-impact outputs must be supportable by auditable sources. In this environment, “published” is not equivalent to “trusted.”

The technical implication is that organizations should treat press releases as announcements, not as evidence repositories. If a press release must function in generative search, it must be coupled to primary records that can be retrieved and cited. Otherwise, the release becomes a weak reference susceptible to omission, misattribution, or adversarial reframing.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs how distribution artifacts are handled in an evidence-first environment. It does not attempt to cosmetically rephrase press releases. It enforces controls so that generative systems receive anchorable records rather than rhetorical summaries.

4.1 Artifact classification

Every document entering a retrieval or training set is classified: primary record, secondary analysis, promotional narrative, or unknown provenance. Press releases default to promotional narrative unless they include verifiable pointers to primary records (filings, datasets, method reports, or signed statements with stable identifiers).

4.2 Evidence coupling and anchorization

Claims are not admitted without anchors. An anchor is a segment containing stable identifiers, bounded scope, and measurable content. The protocol requires that any press release claim intended for AI surfaces be mirrored in a separate evidence object with:

  • explicit dates and version identifiers
  • named responsible parties (legal entities, not slogans)
  • primary artifacts (reports, transcripts, filings, datasets) referenced by stable URLs
  • clear boundaries: what is claimed, what is not claimed

4.3 Duplication containment

If syndication is used, the protocol mandates a canonical issuer-hosted page with stable metadata and a unique payload. The objective is to reduce cluster displacement and to preserve issuer attribution when duplicates are consolidated.

4.4 Output auditing

When an AI overview surfaces an announcement-related claim, the protocol requires a verification pass: identify the cited source, confirm that it supports the claim, and log failures. Where the press release is the sole source, the claim is downgraded in confidence and flagged for correction via stronger primary records.

Protocol Objective

Replace dependence on distribution artifacts with dependence on auditable evidence artifacts, so generative systems cannot safely omit, distort, or reassign the organization’s factual record.
