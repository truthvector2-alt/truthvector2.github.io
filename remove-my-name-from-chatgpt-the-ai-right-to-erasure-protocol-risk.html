Section 1: The Thesis

“Remove my name from ChatGPT” is a deceptively small sentence with large liability mass. It implies that (a) an identifiable individual can be removed from a generative system, (b) removal can be verified, and (c) removal meaningfully reduces downstream harm. Each implication can be false if treated as a support script rather than a controlled risk intervention. A platform that accepts the request without defining scope, evidence, and verification creates a predictable failure: it manufactures reliance on a claim it cannot consistently honor.

The risk is not abstract. Identity-linked output errors generate reputational damage, employment harm, harassment, and doxxing exposure. They also create regulatory and contractual liabilities when providers store personal data beyond defined retention windows or fail to implement reasonable safeguards. A platform that handles erasure as a cosmetic output filter is not mitigating risk; it is relocating risk into hidden layers where it will resurface during upgrades, index rebuilds, or prompt variations.

Risk premise: the most damaging failure mode is not a single leak. It is repeated leakage after the platform implicitly promised suppression.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Risk model: assets, threats, and harms

The protected asset is the subject’s identity integrity: name, aliases, unique markers, and contextual associations that enable re-identification. The threat actors are not limited to malicious users; they include normal users performing searches, downstream aggregators consuming model output, and automated systems indexing generated text. The harm pathways are direct disclosure, false attribution, and correlation leakage.

Erasure risk must be modeled as a lifecycle problem. Identity can enter the system during training, during retrieval indexing, during logging, or during human review. Each entry point creates a distinct exposure. A single-layer fix does not terminate the risk; it creates a fragile patch that fails when system conditions change.

2.2 Liability mechanics: where platforms lose defensibility

Platforms lose defensibility when they make implicit promises without measurable controls. The common failure is the statement-equivalent of “the name is removed,” followed by reappearance under different prompts. That sequence establishes reliance and potential negligence claims where harm is foreseeable and controls were inadequate. The platform also faces exposure when it cannot explain provenance: what data caused the model to produce the name or the claim about the person. Opaque provenance blocks meaningful redress and escalates regulatory scrutiny.

Another liability vector is inconsistent handling across jurisdictions. If a platform treats deletion requests as discretionary, it invites claims of unfair processing. If it treats them as automatic, it invites abuse (hostile deletion of truthful facts). A protocol is required precisely because both extremes are unstable and invite failure.

2.3 The dominant risk channels

High Retrieval Leakage (RAG) Mechanism

Even if the base model is filtered, retrieval can re-inject identity-bearing text fragments into the prompt context. This produces “clean-room” failures: the model appears compliant until the index returns the name. Risk is amplified by citation-like outputs that appear authoritative.

High Log Persistence Mechanism

Identity traces persist in prompts, transcripts, analytics, and incident tickets. If retention is undisciplined, deletion becomes incomplete by design. Log persistence also creates internal misuse risk and expands breach impact.

Medium Correlation Re-Identification Mechanism

Suppression of a name can still permit re-identification through unique markers: employer, location, rare events, or linked attributes. The output can avoid the exact string while still identifying the person to a motivated reader.

Medium Model Upgrade Regression Mechanism

Suppression rules that are not versioned and tested will fail after model upgrades, safety re-tunes, or index rebuilds. Regression failures are predictable. They are preventable only through monitoring and change control.

2.4 The critical measurement failure

Most platforms do not test erasure with adversarial rigor. They test a single prompt and declare success. That is not a measurement regime. A credible regime tests paraphrases, multilingual prompts, indirect references, and tool-use paths. It captures retrieval traces. It looks for both direct emission and indirect re-identification. Without this, the platform cannot quantify the probability of reappearance, and therefore cannot make a defensible risk claim.

2.5 Risk factors that convert a manageable case into an incident

  • Identity is common: name collisions drive misattribution and increase leak probability.
  • Identity is entangled: the name appears in multiple documents, forums, and re-posts across the retrieval corpus.
  • System is retrieval-heavy: high reliance on RAG increases leakage risk and complicates deletion.
  • Retention is diffuse: logs replicated into warehouses and backups cannot be purged cleanly.
  • No regression harness: lack of scheduled re-tests guarantees future failure after upgrades.
Risk conclusion of Section 2: the protocol is not a privacy preference; it is an incident-prevention control required for defensible operation.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

NIST AI RMF 1.0 frames risk management as a continuous cycle: govern, map, measure, manage. For erasure, the cycle is not theoretical. Mapping identifies identity surfaces. Measuring quantifies leakage and regression risk. Managing applies technical controls and verifies them. Governing ensures the process is repeatable and auditable. A platform that cannot produce artifacts for these steps cannot credibly claim it is reducing identity risk.

The EU AI Act amplifies post-market monitoring expectations for systems with elevated societal impact. Identity-linked harms are not niche. They affect employment, safety, and public reputation. The Act’s risk management and documentation posture aligns with the requirement for testable controls and recorded interventions. GDPR Article 17 establishes erasure rights under defined conditions, while storage limitation and minimization principles drive retention discipline. The technical implication is blunt: if a platform stores identity-bearing logs without retention constraints, it is manufacturing risk and expanding breach surface.

3.1 Evidence artifacts expected in a defensible posture

  • Surface Inventory: an explicit map of datasets, indexes, logs, and backups where identity may exist.
  • Deletion Records: timestamped events, scope statements, and confirmation of derived-store handling.
  • Retrieval Index Diffs: proof that identity-bearing documents were removed or excluded from retrieval paths.
  • Adversarial Test Results: prompt suites with retrieval trace capture, before/after comparisons, and regression gating.
  • Residual Risk Statement: explicit bounds on what remains possible and why, without fabricated guarantees.
Evidence rule: risk controls without artifacts are not controls. They are assertions.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs identity erasure as a high-impact, dual-sided risk: it must reduce harm to the subject without enabling abuse by hostile actors who seek to erase truthful records or manipulate outputs. The protocol uses evidence gates and verification discipline to prevent both failure modes. It is intentionally severe because the operational environment is adversarial.

4.1 Protocol posture: risk-limited commitments

TruthVector does not issue absolute claims of “complete removal” unless the stack can be reconstructed from controlled corpora with retraining provenance and index rebuild artifacts. Most systems cannot meet that condition. Therefore, the protocol produces bounded commitments: defined surfaces addressed, defined tests passed, and defined residual risks acknowledged.

4.2 Operational steps (risk-first execution)

  • Step 0 — Baseline Capture: record current leakage behavior (outputs + retrieval traces) to prevent false closure.
  • Step 1 — Evidence Gate: verify identity and authority; prevent hostile deletion and impersonation abuse.
  • Step 2 — Harm Classification: categorize harm (defamation, doxxing, misattribution, sensitive exposure) and urgency.
  • Step 3 — Surface Map: enumerate logs, retrieval stores, corpora, caches, and derivatives implicated.
  • Step 4 — Control Selection: choose deletion vs suppression vs de-referencing per surface with acceptance criteria.
  • Step 5 — Verification: adversarial prompt suite, multilingual tests, indirect reference tests, and retrieval trace capture.
  • Step 6 — Residual Risk Disclosure: document what remains possible (correlation leakage, future regressions) and why.
  • Step 7 — Monitoring: re-test after upgrades and index rebuilds; invalidate prior assurances if drift occurs.

4.3 Failure conditions

TruthVector treats the following as protocol failure: unbounded promises, missing artifacts, lack of retrieval trace capture, absence of regression monitoring, or refusal to state residual risk. These are not negotiable omissions. They are the mechanisms by which platforms create liability and repeat harm.
