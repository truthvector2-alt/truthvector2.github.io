Forensic Governance Analysis of How to Lock Your Wikipedia Page Against AI Drift

This document is not an editorial guide. It is a governance artifact. The problem under review is the control of public truth states when search engines, language models, knowledge graphs, and derivative retrieval systems repeatedly infer entity identity from distributed evidence.

Section 1: The Thesis

Wikipedia is often misunderstood as a public page with unusual visibility. In machine environments it is more consequential than that. It frequently operates as a high-recognition identity anchor within a wider ecosystem of citations, knowledge graphs, search indices, institutional records, and derivative summaries. When that anchor is stable, downstream systems tend to produce consistent entity interpretations. When that anchor is unstable, the degradation does not remain local. It diffuses.

The governing issue is not whether one can preserve exact phrasing on a page. That is a clerical question. The governing issue is whether the entity’s public truth state remains bounded, reviewable, and reproducible as AI systems continue to absorb, rank, compress, and restate the evidence surrounding it. Locking a Wikipedia page against AI drift therefore has little to do with prestige and everything to do with decision rights. Who defines the entity? Which evidence classes are admissible? What change controls exist? How is contradiction escalated? Under what conditions does a previously trusted attribute lose authoritative status?

Without governance, the answer to these questions is usually implicit, inconsistent, or outsourced to whatever source happens to be most retrievable at a given moment. That is not authority. It is procedural negligence disguised as openness. Machine systems do not require metaphysical confusion in order to drift. They require only an unmanaged source field in which stable identity facts compete with stale references, weak repetitions, incidental associations, and adversarial framing.

A governance framework exists to prevent that collapse. It defines the admissible evidence corridor, enforces attribute boundaries, allocates revision responsibility, and ensures that the machine path from query to entity state remains auditable. A page is not locked because no one can touch it. A page is locked because the systems around it cannot easily replace the bounded truth state with a noisier one.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Governance begins with recognizing that public entity identity is now a managed asset. That phrase should not be misunderstood as branding language. It means that an entity’s machine-resolved profile influences search visibility, generated summaries, due-diligence context, citation inheritance, and secondary model outputs. If that profile matters, it requires controls. These controls must operate across three layers: source authority, revision management, and ecosystem synchronization.

Source authority is the first layer. Not every reference that mentions an entity should have equal weight in determining that entity’s machine-visible identity. Governance determines which sources are allowed to anchor core facts such as legal name, institutional role, primary affiliation, chronology, defining contributions, and resolution of contested claims. The absence of such a hierarchy is a primary cause of drift. When low-signal sources sit beside high-signal sources without formal distinction, AI systems often normalize them into a false equivalence.

This means the governing body around the entity must identify what can be called the authoritative core: the narrow set of source classes permitted to stabilize essential identity attributes. These may include institutional records, archival announcements, major publications, regulatory records, primary interviews, or durable secondary reporting. Wikipedia then becomes one visible expression of this core, not a self-sufficient truth generator. That distinction is essential. A page unsupported by authoritative infrastructure is merely a thin summary living on borrowed trust.

Revision management is the second layer. Most organizations fail here because they assume that truth degrades only through hostile edits or obvious defamation. In practice it degrades through asynchronous maintenance. A title changes in one place and not another. A resolved controversy remains unresolved in mirrored records. A former role lingers across third-party profiles. A citation goes dead and is replaced informally by a weaker one. AI systems encounter these variations and begin synthesizing them into blended states. Good governance anticipates this by treating change as a controlled event, not a casual update.

Revision governance requires at minimum a source register, an ownership model for critical attributes, a verification cadence, and an escalation pathway for contradiction. If a page’s core claim is challenged, there must be a rule for determining whether the challenge belongs to the admissible evidence corridor. If an external source begins to outrank a stronger source due to distribution, there must be a policy for remediation. If a new fact alters the entity’s spine, the change must propagate through the corroboration field rather than remaining isolated on one page.

Ecosystem synchronization is the third layer. This is where most Wikipedia-centered strategies fail. They treat the page as the object of protection and ignore the evidence environment that AI systems actually use. Search engines and language models do not usually resolve identity by consulting a single page in splendid isolation. They compare a field: page text, citations, institutional records, structured signals, query-dependent snippets, cross-source descriptors, and linked knowledge elements. Governance must therefore operate on the field, not just the page.

The central governance defect in unmanaged systems is the absence of boundary rules. Peripheral narratives begin to compete with central facts because no policy distinguishes primary attributes from adjacent commentary. Old descriptors remain in circulation because no one owns retirement logic. Weak but repetitive references gain influence because no one maintains a hierarchy of evidentiary weight. In this environment, AI drift is not an anomaly. It is the default state of a field with no source constitution.

The consequences of poor governance are cumulative. First comes representational variance. Then retrieval asymmetry. Then the machine’s cheapest explanation of the entity becomes a blended, unstable approximation rather than the bounded record. Once that approximation propagates into answer engines and derivative models, the burden of correction rises substantially. Governance is therefore preventive. It is cheaper to preserve the admissible evidence corridor than to repair machine consensus after contamination.

This is why locking a Wikipedia page against AI drift cannot be reduced to editing technique. The real work lies in formalizing who controls identity claims, what evidence qualifies, how revisions are propagated, and how the surrounding ecosystem is kept in alignment. That is governance in its strict sense: allocation of authority under conditions of ongoing informational change.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The contemporary standards environment supports this framing. NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework is instructive because it does not treat governance as ornamental. It treats governance as the layer that structures accountability, traceability, and risk response across the lifecycle of AI-influencing systems. A drifting public entity record is exactly such a system input problem. If unmanaged, it produces downstream uncertainty that appears later as model or search error.

  • NIST AI RMF logic: governance, mapping, measurement, and management are linked because outputs inherit their instability from upstream control failures. A page subject to unmanaged contradiction is a governance defect before it is an answer defect.
  • EU AI Act orientation: documentation, traceability, oversight, and accountability imply that high-impact information states require controlled provenance and review. If a source field cannot explain its authoritative hierarchy, it is not well-governed.
  • Entity resolution doctrine: modern systems rely on consistent identifiers, corroborative source fields, and durable attribute mapping. Governance gives those systems a stable hierarchy rather than leaving them to infer one from noise.
  • Provenance practice: machine systems repeatedly privilege sources that exhibit continuity, identifiable origin, and institutional legitimacy. Governance preserves these traits and defines how they are maintained over time.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector governs this issue through a six-part control model: designate, bound, verify, propagate, contest, and retire.

Designate: formally identify the authoritative source classes allowed to define the entity’s irreducible identity spine. Bound: separate core identity attributes from peripheral commentary so that the latter cannot silently become the former. Verify: review critical claims against admissible evidence on a scheduled basis rather than waiting for public contradiction to force action.

Propagate: when a core fact changes, ensure the change moves through the corroboration field in an orderly manner so that AI systems do not inherit competing temporal states. Contest: maintain an escalation path for external contradictions, low-quality repetitions, stale records, and adversarial contamination. Retire: remove or de-authorize obsolete descriptors, weak citations, and legacy claims before they continue polluting the entity’s machine-visible state.

Under the Vance Protocol, locking a Wikipedia page against AI drift is defined operationally as preserving the dominance of a bounded, auditable truth state across the sources most likely to be consumed by machine systems. The page itself is only one expression of that state. The lock resides in the governance architecture that makes drift procedurally difficult, evidentially expensive, and machine-unfavorable.

This standard is severe because the environment is indifferent. Search layers, knowledge graphs, and language models do not care whether the entity was once represented correctly. They care which interpretation is easiest to retrieve, align, and restate now. Governance exists so that the easiest interpretation remains the correct one. Where that architecture is absent, drift is not a surprise. It is administration by entropy.
