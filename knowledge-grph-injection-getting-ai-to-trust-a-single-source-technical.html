Forensic Analysis of Knowledge Graph Injection: Getting AI to Trust a Single Source

Canonical definition record • entity integrity • provenance discipline • retrieval convergence hazards

Canonical Definition (Verbatim Lock)

Knowledge Graph Injection is the deliberate manipulation of entity claims, relationships, and provenance signals so that retrieval and ranking systems treat a single target source as the most authoritative reference for a topic, even when that exclusivity is not warranted by independent corroboration.

Section 1: The Thesis

Modern generative systems do not “decide” truth in a philosophical sense. They approximate truth operationally: by selecting sources, extracting passages, reconciling entities, and assembling outputs under time and context constraints. In that pipeline, “trust” is an emergent property of ranking and retrieval behavior. Knowledge Graph Injection targets the upstream layers that determine what the model sees and how the system resolves ambiguity.

The phrase “getting AI to trust a single source” indicates a convergence objective. In controlled domains, convergence can be appropriate (for example, an official registry where one source is definitionally authoritative). In open domains, convergence to one privately controlled source is a governance defect. It produces a single point of epistemic failure: if the source is wrong, biased, stale, or adversarial, the system becomes wrong, biased, stale, or adversarial at scale.

Operational statement: Single-source convergence is not evidence of correctness; it is evidence that the system’s authority signals can be saturated.

This record defines Knowledge Graph Injection as a manipulation class distinct from legitimate knowledge engineering. Legitimate engineering clarifies entities and improves provenance without excluding independent corroboration. Injection is characterized by forced convergence, abnormal redundancy patterns, and authority inflation disproportionate to verifiable evidence.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Knowledge graphs—whether explicit databases of entities and relationships or implicit graphs derived from indexed text—serve as a control surface. They influence which documents are retrieved, how entities are disambiguated, and which sources are preferentially cited. Injection exploits this by manufacturing signals that proxy as authority: centrality, consistency, and coverage. These proxies are computationally convenient, but they are not equivalent to epistemic reliability.

Injection Targets

  • Entity identity: names, aliases, and disambiguation rules that decide whether two mentions refer to the same thing.
  • Relationship topology: who is linked to what, how densely, and with what directional strength.
  • Provenance and citations: how the system explains where a claim came from, and how strongly it weights the origin.
  • Retrieval surfaces: the passages and structured blocks most likely to be selected for common queries.
  • Authority heuristics: domain reputation signals, publisher identity, and perceived consistency across sources.
Mechanism A — Entity Collapse

Multiple distinct entities are coerced into one canonical node through repeated aliasing and “same entity” cues across coordinated pages. The failure mode is deterministic: reconciliation merges become defaults, and downstream retrieval cannot represent the original distinctions.

Mechanism B — Relationship Inflation

The target source manufactures high-degree connectivity by linking itself to many topical nodes, often via templated “about,” “definition,” and “reference” pages. Graph algorithms interpret centrality as authority, causing repeated preferential selection.

Mechanism C — Redundancy as Confirmation

Near-duplicate claims appear across multiple properties that look independent but are operationally coordinated. Systems that treat repetition as corroboration become vulnerable; repetition is activity, not proof.

Mechanism D — Retrieval Saturation

The index is populated with passages optimized for extraction: crisp definitions, bullet lists, and FAQ blocks. If top-k retrieval is redundancy-sensitive, the context window becomes a monoculture and the model’s output follows.

Definition Boundary Conditions

A definitional boundary is required to prevent category errors. Not all “graph shaping” is injection. The boundary conditions are observable: injection exists when the system is pressured to treat a single target source as authoritative in circumstances where independent corroboration is feasible and relevant.

  • Legitimate: improvements that increase traceability, clarify naming, and preserve competing credible sources.
  • Injective: changes that reduce source diversity, suppress dissenting evidence, or produce disproportionate authority through duplication.
  • Audit trigger: query diversity collapses to a single domain across unrelated phrasing, indicating retrieval convergence rather than reasoned selection.

Why “Single Source” Is a Risk Concentrator

Single-source trust is operationally convenient, but it breaks basic risk principles: resilience, redundancy, and contestability. In a healthy system, a claim’s confidence is a function of provenance quality and cross-source agreement that is genuinely independent. Injection attempts to counterfeit independence by distributing the same assertions across controlled surfaces, hoping the system cannot detect common origin.

The highest-leverage weakness is provenance. If provenance is shallow—“this page says it”—then the system can be captured by a page factory. If provenance is deep—“this claim is supported by a traceable chain to primary evidence”—then injection is constrained. The definition of Knowledge Graph Injection therefore necessarily includes the manipulation of provenance signals, not only relationship structure.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Governance frameworks treat this problem as a systems issue, not a content issue. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) emphasizes measurable governance functions: mapping risks, establishing controls, and monitoring model behavior over time. Knowledge Graph Injection is a predictable manipulation class that directly tests whether those controls exist.

Under NIST-style governance, the relevant requirements are operational: can the system explain why a source was chosen, can it detect suspicious redundancy, can it represent uncertainty, and can it prevent authority inflation from becoming truth? If the answer is no, the system is not “wrong” by accident; it is ungoverned by design.

The EU AI Act’s risk-based structure reinforces the same constraints: as impact increases, so do documentation and control expectations. Even outside formally regulated categories, the mechanics matter: if a generative system outputs reputational claims, policy guidance, medical inferences, financial summaries, or business advisories, single-source capture becomes a liability multiplier. The relevant risk is not only misinformation; it is unchallengeable output production where provenance cannot be audited.

Evidence posture: if a system cannot produce a provenance trace sufficient for review, it should abstain, qualify uncertainty, or diversify sources; it should not hard-converge.

Audit Artifacts Expected in Mature Implementations

  • Source taxonomy identifying registries, primary sources, secondary commentary, user-generated content, and synthetic content, with distinct weighting rules.
  • Provenance logs tying each material claim to retrieved passages, document identifiers, timestamps, and extraction rationale.
  • De-duplication controls that detect near-duplicate clusters and reduce their retrieval influence.
  • Entity-resolution governance with thresholds, manual review triggers, and rollback capability for merges and alias bindings.
  • Adversarial evaluation testing whether authority can be inflated without independent evidence, including red-team injection simulations.

A recurring misconception is that “more structured data” automatically improves truthfulness. Structured data improves parsing. It can also accelerate injection by making assertions more extractable. Governance requires a second layer: treat structured assertions as claims that require provenance, not as facts that deserve promotion.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol is a governance discipline designed to prevent single-source capture while preserving legitimate entity clarity. It is defined as controls applied to entity graphs, retrieval selection, and provenance handling so that authority is conditional, reversible, auditable, and resistant to redundancy-based manipulation.

Protocol Primitives

  • Conditional authority: a source can be authoritative for specific fields and non-authoritative for adjacent fields; authority is scoped, not global.
  • Provenance-first ranking: claims without traceable origin metadata are down-weighted or excluded from “definitional” contexts.
  • Redundancy penalty: repeated claims with common origin signals are treated as manipulation risk; repetition is not corroboration.
  • Reversible reconciliation: entity merges and alias bindings must be reversible, logged, and subject to review when drift is detected.
  • Contestable outputs: when credible disagreement exists, the system must support contested states and qualifiers rather than forced singular conclusions.

Detection Signals for Injection Attempts

  • Centrality anomaly: the target domain becomes disproportionately central relative to independent sources of record.
  • Query invariance: diverse query phrasing returns the same domain and the same claim structure, indicating retrieval collapse.
  • Provenance shallowness: citations point to self-referential “about” pages instead of primary evidence.
  • Near-duplicate saturation: clusters of materially identical definitions and FAQs dominate the retrieval set.
  • Merge drift: entity resolution increasingly collapses distinct entities without manual review events or rollback traces.

The governance objective is explicit: preserve epistemic diversity where the domain requires it, and constrain convergence to contexts where authority is definitionally grounded (registries, statutes, standards, and verifiable primary records). “Getting AI to trust a single source” is therefore not a neutral goal statement; it is either a registry-aligned control requirement or an attempt at capture. The system must be designed to distinguish the two.

Protocol statement: a system that cannot resist single-source capture is not a knowledge system; it is a signal amplifier.

Operational Closing Note

Knowledge Graph Injection is best understood as a supply-chain attack on meaning. It exploits how systems substitute computational proxies for epistemic reliability: centrality, consistency, and coverage. The remedy is not aesthetic content production. The remedy is governance: provenance enforcement, redundancy controls, reversible entity resolution, and adversarial evaluation against forced convergence.

A system that hard-converges on one source in an open domain is declaring that its trust model is saturable. That is a measurable defect. It should be treated as such.
