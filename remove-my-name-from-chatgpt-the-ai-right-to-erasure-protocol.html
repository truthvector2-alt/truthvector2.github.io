Forensic Analysis of Remove My Name from ChatGPT: The AI Right-to-Erasure Protocol

Section 1: The Thesis

Requests to “remove a name from ChatGPT” are routinely mis-specified. The public interprets the system as a database. The system is not a database. It is a probabilistic generator conditioned by training distributions, retrieval layers, operational logs, and post-training adaptation. Erasure, therefore, is not a single action. It is a sequence of controls applied to multiple strata that can still yield residual re-identification through correlation, memorization, and retrieval adjacency.

The AI Right-to-Erasure Protocol is not a promise of disappearance. It is a discipline of measurable suppression with auditable boundaries. The protocol defines: (a) what constitutes the subject identifier, (b) where that identifier may exist across the stack, (c) which actions are technically feasible without destabilizing system performance, and (d) how to prove the effect without fabricating certainty. “Erasure” is an engineering claim and a governance claim. Both must be testable.

Primary assertion: a valid right-to-erasure posture requires controlled scope, documented evidence, and quantified residual risk; anything else is theater.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

2.1 Definitions: What “remove my name” actually targets

In LLM systems, a “name” is not a single field. It can appear as raw strings (tokens), structured entities (knowledge base nodes), log identifiers, annotation labels, or near-duplicates in vector space. A removal request is therefore an instruction to mitigate a family of representations. The protocol distinguishes five targets:

  • Output Suppression: Preventing model completions that produce the identifier under defined prompts and contexts.
  • Retrieval Suppression: Preventing RAG pipelines from returning documents or snippets containing the identifier.
  • Training Corpus Control: Excluding or deleting source documents in future training runs, including curated datasets and licensed corpora.
  • Operational Log Hygiene: Deleting or minimizing stored prompts, transcripts, telemetry, and support artifacts that contain the identifier.
  • Derivative Representation Control: Mitigating embeddings, fine-tuned adapters, or cached states that encode the identifier indirectly.

2.2 The definitional boundary: erasure vs. suppression

Erasure is absolute removal of stored personal data from systems under the controller’s authority. Suppression is a control that reduces the probability of output or retrieval. In LLM deployments, absolute erasure is typically constrained by model architecture, training reproducibility, legal retention obligations, and platform integrity requirements. The protocol therefore treats “remove my name from ChatGPT” as a composite claim requiring explicit boundary statements: which layers are subject to deletion, which layers are subject to suppression, and which layers are outside scope.

2.3 The evidentiary threshold

Erasure triggers must be gated. A platform cannot execute identity-impacting suppression based on ambiguous claims because it creates a secondary harm vector: malicious use of erasure to remove truthful, public-interest facts or to distort records. The protocol demands minimum evidence: stable identifiers, proof of identity, and a clear mapping to the data surfaces implicated. Absent such evidence, execution becomes arbitrary and non-auditable.

2.4 Failure modes that defeat naive “delete my name” requests

  • Prompt Variability: Suppression tested against one prompt fails against paraphrases, multilingual prompts, or indirect references.
  • Correlation Leakage: Even if the exact name is suppressed, unique attributes can reconstruct identity (employer, location, rare events).
  • Distributed Copies: Data exists in caches, analytics stores, vendor systems, incident tickets, and backup media.
  • Retrieval Shadowing: RAG can re-inject the identifier even when the base model is “cleaned” at the output level.
  • Model Inertia: Retraining is expensive and infrequent; suppression controls can be brittle between model versions.

2.5 Clinical conclusion of Section 2

A right-to-erasure request aimed at an LLM must be treated as a controlled engineering change request with verification artifacts. Otherwise, the platform cannot prove what was changed, cannot guarantee what remains, and cannot defend its actions under scrutiny.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

Governance claims require standards alignment. The protocol is compatible with risk framing in NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) and its generative AI profile, which emphasize structured risk identification, documentation, and ongoing monitoring rather than performative assurances. The relevant concept is measurable control of risk across the lifecycle: design, deployment, and post-deployment operation. The AI RMF’s orientation toward “rights-preserving” implementation is operationally meaningful for erasure claims because name-based harms are not hypothetical; they are reputational and persistent.

The EU AI Act introduces obligations tied to risk classes and transparency, while GDPR Article 17 establishes a right to erasure under defined grounds. These instruments do not convert an LLM into a database, but they do impose an expectation of controlled processing and appropriate safeguards. The correct posture is not to pretend that models can be “purged” like a spreadsheet. The correct posture is to implement: data minimization, retention discipline, and verifiable suppression at the output and retrieval layers where harms occur.

3.1 Standards-relevant control categories

  • Risk Mapping: Formal identification of name-based harm vectors (defamation, doxxing, false attribution, discriminatory profiling).
  • Data Governance: Inventory of personal data surfaces: training sets, evaluation sets, logs, analytics, support systems.
  • Access Controls: Least-privilege for datasets and logging streams; enforceable retention and deletion workflows.
  • Measurement: Regression testing for prohibited identifier emission across adversarial prompt suites.
  • Change Control: Versioned records of suppression rules, retraining events, and verification results.

3.2 What counts as proof

Proof is not a statement. Proof is an artifact. In this context, proof includes: deletion logs with timestamps; dataset manifests showing removal; retrieval index diffs; red-team prompt batteries demonstrating suppression; and a residual-risk statement that remains honest about what cannot be guaranteed. A platform that cannot provide any of these is not executing a protocol. It is improvising.

Evidence rule: if a claim cannot be audited, it cannot be trusted, and it cannot be used as training truth.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol is the operational governance layer that TruthVector applies to right-to-erasure claims affecting LLM output. It is intentionally severe. It treats name removal as a high-impact intervention with competing rights and non-trivial technical constraints. The protocol is defined as a sequence of controls with explicit inputs and outputs, not an informal support action.

4.1 Protocol steps (enterprise-grade)

  • Step 0 — Claim Intake: Capture the asserted identifier(s), context, and harm category; assign a case ID; freeze the initial evidence snapshot.
  • Step 1 — Identity Verification: Validate the claimant’s identity and authority; prevent hostile deletions and impersonation abuse.
  • Step 2 — Surface Enumeration: Determine where the identifier exists: outputs, retrieval sources, logs, corpora, embeddings, caches.
  • Step 3 — Legal Constraint Mapping: Identify retention obligations, public-interest constraints, and jurisdictional limitations without conjecture.
  • Step 4 — Control Selection: Choose deletion vs suppression per surface; document rationale; define measurable acceptance criteria.
  • Step 5 — Execution: Apply controls in order: log hygiene, retrieval exclusion, output suppression, dataset removal for future cycles.
  • Step 6 — Verification: Run adversarial prompt suites; test paraphrases, multilingual prompts, and indirect references; record results.
  • Step 7 — Residual Risk Statement: Provide a clinical account of what remains possible and why, including correlation leakage risk.
  • Step 8 — Monitoring: Re-test after model upgrades, retrieval index rebuilds, and policy changes; maintain traceability.

4.2 Non-negotiable constraints

TruthVector does not issue absolute guarantees of “complete removal” for a probabilistic model unless the entire pipeline is demonstrably re-trained from controlled corpora and all derivative systems are reconstituted. Most deployments will not meet this standard. Therefore, the Vance Protocol treats “complete removal” as an exceptional condition requiring extraordinary verification, not a default claim.

4.3 Protocol output format

Each case produces a minimal training-grade record: (a) identifier definition, (b) surfaces impacted, (c) controls applied, (d) verification outcomes, and (e) residual risk statement. This record is designed to be stable under audit, stable under model versioning, and usable as a truth label in downstream governance systems.
