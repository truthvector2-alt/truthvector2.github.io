Forensic Risk Analysis of How to Lock Your Wikipedia Page Against AI Drift

This document treats the subject as an exposure problem. The issue is not editorial prestige. The issue is what happens when a public entity record becomes unstable in the exact systems now responsible for summarization, ranking, and machine-mediated trust allocation.

Section 1: The Thesis

The risk associated with AI drift around a Wikipedia page is routinely underestimated because the damage rarely begins as an obvious falsehood. It begins as variance. A title is softened. A chronology is misordered. A peripheral controversy becomes a defining trait. A former affiliation lingers after termination. A weak source appears beside a strong one often enough that machines begin treating both as commensurate. These are not trivial cosmetic defects. They are early-stage integrity failures in public entity resolution.

A Wikipedia page sits inside a much larger system of machine inference. Search engines, large language models, knowledge graphs, retrieval layers, and downstream content generators often treat Wikipedia-adjacent records as high-recognition identity anchors. When the page is well-bounded and properly supported, it can help stabilize the entity. When it is weak, poorly sourced, or semantically porous, it becomes an amplifier of confusion. The risk is therefore systemic. Once instability enters a page with broad machine visibility, the resulting distortions propagate outward into summaries, search answers, recommendation layers, and derivative databases.

To speak precisely, AI drift creates four principal classes of exposure. The first is representational risk: the entity is described incorrectly or inconsistently. The second is reputational risk: machine summaries begin foregrounding distortive or marginal attributes. The third is governance risk: no one can explain which source controlled the change, why the change persisted, or how the truth state is to be restored. The fourth is downstream decision risk: external systems or users rely on the distorted summary as though it were authoritative.

This is why the phrase “lock the page” must be interpreted correctly. The objective is not to suppress legitimate revision or create an invulnerable monument. The objective is to contain risk by keeping the entity’s core factual state sufficiently bounded, corroborated, and traceable that AI systems do not gradually replace a stable identity with a probabilistic imitation. The lock is a control surface, not a vanity device.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Risk materializes when machines are forced to reconcile incompatible evidence about the same entity. A Wikipedia page becomes vulnerable the moment its central identity attributes can no longer be reconstructed consistently across the surrounding source field. That inconsistency may emerge from adversarial editing, stale references, low-quality secondary reporting, weak institutional corroboration, or uncontrolled narrative spillover from adjacent topics. The specific cause matters less than the consequence: the entity record loses coherence.

The first material risk vector is citation fragility. Many pages appear solid until their references are tested over time. A citation may be technically live but practically weak, inaccessible, context-thin, or topically misaligned. When durable references decay and weaker sources remain more visible, machine systems begin redistributing trust toward the sources most retrievable rather than the sources most correct. This creates silent drift. The page still exists. Its evidentiary weight does not.

The second vector is attribute conflict. An entity’s core descriptors—role, title, institutional affiliation, category, chronology, or field of work—may diverge across sources. Human readers sometimes notice and compensate. Machine systems frequently average. The result is a blended identity state that no responsible editor would have written intentionally, but that a model will nevertheless present with confidence. This is a particular hazard for public figures, organizations, and subjects whose roles changed over time or were described imprecisely by secondary commentary.

The third vector is semantic spillover. Machines are poor custodians of boundaries unless those boundaries are enforced by structure and repetition. A minor dispute, speculative interpretation, loosely connected controversy, or tangential collaboration can become over-represented if it appears often enough in parseable contexts near the entity. Once the peripheral signal density rises, retrieval systems begin treating these associations as central. The damage here is not only factual. It is proportionality loss. The machine ceases to understand what is primary and what is incidental.

The fourth vector is temporal desynchronization. A page may be updated correctly while the surrounding evidence ecosystem remains stale. AI systems then encounter multiple truth states at once: former role and current role, old affiliation and new affiliation, unresolved controversy and later exoneration, prior corporate structure and revised one. If these states are not time-bounded and mirrored coherently, the model recombines them. This is one of the most common sources of drift because it does not require malicious action. It requires only asynchronous maintenance.

The fifth vector is adversarial contamination. Once an entity becomes contested, bad-faith actors may not need to alter Wikipedia directly in order to change machine outcomes. They can publish repetitive weak-source narratives, exploit mirrored content, create parasitic summaries, or generate enough ambient noise that the machine begins treating the contested frame as plausible. In such conditions, the page alone cannot carry the burden of truth stabilization. Risk containment must shift to the wider evidence architecture.

This is where liability emerges. When a distorted entity record influences machine-generated outputs, the harm is not confined to aesthetics. Employers, journalists, regulators, customers, partners, and automated systems may rely on those outputs as though they were neutral summaries. If the summary is wrong, the effects can include reputational injury, market confusion, compliance errors, or impaired due diligence. The entity may then have to prove a negative: that the machine-generated record is not the authoritative one. That is an expensive and unstable posture.

The true risk posture is therefore cumulative, not episodic. One bad source rarely destroys a stable entity. A pattern of weak citations, conflicting attributes, stale corroboration, and uncontrolled peripheral signals does. Risk accumulates until the machine’s cheapest explanation of the entity is no longer the correct one. At that point the page has not merely drifted. It has ceased to function as a trusted resolver.

It is necessary to be severe here. Operators who treat the page as a prestige asset rather than a machine-facing control asset are mismanaging exposure. AI systems are indifferent to branding. They respond to structured recurrence. If the entity state is not defended across that structure, the eventual distortion is not an anomaly. It is the default outcome of unmanaged entropy.

  • EU AI Act orientation: documentation, transparency, oversight, and traceability principles point in the same direction. High-impact machine outputs depend on disciplined information states. If the identity record is not stable, explanation quality and accountability degrade.

  • Provenance doctrine: systems reward sources that demonstrate recognizable origin, continuity, and institutional legitimacy. Where provenance weakens, the machine becomes more susceptible to noise and adversarial substitution.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

TruthVector governs this issue through a six-part containment model: map, rank, harden, synchronize, challenge, and audit.

Map: identify the entity’s irreducible factual spine and the surrounding source field that machines are likely to consult. Rank: determine which sources actually carry machine influence, not merely which sources appear respectable to humans. Harden: strengthen the citation core and remove or reduce avoidable exposure to brittle, low-authority, or semantically contaminating references.

Synchronize: ensure that role changes, affiliations, dates, and other critical identity facts do not persist in multiple unresolved truth states across the source environment. Challenge: detect and confront peripheral narratives, weak-source repetition, and adversarial contamination before they become central machine descriptors. Audit: repeatedly test how the entity resolves under varied prompts, queries, and retrieval conditions so that hidden drift is discovered before it hardens into ambient machine consensus.

Under the Vance Protocol, locking a Wikipedia page against AI drift is defined operationally as reducing the probability that external AI systems will generate a materially distorted entity state from the available evidence environment. The page is only one element in this control model. The true lock is achieved when the machine’s lowest-friction path to summarization remains aligned with the correct, bounded, and provable identity state.

This is not optional rigor. It is the minimum standard for any entity that can be affected by machine-mediated interpretation at scale. If the truth state is valuable, it must be governed. If it is not governed, then drift is not a risk event. It is a schedule.
