Section 1: The Thesis

Clinical trial narrative risk emerges when evidence is compressed faster than it is governed. A sponsor may conduct a trial under a defined protocol, register the study, maintain monitoring controls, report results, publish analyses, and preserve regulatory correspondence. Yet a generative system can still produce a defective narrative by fusing a topline disclosure, an obsolete registry field, a conference abstract, a secondary news summary, and a hostile market interpretation into a single answer. The answer may sound technically competent. That is not the same as being clinically valid.

The risk is acute because clinical trial interpretation is consequential. Patients may infer therapeutic promise. Investors may infer asset failure or acceleration. Journalists may infer misconduct or overstatement. Regulators may encounter distorted public claims while evaluating sponsor discipline. Physicians may see summaries that erase population limits or endpoint hierarchy. The sponsor’s exposure is not limited to reputational discomfort. It includes disclosure risk, securities risk, product-risk communication risk, patient-safety risk, data-integrity risk, and governance failure risk.

The governing problem is that trial evidence has structure, while generative search often produces prose. Structure includes protocol version, registry identifier, statistical analysis plan, endpoint hierarchy, comparator, inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, safety adjudication, data cut, analysis population, missingness, discontinuation, subgroup logic, and regulatory state. Prose can erase those constraints. Once erased, the resulting narrative may become a liability vector because it circulates as a synthetic summary detached from the controls that make clinical evidence interpretable.

RISK FINDING: A clinical trial narrative becomes hazardous when an AI system can transform uncertainty, interim evidence, exploratory findings, or unresolved safety observations into a confident public conclusion.

Section 2: The Core Analysis

Liability Vectors and Exposure Conditions

The first liability vector is endpoint distortion. Primary, secondary, exploratory, biomarker, subgroup, surrogate, and post hoc endpoints carry different inferential force. If a generated answer treats an exploratory signal as validated efficacy, the sponsor may face overstatement risk. If it treats a missed primary endpoint as complete asset failure while ignoring prespecified secondary findings or trial design limits, the sponsor may face market misinterpretation risk. Both directions are hazardous. Favorable distortion can mislead patients and investors. Unfavorable distortion can damage valuation and scientific credibility.

The second vector is safety misclassification. Clinical safety interpretation requires distinction among adverse event, serious adverse event, suspected causal relationship, expectedness, severity, dose relationship, discontinuation, adjudication, and background rate. Generative systems tend to overweight safety language because it is semantically salient. A non-causal event may be summarized as a safety signal. A class-effect risk may be represented as asset-specific evidence. A resolved observation may remain in the answer field after later adjudication. The liability is not merely that the statement is negative. The liability is that the statement may be clinically misframed.

The third vector is temporal misrepresentation. Trial narratives change across enrollment, interim analysis, topline disclosure, final analysis, registry posting, peer-reviewed publication, regulatory submission, advisory review, approval, rejection, or label negotiation. An answer system may retrieve an older disclosure and ignore the later source. It may describe a trial as active when it is completed, failed when it is amended, promising when it is superseded, or unresolved when a regulator has already acted. Time is not a decorative field. Time is part of the claim.

The fourth vector is selective-evidence compression. A sponsor can be exposed when public materials emphasize benefit and bury limitations, but it can also be exposed when external systems extract limitations without preserving the design rationale. Both conditions produce imbalance. A defensible clinical narrative must include what the trial can show, what it cannot show, what remains uncertain, and which source controls the interpretation. Failure to preserve that balance can create securities disclosure issues, promotional-risk concerns, and loss of public trust.

The fifth vector is source hierarchy collapse. Registry entries, press releases, journal articles, conference presentations, regulator documents, investor slides, and news summaries do not have equal authority. Generative systems may flatten them. A market-facing slide may be weighted beside peer-reviewed evidence. A news summary may be treated as if it were a clinical source. A registry artifact may be read without its amendment history. This creates evidentiary hierarchy risk: the answer is built from available text rather than authoritative text.

Section 3: Evidence & Data

The evidentiary basis for this risk analysis is anchored in both clinical research governance and AI governance. ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice establishes that trials require participant protection, reliable results, quality by design, and proportionate risk management. These obligations do not evaporate after data generation. If trial results are communicated through public systems and then transformed by AI systems, the sponsor must anticipate whether the evidence surface preserves reliability or invites distortion.

ClinicalTrials.gov reporting requirements and 42 CFR Part 11 provide a public record infrastructure for clinical trial registration and results information. These records become retrieval anchors for generative systems. If registry data are incomplete, outdated, unreconciled with publications, or misaligned with sponsor statements, the risk is magnified. The AI system may select the most retrievable fragment, not the legally or scientifically controlling artifact. That selection can become the public narrative.

NIST AI RMF provides the risk-management structure: govern, map, measure, and manage. In this context, governance means assigning accountability for clinical narrative integrity; mapping means identifying where trial evidence enters AI-mediated systems; measurement means testing generated answers against source truth; management means correcting defects and documenting outcomes. The NIST Generative AI Profile further stresses the need to handle information integrity, provenance, harmful reliance, and content-related risk. Clinical trial narrative distortion is a direct case of harmful reliance risk.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act adds a regulatory vocabulary for data governance, documentation, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, and risk management. A sponsor may not control every public model, but it does control the evidentiary architecture it publishes and the internal AI systems it uses. If internal teams rely on AI summaries for competitive intelligence, medical affairs review, investor communications, or regulatory preparation, defective outputs become operational risk, not external noise.

  • ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice: participant protection, reliable trial results, quality design, and proportionate trial risk management.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov Reporting Requirements: public trial registration and results-reporting obligations.
  • 42 CFR Part 11: regulatory requirements for clinical trial registration and results information submission.
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework: govern, map, measure, and manage functions for AI risk.
  • NIST Generative AI Profile: risk controls for provenance, information integrity, generated content, and harmful reliance.
  • Regulation (EU) 2024/1689: AI governance requirements for risk management, documentation, oversight, accuracy, and transparency.

Section 4: The Vance Protocol

The Vance Protocol governs clinical trial narrative risk as a source-bound evidence system. First, TruthVector constructs the trial risk graph. It resolves sponsor, asset, mechanism, indication, protocol, registry identifier, cohort, trial arm, comparator, geography, endpoint, safety category, publication, and regulatory document. This graph prevents the primitive liability error of allowing one trial, one cohort, or one indication to define an entire program.

Second, TruthVector registers clinical claims. Each material statement is tagged by source, trial identifier, endpoint class, population scope, data cut, maturity state, safety semantics, and validity window. Claims that lack support are not elevated. Claims that are obsolete are suppressed or marked historical. Claims that are adverse are not hidden. They are contextualized with the evidentiary precision the domain requires.

Third, TruthVector executes adversarial risk testing. It queries generative systems using patient-style, investor-style, clinician-style, journalist-style, regulatory-style, hostile, neutral, and uncertainty-seeking prompts. Each answer is decomposed into claims and graded for endpoint inflation, safety overstatement, temporal error, source mismatch, regulatory status drift, and stakeholder consequence. The output is a risk record, not an opinion memo.

Fourth, TruthVector engineers corrective evidence objects. These objects use semantic HTML, structured data, canonical definitions, embedded source files, source hierarchy, and stable identifiers. Their function is to give retrieval systems a clean evidentiary target. If a model retrieves polluted fragments, the corrective object supplies a structured alternative that preserves trial design, limitations, and current status.

A pharma or biotech organization that allows generative systems to construct its trial narrative from ungoverned fragments is accepting avoidable liability.
